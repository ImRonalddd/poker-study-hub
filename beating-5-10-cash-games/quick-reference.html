Mid-Stakes Game Adjustments

Key Insight

At $5/$10 live, many players play too loose preflop and too tight postflop. They splash wide ranges in, then fold top pair when facing aggression. Exploit both tendencies: punish loose preflop callers with value bets, and bluff players who over-fold after the flop.

SituationStandard AdjustmentWhy
Opponents call big raises (10-12 BB) Raise big for value, don't bluff large Loose callers pay off; don't need fancy sizing
Playing 300-400 BB deep 4 BB open is fine; suits/connectors gain value Implied odds justify wider hands deep-stacked
Splashy player at the table Call wider in position; don't 3-bet them out You want their money in the pot, not out of it
Tight player at the table Attack their blind; bluff them more postflop Their range is narrow; they over-fold to pressure
Weak player leaves the table Take your break now Win rate drops sharply without the fish

Trap — Limping Garbage

Do not limp hands like 72o, 52o, K4o, Q6o from any position. When you flop the best hand with 52, opponents fold. When you don't, you bleed money. You make opponents play correctly vs. you. Stop limping junk entirely.

Key Insight — Suited Hands vs. Offsuit

Each suited hand has only 4 combos vs. 12 combos for offsuit. Adding suited speculative hands (64s, 75s) barely widens your range by combo count, but gives you much better playability. Prefer suited over offsuit for any marginal addition to your range.

Trap — Oversized 3-Bets

A 3-bet to $205 over a $40 open forces opponents to go all-in or fold. You lose value against hands like AJ, QQ that would have called a reasonable 3-bet. When you 3-bet big, you only get action from the hands that crush you.

Flop Bet Sizing Guide

Definition — Range Advantage

When you raised preflop and the flop is dry (big card, little card, little card), you have a range advantage: your opponent's range is weak because they called rather than 3-bet. This is when you bet small. When both players connect to the board (connected, middling cards), bet larger.

Board TextureBet Size (of pot)Example Boards
Dry — big + two small cards 25–33% K-5-2, A-8-3, Q-7-2
Paired board (low pair) 25–33% 9-2-2, K-6-6, 5-2-2
Coordinated / middling 50–66% J-10-8, 9-8-6, Q-J-T
Wet / flush draw present 50–75% K♥-8♥-3♦, A♠-J♠-5♣
Multi-way pot (3+ players) 33–50% or check Need stronger hand to bet; bet/fold available
Key Insight — Small Bets with Range Advantage

When your opponent is drawing very thin (or dead), bet small. A large bet induces them to play correctly — they fold. A small bet keeps them in with garbage. You want their money, not their fold. Bet ~$50 into a $135 pot on K-5-2 rather than $120.

Key Insight — Bet Small / Bet Small / Bet Big (Triple Barrel)

With strong hands and bluffs on dry boards: bet small on the flop, bet small on the turn, then bet large on the river. This builds a pot gradually so opponents stay in, then charges maximum on the final street. Play bluffs the same way your value hands play.

Over-Betting the Turn

Worked Example — Turn Overbet with Draw

Setup: CO raises, BTN calls. Pot ~$200 on the turn. BTN has a strong draw (flush draw + straight draw) on a wet board. Stack ~$2,000.

Analysis: When you have a draw in position and the pot is ~$430, bet $600+ instead of $300. A $300 bet will not fold anyone with top pair. A $600 overbet can fold aces and creates leverage. Elite players overbet the turn with draws that need fold equity. Use it when you have a weird semi-bluff and nothing else will work.

Value Betting Rules

RuleDetail
51% rule (cash games) Bet the river any time you will be called by worse hands more than 50% of the time. No need for 55-60% like tournaments.
Thin value threshold In cash games, always reload — there is no chip preservation. Go for thin value on turns and rivers. Not going for value is the #1 leak of cash game players.
Sizing for maximum calls Bet slightly less than your estimate of your opponent's calling threshold. If they call up to $600 but fold $750, bet $580 and get called every time, not $750 and get folded half the time.
Blocker bets (in position) With a marginal hand and a queen blocker (for example), bet small to charge draws and weaker pairs, rather than letting them bet large into you.
Trap — Checking Down Big Hands

Checking down pocket aces after the flop and showing at showdown is embarrassing. You had three streets to extract value from weaker pairs and draws. Against wide, splashy ranges, your opponent has garbage much more often than the nuts. Bet every street with your strong hands.

Opponent Type Exploits

The Splashy/Loose Player

  • Plays too wide preflop (72o, 84s, K4o)
  • Pays off big river bets with top pair
  • Bets too large without thinking why
  • Exploit: Call wider in position; let them bloat pots; 3-bet for thin value; avoid 3-betting them out of the pot
  • Sizing: Bet small for value (they call anything); bet large as bluff is pointless

The Tight/Passive Player

  • Plays very few hands; earns no action when strong
  • May fold top pair to aggression
  • Rarely bluffs; rarely 3-bets
  • Exploit: Raise their limps; c-bet into them relentlessly; attack their blinds
  • Sizing: A small c-bet (>60%) on dry boards forces folds from most of their range

The Loose-Preflop / Tight-Postflop Player

  • Calls wide preflop then folds top pair under pressure
  • Very common at $5/$10 live
  • Exploit: 3-barrel bluff on good run-outs; represent overpairs/sets; use polarized large bets
  • They built a wide range they can't defend

The "Almost All-In" Bet Pattern

  • Some players bet almost all-in (leaving $200 behind)
  • This almost always means the nuts or near-nuts
  • If you see a player do this twice, assign them nuts every time
  • Exploit: Fold strong hands that are behind; do not call off with one pair

Key Insight — Do Not Show Bluffs

When an opponent makes a correct fold, never show the bluff. You want them to think they played the hand wrong. If they know you had nothing, they will adjust and call you down next time. Silence after a fold is more profitable than ego.

Key Formulas

Pot Odds

\[\text{Pot Odds} = \frac{\text{Call Amount}}{\text{Total Pot After Call}}\]

Example: Facing $300 into a $600 pot. Total pot after call = $900. You need \(\frac{300}{900} = 33\%\) equity to break even.

Minimum Defense Frequency (MDF)

\[\text{MDF} = 1 - \frac{\text{Bet}}{\text{Bet} + \text{Pot}}\]

Example: Opponent bets $60 into a $140 pot. \(\text{MDF} = 1 - \frac{60}{200} = 70\%\). You must continue with 70% of your range to prevent them from profiting with any two cards.

Minimum Bet to Profit as a Bluff

\[\text{Fold\%\ Required} = \frac{\text{Bet}}{\text{Bet} + \text{Pot}}\]

Example: Bet $60 into $140 pot. Need opponent to fold \(\frac{60}{200} = 30\%\) of the time for an immediate profit. If they fold more than 30%, every bluff shows profit.

Breakeven on River Bluff-Catch

\[\text{Must win} = \frac{\text{Call}}{\text{Total Pot After Call}}\]

Example: Facing $900 into $1,330 pot. Need \(\frac{900}{2230} \approx 40\%\) equity (i.e., opponent must be bluffing 40%+ of the time) to call.

Key Insight — Cash vs. Tournament EV Thresholds

In cash games, bet the river for value whenever called by worse hands >50% of the time. In tournaments, raise this to 55-60%+ because going broke costs you future EV opportunities. At $5/$10 live with deep stacks, be more aggressive about thin value than you would be in a MTT.

Definition — SPR (Stack-to-Pot Ratio)

SPR = Effective Stack / Pot Size on the Flop. Low SPR (1-4): commit with top pair or better. Medium SPR (4-13): need two pair+ to feel comfortable stacking off. High SPR (14+): need sets or better for full commitment; big pair hands have showdown value only.

Raise / 3-Barrel Bluff Conditions

Key Insight — When to Bluff with the Bottom of Your Range

Three conditions align to make a bluff profitable: (1) You hold the stone-bottom of your range with no showdown value. (2) You block key parts of your opponent's continuing range (e.g., you hold a 7, blocking straights). (3) Your betting line is consistent with value hands (you checked a street they expect you to check with value).

ScenarioBluff?Reasoning
River: draw missed, opponent checked to you Yes — bet large Bottom of range; no showdown value; fold equity exists
Turn: you have backdoor equity + block straight cards Yes — bet large (overbet) Block reduces opponent's nut holdings; overbet adds fold equity
Multi-way pot, checked to you on turn/river Rarely; proceed carefully Multiple opponents means someone likely has something
Opponent's range is very polarized toward value No Tight players are honest; don't try to bluff the calling station either
Board significantly upgrades (flush/straight completes) and you hold none Yes, if opponent likely holds draws Scare card polarizes board; bet to rep the made draw

Checking with Strong Hands vs. Slowplay

Trap — Jamming Flop with Overpair After 3-Bet

You 3-bet with KK, get called. Flop is A-7-4. Opponent checks to you. Going all-in here is awful: every ace calls, every pocket queen and worse folds. You make your opponents play perfectly. Instead, check and let them bluff, then value bet the turn and river small. You win more against the hands that are drawing thin and save the all-in for when you are ahead.

HandAction After 3-Bet/4-BetWhy
KK on A-high board Check flop; bet small turn and river Ace folds everyone who can't beat you if you jam; checking invites bluffs
AA on connected board (JT8) Bet flop (bigger); continue betting turns Opponent has many draws; charge them
Set on dry board (222) Bet small all three streets Opponent can't fold; maximize value; don't scare them off
Flopped set, opponent shows strength Slow down if shove would fold everything except better Calling is better when raising only gets called by hands beating you

Table Image & Meta-Strategy

Key Insight — Building a Playable Table Image

If you play zero hands for 20+ hands, your opponents will fold to your first raise (they folded to Ace-Queen and it won the blinds). You must also raise with suited connectors and occasional speculative hands so that when you have Aces or Kings, your opponents will think you might be bluffing. Play snug, but stay active enough to earn action.
