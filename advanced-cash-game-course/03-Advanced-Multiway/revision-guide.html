1. Core Concepts

Diminished Advantages Multiway

In heads-up pots you often have 58%+ equity and a clear range/nut advantage. Multiway, these advantages shrink dramatically:

Players in PotYour "Good" EquityTypical Bet Frequency
2 (heads-up)~55-65%60-100%
3~37-42%30-60%
4+~25-35%Rarely
Key Rule
You are essentially never betting your entire range multiway. Even on boards that strongly favor you (like K-Q-5 for the cutoff), GTO bets only ~58% of the time.

Minimum Defense Frequency -- Shared

In heads-up, you defend ~70% vs a pot-sized bet. Multiway, this is divided among all players:

  • 2 players to act after bet: ~35% each
  • 7 players to act after bet: ~10% each (basically just the nuts)
Position Adjustment
The player closer to closing the action defends more (better equity realization). The first player to act after the bettor defends less.

Hand Strength Downgrade

HandHeads-Up3-Way5-Way
AA on 8-7-5Never folding to raiseFold to bet + raiseFold to any action
J-high flushExcellentMarginalBarely playable
Top pair good kickerStrong value betThin value / checkCheck-fold to aggression
Critical Insight
When you bet multiway and get called + raised, your hand is almost certainly beaten. Top pair on a coordinated board facing a raise should be folded.

Bet Sizing -- Default Small, Sometimes Big

Default
Default multiway bet size: ~30% pot. You are using small sizes because you rarely have a significant nut advantage.

When to use bigger sizes (50-70% pot):

  • You have a clear nut advantage (e.g., A-T-5 from the cutoff vs blinds)
  • Overpairs on uncoordinated low boards (8-3-2 from EP vs blinds)
  • Turn/river when your range improves (e.g., overcards that pair up)

Never overbet on the flop multiway. Even 70% pot is considered "big" in these spots.

Linear Betting Ranges

Multiway, your betting range shifts from polarized (nuts + air) to linear (hands with good equity):

  • Bet: Top pair, middle pair needing protection, strong draws, flush draws
  • Check: Underpairs, overcards without draws, total trash, AND some slow-played strong hands
  • Bluffs are equity-driven: Backdoor flush draws, gutshots to the nuts, overcards with draws. Almost never pure air.

Protecting Your Checking Range

  • Always check back some strong hands (overpairs, sets) to protect your checking range
  • If your checking range is too weak, competent opponents will run you over
  • Hands that are "almost always good but not amazing if tons of money goes in" are ideal check-backs
  • Example: QQ on 8-7-6 checks back to protect the range of 9s, 8s, under-pairs

2. C-Betting Strategy (as PFR)

When to Check Entire Range

Board type: Medium-connected boards where button/caller has similar nut/range holdings.

Example: T-7-5 Two-Tone (5-10 NL, Aria -- Cutoff vs BTN + BB)
Cutoff raises to 30, button calls, BB calls. Pot ~95. Flop: T-7-5 two spades.
Equity: cutoff 37%, button 39%, BB 24%. Button and cutoff have essentially the same nuts (sets of T, 7, 5; straight draws; flush draws). The cutoff is out of position with no nut or range advantage. GTO: check 100% of range. Even with a hand like K-J suited, check.
Why? Betting OOP without an advantage costs you money -- weaker hands get raised off their equity and stronger hands lose value by not being checked for range protection.

On the turn after checking flop: If an overcard comes (e.g., Queen on T-7-5), the cutoff regains nut and range advantage -- their range contains AK, KQ, KJ, QJ, AQ all of which improved. Now bet frequently: Queens, flush draws (front and backdoor), tens, strong draws. NOT pure trash -- always equity-driven. Hands like K-6 offsuit (no draw) still check. KJ suited with a club (backdoor flush draw) bets. River strategy: only bet with nut/king-high flushes (overbet 130%) or sets/overpairs (60% pot); KJ with no spade checks even facing a checked river.

When to Bet Frequently (~58%)

Board type: High-card boards where you have all premium hands and opponents 3-bet most of theirs.

Example: K-Q-5 (2-5 NL, Cutoff vs BTN + BB)
Cutoff raises to 15, button + BB call. Pot ~45. Flop K-Q-5.
Cutoff has: KK, QQ, AK, KQ, AQ, AJ (all the premium hands). Opponents 3-bet KK, QQ, AK preflop -- so they lack these. Clear nut advantage + range advantage for cutoff. Equity: CO 40%, BTN 35%, BB 25%.
GTO: bet ~58% of range, mostly 30% pot size. Bet: K-x hands, straight draws (AJ, AT, JT), small pairs for protection (66, 44 for equity denial), backdoor flush draws with overcards. Check: underpairs (JJ, TT), Q-x without draws, total trash, and slow-played strong hands (some KK/QQ) to protect checking range.

3. Playing In Position When Checked To

Two Scenarios Based on PFR's Strategy

PFR Should...Their Check Means...You Have...Bet Size
Check entire rangeThey're uncapped (have nuts)Range advantage onlySmall (30% pot)
Bet frequentlyWeak range checkedNut advantageBig (70% pot)

Example: 8-7-6 (Check, Check to Button)

Setup: LJ raises, you call on button, BB calls. Pot ~95. Flop 8-7-6. BB checks, LJ checks (LJ should check entire range -- they have no nut or range advantage on this connected board). Action to you.

  • PFR checks entire range → their range is uncapped (they have nuts in it: sets of 8, 7, 6; straight draws with 9x, 5x) → you don't have a nut advantage
  • Button bets 60% of the time at 30% pot (range advantage only)
  • Bet: 10x (overcards + straight draw), 9x (open-ender), 7x (pairs needing protection), overcards (KJ, AJ, QJ, KQ, AQ offsuit almost always -- they can make better hands fold some of the range)
  • Check back: QQ/JJ (almost always good, comfortable calling turn + river bets, but betting and getting raised is miserable), 76s (protect checking range with strong hands), sets of 7s/6s occasionally, ace-high/king-high/underpairs (need strong hands to protect these weak hands in your check range)
Why QQ Checks on 8-7-6
QQ on 8-7-6 is almost certainly the best hand. But if you bet and get check-raised, you're in a nightmare -- you can't comfortably call with multiple draws out there. Instead, check it back: your opponents will bet/bluff draws into you on the turn, and you can call them down profitably. This also means your checking range (which contains lots of ace-high and underpairs) is protected.

Example: A-T-5 (Check, Check to Button after LJ Would Normally Bet)

Setup: Lojack raises, you call on button with 6-5 suited, BB calls. Pot ~95. Flop A-T-5. BB checks, LJ checks. On this board LJ would normally bet their strong portion -- AK, AT, sets. So their check signals a weak, capped range.

  • Lojack checks = bottom of range (weak, capped) → you gain the nut advantage → bet bigger: 70% pot when you do bet
  • You only bet ~45% frequency (less than 8-7-6 scenario, because you have less range advantage but a nut advantage)
  • Bet big (70% pot): Bottom pair + backdoor draws (the 6-5 suited with pair of 5s and flush draw), JT/QT/T9 (middle pair with equity, need protection), pocket 4s (almost always good vs. LJ's weak range, getting equity denial vs. LJ's overcards like Q-J), nuts like 10s/5s when you have them
  • Check back: Ace-x without draws (A-Q, A-J, A-9 -- likely good, can call turn + river bets; also protects your check range so opponents don't blast you off your ace-highs), 9x, underpairs that can comfortably call two streets

Flush Turn Caution

Warning
When the flush completes on the turn: sets should check some of the time, top pair checks almost always, overpairs check almost always. Only bet with flushes and blocker bluffs (Ace/King of flush suit).

4. Spots to Use Big Bets Multiway

Driving Factor: Nut Advantage. "Big" multiway = 50-70% pot (not overbets).

Board 1: A-T-5 (Cutoff vs Small Blind + Big Blind)

Setup: 5-10 NL. Cutoff raises, SB and BB call. Pot ~95. Flop A-T-5 rainbow.

  • Cutoff has: AA, TT, 55, AT, A5s, AK, AQ, AJ -- opponents 3-bet most of these preflop, so they lack them
  • Equity: CO 39% vs SB 32% vs BB 29% -- gigantic nut advantage (red line far above opponents in top range region)
  • Big (70% pot): AK, AQ, AJ, AT (strong but vulnerable -- can be outdrawn by flush cards or running straights), sets of T and 5 (need protection), K5/65/54 (two-pair draws/protection), low pairs 4s/3s (value+protection vs overcards), KQ/KJ/QJ (blocker bluffs -- block opponent's Ax they'd fold)
  • Small (30% pot): Top set AA (almost never outdrawn, can bet small and still get value), A9/A8 (medium Ax, don't need protection as urgently), QT/JT (middle pair with reasonable equity)
  • Check: KK/QQ/JJ (good hands but won't improve; check to protect range), K-high without draws, A-x weak like A3 suited (checking protects range, can call turn bets)
  • Bet 60% of range overall
Why AA Bets Small on AT5
AA (top set) is virtually impossible to outrun in a typical multiway pot. It doesn't need protection. By betting small, it extracts value from Ax hands and builds the pot. AK, however, can lose to running flush cards or get counterfeited. AK bets big to charge opponents and deny equity.

Board 2: 8-3-2 (UTG+1 vs Small Blind + Big Blind)

Setup: EP raises, SB and BB call. Pot ~95. Flop 8-3-2 rainbow.

  • EP range has ALL overpairs (AA-88) plus A8s, K8s -- SB/BB don't have 8-high sets either (no one calls raises with 82s, 83s)
  • Effective nut advantage: opponent's best hands (like 8-7 suited) still lose to every overpair in EP's range
  • Bet 70% pot with entire betting range (60% of hands). This is the "put them in miserable spots" strategy
  • Bet big: All overpairs (AA-99), K8+, A3 (two pair), pocket 5s (protection), overcards with backdoor flush draws (AJ of clubs on a board with two clubs), gutshots (4-5 for wheel draw)
  • Check: A9/K9 without backdoor draws, pure overcards without any equity hook
The Opponent's Nightmare
Give opponent 8-7 suited (one of their best hands on 8-3-2). Your value range: AA, KK, QQ, JJ, TT, 99 all crush 8-7. Even A-3 has two pair. By betting 70% pot immediately, opponent faces a fold-or-stack decision with essentially their entire range in a losing position.

Turn Play After Big Flop Bet

When a queen, king, or ace hits the turn on 8-3-2: keep betting big. Your range now contains QQ/KK/AA that "improved" (not really, they were already ahead) plus a queen as effective nuts. Opponent's best hand (8-x) stagnated. Even queens (a near-air hand before) now become the "nuts" in this context -- you're happy loading more money in with them.

5. Facing Aggression Multiway

Fundamental Rule
Fold WAY more multiway than you do heads-up.
ScenarioFold Frequency
Heads-up vs bet~30%
3-way, bet to you, one behind~45%
3-way, bet + call to you~55-65%
Bet + raise before you80%+

Example: J-8-5 (Big Blind Facing Cutoff Bet)

Setup: 5-10 NL. Cutoff raises, SB calls, you defend BB with Q-8 of spades. Pot ~95. Flop J-8-5. Both check to cutoff who bets 15.

If small blind folds (now heads-up, closing action): Fold 32%, call 57%, raise 11%. You stick around with all jacks (top pair), good pairs + draws, backdoor flush/straight draws. You raise: sets (88, 55), two pair, strong top pairs with draws (QJ), gut shots (Q9, 107), open-enders (97, 76). You fold: Ax without backdoor draws, king-high no draw, total trash.

If small blind calls (now 3-way, NOT closing action): Fold 55% (up from 32%), call 32%, raise 13%. Dramatic tightening. Now folding: pocket 7s, pocket 5s (good hands heads-up!), Ax without flush/straight draw, marginal gut shots like 10-6. Raising becomes more value-heavy: Q-J always, J-9/J-8, sets. You can no longer loosely call with "ok" hands because the SB calling squeezes your equity realization badly.

The Key Shift
Going from "bet to you" to "bet + call to you" roughly doubles your fold frequency (32% to 55%). This is not exploitable tightening -- it's correct. The extra player compresses your equity realization and creates squeeze risk behind you.

Closing the Action Matters

  • When you close the action (last to act after bet): Defend wider than shared MDF suggests -- but still much tighter than heads-up
  • When you do NOT close the action: Overfold compared to shared MDF. Risk of getting raised behind you is real.

6. Key Hand Examples

1. Pocket Jacks -- T-6-5 Multiway (Bet Frequency Example)

Full Situation
Game: 5-10 NL cash game. You raise JJ from the cutoff to 30. Button calls, big blind calls. Pot: ~95. Stack depths: 200BB effective.
Flop: T-6-5 rainbow. Big blind checks, you check (GTO: check entire range on this board -- CO 37% equity, BTN 39%, BB 24%; no nut or range advantage vs BTN). Button checks behind. Pot still 95.
Turn: King. Big blind checks. Now you bet ~50% pot (~47).
Why? The king dramatically shifts the equity graph. Your preflop raising range includes AK, KQ, KJ, KT -- all checked the flop and improved. The button and big blind 3-bet KK preflop, so you now have a massive nut advantage on this turn card. JJ is still likely the best hand and needs protection vs two opponents who could have straight draws, overcards.
River: If you don't improve, check back. JJ is unlikely to extract value from worse and you don't want to bloat the pot unnecessarily.
EV of mistake: Betting the flop instead of checking costs ~0.3-0.5bb per occurrence -- you bet into opponents with equal or better range equity, letting them fold better than they should.

2. King-Queen -- K-7-6 Facing Bet + Raise

Fold Immediately -- Full Situation
Game: 5-10 NL. Cutoff raises, you call with KQ on the button, big blind calls. Pot: ~95.
Flop: K-7-6 with two diamonds. Big blind checks, cutoff bets small (15), big blind raises to 65, action on you with KQ (top pair, second kicker).
Why fold? The big blind is raising into a bettor and a caller on K-7-6. Their raising range here is nearly exclusively: sets of Kings, sets of 7s, sets of 6s, K7 suited (two pair), K6 suited (two pair), and strong draws like 8-9d (open-ender with flush draw). Your KQ is beaten by most of this range and nearly every turn card is bad -- any diamond completes a flush, any 5/8/9/T completes a straight, any 7 or 6 makes two pair worse for you.
EV of mistake (calling): You're roughly a 30-35% equity underdog with 130+ behind, facing two opponents. Expected loss vs. clean fold: approximately 40-60bb EV difference. Jonathan's words: "I don't even think this is a close spot."

3. Kings -- K-K-6 Top Set Multiway (Trap the Trap)

Full Situation
Game: 5-10 NL. You raise KK, button calls, big blind calls. Pot: ~95.
Flop: K-K-6. You have top set (trip kings with the board pairing). Bet small, ~30 into 95. Both opponents call. Pot: ~185.
Turn: 6 -- you now have kings full of sixes (KKK66). Check.
Why check the turn with the nuts? You block all Kx hands -- opponents cannot have kings in their range. The board paired again (6), so draws aren't betting. If you bet, the only hands that call are sets of 6s (now a worse full house) -- a tiny fraction of the range. By checking, opponents with draws think you're weak; a button with the 5d may bet as a bluff; someone with a six may try to value-bet thinking it's good.
Result from transcript: Button bets 50, big blind calls. You call. River: 5 diamonds. You check. Button bets 140, big blind jams. You call off -- they showed A7 diamonds (a flush) and a 5-high flush. You win a massive pot by inducing two bets you would never have gotten by blasting every street.
EV of mistake (betting turn): By betting the turn, you get only a fraction of value -- opponents fold draws and you lose 2 streets of action. Checking gains 1-2 extra big bets from multiple opponents.

4. Pocket Queens -- J-7-5 Check-Raise Gone Wrong

When to Abandon Ship -- Full Situation
Game: 5-10 NL. You raise QQ, get two callers. Pot: ~95.
Flop: J-7-5. Both check to you, opponent bets small, another calls, you check-raise to ~3x (this is correct: QQ is almost always good but vulnerable; check-raising for protection and value). Both opponents call the check-raise. Alarm bells.
Why alarm bells? When both call a check-raise on J-7-5, their ranges are: sets (JJ, 77, 55), two pair (J7, J5, 75), strong straight draws with equity (T8, 86), strong jacks (QJ, KJ). Almost nobody calls a check-raise multiway as a pure bluff.
Turn: A-spades (flush completes). Even with Q-spades giving you a flush draw: fold to any bet.
Why? Your Q-high flush draw loses to every flush already made. Opponents who called a check-raise on J-7-5 and then see a flush complete will often have the flush (they had suited connectors, suited aces). You're a significant underdog drawing to a queen-high flush that may itself be second-best. Pot odds of 165 to win 1,050 look tempting but you're drawing dead or nearly dead much of the time.
Transcript confirms: They showed king-high flush and 5-high flush. The player was drawing completely dead.

5. Ace-Four Suited -- River Blocker Decisions

Blocker Logic -- Full Situation
Game: Live cash. You raise A4s, cutoff and big blind call. Pot: ~95. You have the nut flush draw.
Flop: Check-raise with the nut flush draw (good: high equity, can barrel turns). Get called.
Turn: Brick. You barrel again with your strong draw. Get called.
River: Flush misses (9 clubs hits). Pot is large. Should you jam?
Answer: No. The A-spades in your hand blocks the hands that would auto-fold to your river bluff -- specifically, opponent's busted A-spade flush draws. Those are exactly the hands (AK of spades, AQ of spades, A9 of spades) that would fold to any river bet. By holding the ace of spades, you prevent them from having those folding hands, meaning your bluff has terrible fold equity.
When to bluff instead: Bluff with AK without the spade, or with hands like J-T with no spade blocker -- hands that don't block the opponent's busted draws they'd fold.
EV insight: A blocker bluff with Ax-spades on a spade-miss board can be 0.3-0.5bb worse per attempt than simply checking, because you're eliminating the most likely folding hands from your opponent's range.

7. Quick-Reference Flowcharts

Multiway Flop Decision (as PFR)

Do I have range + nut advantage? YES (e.g., KQ5, AT5) → Bet 30-70% pot, ~60% frequency SOME (nut but no range) → Bet 70% pot, ~40% frequency NO (e.g., T75) → Check entire range After checking flop, TURN overcard hits? Re-evaluate: likely gained advantage → bet

Am I closing the action? YES → Defend closer to shared MDF Good hand + draw → Call or raise Marginal hand → Call if equity is there Trash → Fold NO (players behind) Strong hand + draw → Call (sometimes raise) Marginal → Lean fold Trash → Always fold If facing BET + CALL: Fold 55%+, only continue with strong hands + draws If facing BET + RAISE: Fold almost everything (only continue with super nuts)

8. Common Mistakes

  1. C-betting your entire range multiway -- You should check 40-100% of hands depending on the board
  2. Using big bet sizes on the flop -- Default to ~30% pot; only go bigger with clear nut advantage
  3. Calling too wide facing bet + call -- You fold 55%+ of your range, not 30%
  4. Not protecting your checking range -- Always check back some strong hands (overpairs, sets)
  5. Bluffing with pure air -- Multiway bluffs must have equity (flush draws, gutshots, overcards)
  6. Treating top pair as premium multiway -- Top pair facing aggression from 2+ players is often a fold
  7. Ignoring blocker effects on the river -- Don't bluff when you block opponent's folding range
  8. Playing the same strategy heads-up and multiway -- These are fundamentally different games
  9. Not adapting to turn texture -- An overcard turn can give you the advantage you lacked on the flop
  10. Raising in position without nut advantage -- In position multiway, you mostly just call
