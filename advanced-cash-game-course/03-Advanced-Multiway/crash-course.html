1. Multiway Foundations

Core Principle
Multiway pots are fundamentally different from heads-up pots. Your range advantage, nut advantage, equity, and ability to bluff are all dramatically reduced when facing two or more opponents.

In a heads-up pot, you might have 58% equity and bet 80% of your range. In a 3-way pot, a heavily favored player might only have 40% equity. The result: you check far more often, bet smaller, and construct your ranges in a much more linear manner.

The Three General Adjustments

AdjustmentHeads-UpMultiway
Bet FrequencyOften 70-100%Rarely above 60%, often 0%
Bet Sizing33-150% pot depending on advantageDefault ~30% pot; big = 50-70% pot
Range ConstructionPolarized (nuts + air)Linear (hands with good equity)
Key Insight
In multiway pots, your "bluffs" are not really bluffs in the traditional sense. They are hands that have real equity: flush draws, straight draws, overcards with backdoor draws, or middle pair needing protection. You are almost never bluffing with pure nothing on the flop.

2. Range & Nut Advantage Multiway

Definitions
Range advantage: Your range has more overall equity than your opponents'. Drives bet frequency.
Nut advantage: You have significantly more super-strong hands (sets, top two, overpairs) than opponents. Drives bet sizing.

In heads-up pots, the preflop raiser frequently has both advantages. Multiway, these are diluted:

Heads-Up Equity

PFR often has 55-65% equity. Clear range advantage on most boards.

3-Way Equity

PFR typically has 37-42% equity. Range advantage is small or nonexistent.

Reading Equity Distribution Graphs

The equity distribution graph shows how each player's hands rank from strongest to weakest:

  • Top region (left side): Sets, two pair, overpairs — this is the nut advantage zone
  • Middle region: Top pairs, middle pairs, draws — this is the range advantage zone
  • Bottom region (right side): Trash, ace-high, underpairs — irrelevant for betting decisions
How to Read the Graph
If your line is clearly above opponents in the top region, you have the nut advantage → use bigger sizing.
If your line is above in the middle region, you have the range advantage → bet more frequently.
If lines overlap everywhere, you have neither → check your entire range.

Example: T-7-5 Two-Tone (Cutoff vs Button + BB)

  • Cutoff equity: 37% — Button: 39% — BB: 24%
  • Button and cutoff have essentially the same nuts (sets, straight draws)
  • No nut advantage, no range advantage → check 100% of range

Example: K-Q-5 (Cutoff vs Button + BB)

  • Cutoff equity: 40% — Button: 35% — BB: 25%
  • Cutoff has KK, QQ, AK, KQ (opponents three-bet these preflop)
  • Clear nut advantage + range advantage → bet ~58% of range

3. Minimum Defense Frequency

MDF Multiway
In heads-up, you defend ~70% vs a standard bet. Multiway, the MDF is shared among all remaining players.

The formula is straightforward. If someone bets and there are \( n \) players remaining:

$$\text{Each player's share} \approx \frac{\text{Heads-up MDF}}{n}$$
Players After BetShared MDF (Each)Practical Meaning
1 (heads-up)~70%Defend most hands
2~35%Defend selectively
3~23%Defend tight
7~10%Basically just the nuts

Position Adjustments

Not all players defend equally. The player closest to closing the action defends more, because they realize equity better:

  • First to act after bet: Defend ~30% (below shared MDF)
  • Last to act (closing action): Defend ~40% (above shared MDF)
Why Overfold When Not Closing Action
If you call and then someone behind you raises, you're in a terrible spot. Even if they just call, you now face two opponents on the turn from a bad position. The risk of being squeezed or outdrawn justifies folding more than the math alone suggests.

4. Hand Strength Downgrade

This is one of the most important multiway concepts. Hands that are strong heads-up become marginal or worse when facing multiple opponents.

Example: Pocket Aces on 8-7-5
Heads-up: If you bet and get check-raised, you are NOT folding.
4-way: If you bet and face a call + call + raise, you should fold immediately. One of the many opponents almost certainly has you beat with a set, two pair, or a straight.
Example: Jack-High Flush on 9-8-2-3-4♠
Heads-up: Excellent hand, bet for value confidently.
4-way: Marginal. If you bet and get called, the callers likely have higher flushes. Ace-high and king-high flush draws are the most common draws, and they all beat you.

Hand Strength Comparison Table

Overpair (non-top set)Strong — rarely foldingBet for protection, fold to raiseVery vulnerable
Middle pairMarginal — check-callBet for equity denialUsually check-fold

5. C-Betting Opportunities

Default Multiway C-Bet
When you decide to bet multiway, the default size is approximately 30% pot. You are never betting 100% of your range. Even on the best boards for your position, GTO bets only ~58-60% of the time.

When to Check Your Entire Range

Check everything when you have neither nut advantage nor range advantage. This happens on boards where all players connect roughly equally.

BOARD: T-7-5 two-tone POSITION: Cutoff vs Button + Big Blind Equity: CO 37% | BTN 39% | BB 24% Nut advantage: None (BTN has same nuts) Range advantage: None (BTN slightly higher) DECISION: Check 100% of range

When to Bet Frequently (~58%)

Bet when you have both range and nut advantage. Typically on boards with high cards that opponents three-bet preflop.

BOARD: K-Q-5 POSITION: Cutoff vs Button + Big Blind We have: KK, QQ, AK, KQ, AQ, AJ Opponents three-bet these preflop! Equity: CO 40% | BTN 35% | BB 25% DECISION: Bet ~58% of range, mostly 30% pot BET: Kx hands, straight draws, small pairs for protection CHECK: Underpairs, Qx without draws, total trash

Turn Play After Checking the Flop

After everyone checks the flop, re-evaluate on the turn. Overcard turns that favor your range (because you have overcards) let you start betting:

Example: T-7-5 Flop, Queen Turn
After check-check-check on T-7-5, a queen turn gives the cutoff a new advantage. Now betting frequently with: queens, flush draws, tens, straight draws. Still NOT betting pure trash — only equity-driven hands.

River Strategy in Former Multiway Pots

By the river, ranges should be extremely tight. After betting turn and getting called:

  • Value bet big (130% pot): Only nut flushes, king-high flushes
  • Value bet medium (60% pot): Sets, overpairs, weaker flushes
  • Bluff: Only with no blockers to opponent's folding range (no flush-suit blocker)
  • Check: Everything else — even decent hands like KJ with no spade

6. When to Use Big Bets

Driving Factor
Nut advantage drives big bet sizing multiway, just like heads-up. "Big" multiway means 50-70% pot — not overbets.

Scenario 1: High-Card Boards (A-T-5)

Cutoff raises, small blind + big blind call. Flop: A-T-5.

  • Cutoff has: AA, TT, 55, AT, A5s, AK, AQ, AJ (opponents three-bet most of these)
  • Gigantic nut advantage, moderate range advantage
  • Equity: CO 39% vs SB 32% vs BB 29%
CategorySizeHands
Big (70% pot)Value + protectionAK, AQ, AJ, sets, K5, 65, 54, low pairs, KQ/KJ/QJ blockers
Small (30% pot)Medium strengthTop set (AA), A9/A8, QT/JT (middle pair)
CheckGive up or protect rangeKK, QQ, JJ, king-high, under pairs, 97/87 without draws
Why AA Bets Small
Top set (AA) on A-T-5 is almost never getting outdrawn. It doesn't need protection. The big bet is for hands that are strong but vulnerable — like AK, which can lose to running cards or get counterfeited. AA can afford to bet small and still extract value.

Scenario 2: Uncoordinated Low Boards (8-3-2)

UTG+1 raises, small blind + big blind call. Flop: 8-3-2.

  • EP has ALL overpairs (AA through 88) — opponents don't have these
  • Opponents have more low sets, but the overpair advantage is massive
  • Bet 70% pot with entire betting range (60% of hands)
Putting Opponents in Miserable Spots
Give the opponent 8-7 suited (one of their best hands). Now look at your value range: every overpair from 99 to AA crushes it. By betting 70% pot, you force them into a terrible decision with basically their entire range. That's exactly what nut advantage does.

Turn Continuation After Big Flop Bet

When an overcard hits the turn (K, Q, A) that pairs your overcards:

  • Keep betting big — your range improves dramatically
  • Queens become effective nuts on 8-3-2-Q
  • Opponent's range stagnates (they still have the same middle pairs)

7. Playing IP When Checked To

Two Scenarios
Your strategy depends entirely on why the preflop raiser checked:
  1. They should check their entire range → they are uncapped (have nuts) → you have range advantage only → bet small
  2. They normally bet but chose to check → they are capped (weak range) → you have nut advantage → bet big

Scenario 1: PFR Checks Entire Range (8-7-6)

BOARD: 8-7-6 (Check, Check to Button) PFR STRATEGY: Check everything (opponents uncapped) YOUR ADVANTAGE: Range only (no nut advantage) BET SIZE: 30% pot, 60% frequency BET: 10x, 9x, 7x, overcards (KJ, AJ, QJ, KQ, AQ) Overcard bluffs: KJ/AJ/QJ offsuit almost always CHECK BACK: QQ, JJ (strong but hate raises) 7-6s (protect checking range) Sets of 7s/6s sometimes Ace-high, king-high, under pairs

Scenario 2: PFR Normally Bets, Now Checks (A-T-5)

BOARD: A-T-5 (Check, Check to Button) PFR STRATEGY: Would normally bet strong hands YOUR ADVANTAGE: Nut advantage (their check = weak) BET SIZE: 70% pot, ~45% frequency BET: Bottom pair + backdoor draws JT/QT/T9 (middle pair, equity-driven) Pocket 4s (protection) CHECK BACK: Ace-x (strong but can call turn/river) 9x, low pairs Hands that don't need protection

When the Flush Completes on the Turn

Common Mistake
Betting top pair or sets on a flush turn. After the PFR checks everything on the flop, their calling range is loaded with flush draws. When the flush hits:
  • Sets check some of the time
  • Top pair checks always
  • Overpairs check always
  • Only bet with: made flushes and blocker bluffs (A♠ or K♠)

Exploitative River Sizing

When you reach the river with a strong range after a multiway pot:

Your HandSizeWhy
Nut flush / strong flushOverbet (130%+ pot)Get max value from sets/TPTK that can't fold
Weak flushMedium (60-70% pot)Get called by Jx, sets; fold to raise
Sets / top pairMedium (60-70% pot)Get called by Jx, Tx type hands
Blocker bluffsOverbet (130%+ pot)Force out those same Jx hands
Air without blockersCheckNo fold equity against strong ranges

8. Facing Aggression Multiway

Fundamental Rule
Fold far more than heads-up. The more players involved in the action, the tighter you must be. Facing a bet + call means you should fold 55%+ of your range.

Fold Frequency by Scenario

ScenarioFold %Continue With
Heads-up vs bet~30%All pairs, draws, overcards
Multiway, bet to you, closing action~44%Pairs + draws, some overcards
Multiway, bet + call to you~55-63%Strong pairs + strong draws only
Multiway, bet + raise before you~80%+Essentially the nuts

Case Study: J-8-5 (Big Blind Facing Cutoff Bet)

If Small Blind Folds

Fold 32%. Continue with all top pairs, good draws, some backdoor equity hands. Raise 11% with sets, strong top pair + draws.

If Small Blind Calls

Fold 55%. Drop all marginal hands (pocket 7s, 5s, Ax no draw). Raise 13% but more value-heavy (QJ always, sets, strong equity).

Raising Changes When Multiway
In heads-up pots, you raise with some pure bluffs. Multiway facing bet + call, your raising range becomes almost entirely value + high-equity semi-bluffs. Hands like A8/K8/T8 suited (middle pair + backdoor draws) start raising because they need protection and have real equity.

Big Blind OOP: The Extreme Fold

On K-Q-5 facing a Lojack bet + Button call:

  • Big blind folds 82% of their range
  • When continuing: mostly raising (not calling) because equity realization OOP is terrible
  • Raise with: KQ (nuts), sets, strong draws (JT, AJ)
  • Call with: almost nothing — either raise or fold
Common Mistake: Calling Too Light OOP
Many players call with Qx or gutshots from the big blind in multiway pots. This is a significant leak. If you're out of position facing a bet with players behind, you need a very strong hand or a strong draw. Flat calling with Queen-Ten on K-Q-5 from the big blind against two players is burning money.

9. Protecting Your Checking Range

Why It Matters
Deep-stacked multiway, if your checking range is too weak, competent opponents will run you over with bets on every street. You must check back some strong hands to keep your checking range defensible.

Ideal Check-Back Candidates

  • Overpairs (QQ on 8-7-6) — almost always good, hate getting raised
  • Strong middle pairs (JJ on T-6-5) — can comfortably call turn + river bets
  • Bottom two pair (7-6s on 8-7-6) — strong but don't want to bloat the pot
  • Some sets (7s, 6s on 8-7-6) — can withstand aggression on any runout

What Makes a Good Check-Back

PropertyGood Check-BackBad Check-Back
StrengthAlmost always winning vs 1-2 betsNeeds protection urgently
VulnerabilityNot very vulnerable to overcardsGets outdrawn by many turn cards

10. Key Hand Examples

Pocket Jacks on T-6-5 (Check Flop, Bet Turn)

The Setup
JJ raises, button + BB call. Flop: T-6-5. We check (we should check everything on this board multiway). Check through. Turn: King.

Why bet the turn? The king gives us a significant range and nut advantage. Our range includes AK, KQ, KJ, KT — all stayed because we checked the flop. We can bet medium for value (JJ with a king out is still likely best) and for protection (preventing overcards from seeing a free river).

River: If we don't improve, check it back. We're either good or not — no need to value bet further.

King-Queen Facing Bet + Raise on K-7-6

The Fold
KQ calls on button, K-7-6 flop. Cutoff bets small, big blind raises to 65, action to us. Fold immediately.

Top pair feels strong, but consider: the big blind raises into a bettor and a caller. They have sets, two pair, or strong draws. Even if you're ahead right now, almost every turn card is bad: any diamond, any straight card, any 7 or 6, any ace. You need to run out something like 2-3 to feel safe. This is "not even close" to a call.

Kings (Top Set) on K-K-6 Multiway

Trapping with the Nuts
KK raises, button + BB call. K-K-6. Bet small on flop (dynamic board warrants protection). Both call. Turn: 6 (full house). Check.

With the super nuts blocking all Kx, nobody can pay you off. Check to let opponents bluff or improve. Button bets, BB calls — just call. On the river, if a draw completes and they bet, you still just call or raise small. The key lesson: don't blast every street with the absolute nuts when you block the calling range.

Pocket Queens: Check-Raise Gone Wrong on J-7-5

Reading the Situation
QQ raises, two callers. J-7-5. Check, face bet + call, check-raise with QQ (almost always good, vulnerable). Both call the check-raise. Alarm bells.

When both opponents call a check-raise on J-7-5, they have sets, strong draws, or excellent jacks. Turn: A♠ (flush completes). Even with Q♠ for a flush draw — fold. You're drawing dead against made flushes, and the opponents are highly likely to have them. The pot odds look tempting (165 to win 1,050) but you're crushed.

Ace-Four Suited: River Blocker Logic

When NOT to Bluff
A4s check-raises with the nut flush draw, bets the turn with equity. River misses. Do not bluff.

Holding the A♠ blocks the opponent's busted A♠x flush draws — the exact hands that would fold to your river bet. If you block their folds, bluffing is suicidal. Save your river bluffs for hands that don't block the opponent's folding range (e.g., AK without the flush suit).

11. Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: C-Betting Your Entire Range
In heads-up pots, betting 80-100% of your range is often correct. Multiway, even the best boards for your position warrant checking 40%+ of the time. On boards like T-7-5, you check everything.
Mistake 2: Using Big Bets Without Nut Advantage
The default multiway bet is ~30% pot. Only use 50-70% pot when you have a clear nut advantage (A-T-5, 8-3-2 from EP). Never overbet on the flop multiway.
Mistake 3: Calling Too Wide Facing Bet + Call
Going from "bet to me" to "bet + call to me" roughly doubles your fold frequency. On J-8-5, fold rate jumps from 32% to 55%. Drop all marginal hands immediately.
Mistake 4: Not Protecting Your Checking Range
If you always bet your good hands and only check trash, opponents will exploit you mercilessly. Check back overpairs, strong pairs, and even some sets to keep your checking range defensible.
Mistake 5: Bluffing with Pure Air
Multiway bluffs need equity: flush draws, gutshots, overcards with backdoor draws. Almost never bluff with pure nothing on the flop. On later streets, respect blocker effects.
Mistake 6: Treating Top Pair as Untouchable
KQ on K-7-6 facing a bet and a raise = fold. Top pair facing multiway aggression is often dominated. Hand strengths are downgraded significantly.
Mistake 7: Never Folding Good Hands on Scary Turns
QQ with a flush draw on a flush turn where both opponents called your check-raise? Fold. Ace-x without extra equity on a bad turn card? Fold. Adaptability is critical.
