Advanced Bet Sizing in Cash Games

Advanced Cash Game Course -- Chapter 2 Crash Course

1. The Four Factors Driving Bet Size

Definition
Every bet sizing decision on every street is determined by four factors: Nut Advantage (who has more premium combos), Range Connectivity (how well the board hits opponent's continuing range), Stack Depth (the stack-off threshold), and Board Dynamics (how likely current strong hands get downgraded).

Nut Advantage

The player with more combinations of premium hands has the nut advantage. When you have it, you bet larger. When you don't, you bet smaller.

Example
LJ raises, BB calls. Flop: 8-5-2 vs 10-9-7. On 8-5-2, LJ has overpairs (no straights/flushes available), giving a big nut advantage -- bet 2/3 pot. On 10-9-7, BB has all the straights/sets, LJ lacks nut advantage -- bet only 25% pot.

Range Connectivity

When opponent's range connects well with the board (lots of pairs and draws), bet larger because they'll call with decent hands your strong hands crush. When their range is disconnected (queen-high, king-high), bet smaller.

Example
BTN vs BB. Flop: A-9-3 vs K-Q-10. On A-9-3, BB's range is mostly trash (Q-high can't do much) -- bet small. On K-Q-10, any A-J or 9 is a draw, any K/Q/10 is a pair -- bet bigger despite having less equity because opponent has more hands that will call.

Stack Depth

As stacks get deeper, you need closer to the actual nuts to have a nut advantage. Pocket jacks on 10-8-7 is happy to get all-in at 40bb but not at 400bb.

  • 100bb deep: Overpairs count as effective nuts; bet wider, check less (23% checks on 10-8-6)
  • 200bb deep: Must have closer to true nuts; bet less often but BIGGER when you do (44% checks on 10-8-6)

Board Dynamics

Definition
Board dynamics measures how likely the current strong hands are to get downgraded on later streets. When a straight or flush is already available, bet smaller. When there's a straight or flush draw but no made hand, bet larger.
Example
BTN vs BB. Flop: A-K-5 vs A-K-J. On A-K-5 (no straight, but draws like 5-4, Q-J, Q-10), overbet to 120% pot with a polarized range. On A-K-J (straight already available via Q-10), bet only 60% pot despite having a big nut advantage on both boards.

2. When to Bet Very Small on the Flop (~20% Pot)

The Cash Game Masterclass used 33% as the small size. There are boards where you should go even smaller, to around 20% pot, and bet with almost your entire range.

When to Use 20% Pot Bets

  • Monotone boards (three of same suit): Both players have nut flushes, so no nut advantage; you just have a range advantage
  • Paired boards where you lack trips (e.g., 8-6-6 as LJ raiser): Opponent has the trips, you have overpairs
  • Uncoordinated boards (K-6-2): Huge range advantage, minimal nut advantage
  • Boards with a straight already there (7-5-4): Opponent has the straights and two-pair
Insight
As you bet smaller, you get to bet more often. On K-8-3 monotone, using 33% pot lets you bet 40% of the time. Using 20% pot lets you bet every single time -- and makes more EV. Opponents make errors whenever you bet, so maximizing betting frequency is very valuable.
Trap
Don't use big bets on monotone or paired boards just because the board looks "scary." On K-8-3 all hearts (SB 3-bet, BTN calls), if you pot it, you barely get to bet at all. Tiny bets are far more profitable because you deny equity from everything.

The Equity Diagram Signal

When you see both players have roughly equal nuts at the top of the range, but you're favored across the middle and bottom, that's the signal for frequent tiny bets. You're exploiting your range advantage, not your nut advantage.

3. When to Bet Very Large on the Flop (66%+ Pot)

Large bets should be used when you have a large nut advantage, high range connectivity for your opponent, and when the board is dynamic with draws but no made straights or flushes.

Key Conditions for Overbetting the Flop

  1. Overpairs are the effective nuts: Uncoordinated low boards (8-3-2, 7-4-2) with no straight available
  2. Your range has many draw-to-the-nuts combos: A-Q-3 (you have 5-4s, K-J, K-10, J-10 for draws)
  3. Board heavily favors your range: A-K-4 from LJ (big cards + draws = overbet territory)
Example
BTN vs BB, 8-3-2: Overpairs are essentially the nuts. No straights, no flushes. Overbet (100%+ pot). Bluffs are overcards like K-4, Q-9, J-9 that benefit from making K-J, Q-J fold. On 8-5-4 (straight already available), check 44% of the time and never overbet.
Insight
The hands bluffing with big sizes are often surprising: naked overcards like K-4 or Q-9 on 7-4-2. They benefit from forcing K-J, Q-J, and some A-highs to fold. The way to get these hands to fold is a big bet size.

4. The Straight/Flush Shortcut

This is the single most practical heuristic from the entire chapter:

5. Exploitative Flop Sizing Adjustments

Three Reasons to Exploitatively Bet Large

Definition
An inelastic opponent always calls with pairs/draws and always folds air, regardless of your bet size. Against these players, maximize value by betting large.
  1. Opponent is inelastic to bet sizes: They call with pairs/draws no matter what -- load the pot with value hands
  2. Opponent doesn't check-raise enough: Bet bigger with value since you won't get punished. Example: AK on K-10-4, pot it instead of 2/3 pot
  3. Opponent defends BB too wide: More trash in their range = bigger nut advantage = overbet. On 9-5-2 vs wide BB, most 2/3 pot bets become 110% pot overbets

Three Reasons to Exploitatively Bet Very Small

  1. Wide BB defender: Their range contains too much junk. Bet tiny (20% pot) and often (every hand). EV jumps massively (71bb/100 vs 45bb/100 in one example)
  2. Size-inelastic opponent: They fold trash to any bet. Bluff for 20% pot instead of 66% pot -- breakeven at only \(\frac{15}{80} \approx 19\%\) instead of 40%
  3. Opponent won't check-raise: Bet wider for thin value/protection. Gain 26bb/100 extra by betting tiny every time vs 33% pot 63% of the time
Trap
Against tight-passive players, do NOT use big GTO-style bets with your bluffs. On Q-10-7 (draw-heavy, should be big bet in GTO), bet only 15 into 65 (about 23% pot) with 6-5s against a tight-passive player. If they have nothing, they fold to ANY size. Save chips when they do have something.

6. From Rigid to Dynamic Bet Sizes

Insight
Using only 1/3 and 2/3 pot on the flop (rigid strategy) costs real money compared to dynamic sizing. On A-K-5, adding an overbet gains 8bb/100 hands. On 8-4-4, adding a tiny bet gains 5bb/100. A good online win rate is ~5bb/100, so these are massive gains.

The goal is not to memorize specific boards but to understand the concepts:

  • Low paired boards with a straight draw -> tiny bet
  • High boards with draws but no made hand -> overbet
  • Dynamic bet sizes take opponents to unfamiliar areas of the game
Trap
Adding complexity without proper study leads to more mistakes. If you're not comfortable with a spot, use the rigid 1/3 and 2/3 strategy. A simple strategy implemented correctly beats a complex strategy implemented poorly.

7. Check-Raise Sizing

Three Rules

  1. Smaller bet faced -> Larger raise: If they bet 1bb, raise to 10-15bb. If they bet 33% pot, raise to a big size relative to pot
  2. Bigger bet faced -> Smaller raise: If they pot it, raise to about 2.4x their bet (a small raise over the top)
  3. More dynamic board -> Larger raise: Current nuts likely to change = bigger sizing
Example
200bb deep, LJ vs BB. Flop: 10-9-7 vs 10-7-7. On 10-9-7 (dynamic -- straights will change), check-raise BIG. On 10-7-7 (static -- trips stay nuts), check-raise SMALL to keep opponent in with hands drawing thin.

Exploitative Check-Raising

Against auto-c-bettors (players who bet 100% on boards they should check sometimes):

  • On connected boards (10-9-7): They have too many marginal made hands -> check-raise LARGE to pot
  • On uncoordinated boards (A-7-2): They have too much trash -> check-raise SMALL (30% pot). Just fold out king-high, queen-high

8. Turn Continuation Betting

Definition
After you bet the flop and get called, opponent's range has lost all junk (they folded it) and many strong hands (they raised them). You typically have the nut advantage but not the range advantage. This drives polarized betting.

Turn Card Effects on Bet Size

Turn CardSizeReason
Flush completes (heart on K-8-3 two hearts)60% potBoth players have flushes -- no nut advantage
Overcard (K on 10-9-7)150% pot overbetYour bluffs improved; huge nut advantage
Blank (2 on K-8-3)150% pot overbetCurrent nuts preserved; press the advantage
Connects with opponent (8 on 10-9-7)25% potOpponent has tons of straights; small thin-value bets
Insight
Even though your equity may be below 50% after a flop c-bet and call (e.g., 46% on K-8-3 two hearts), your EV is above 50% because you have position plus the nut advantage. Position + nut advantage = over-realization.

The EV Conversion

To convert solver chip EV to a percentage of the pot:

\[\text{EV\%} = \frac{\text{Chip EV}}{\text{Pot Size}} \times 100\]

Example: 5.8 chip EV / 10.7 pot = 54% EV (despite only 46% equity).

9. Turn Probes & Delayed C-Bets

Key Distinction: Position Matters Enormously

Definition
A turn probe is an OOP bet after the preflop aggressor checked behind the flop. A delayed c-bet is when the preflop raiser bets the turn after checking the flop.

OOP Delayed C-Bets (Preflop Raiser Checks Flop)

  • You checked the flop from OOP very often (as you should), so your range still contains strong hands
  • When the nuts don't change, you use 150% pot overbets (e.g., 9-7-3 flop, 2 turn: overbet with overpairs + gutshot bluffs)
  • When an overcard comes (K on 9-7-3), you have range advantage -> bet frequently and small

IP Delayed C-Bets (After Check-Check)

  • When nuts don't change, IP delayed c-bets are rarely huge -- you would have bet your strong hands on the flop
  • Exception: when opponent checks flop AND turn, they've announced weakness twice. You now have both range and nut advantage -> frequent medium bets
Example
LJ raises, BTN calls. 9-7-3 flop, check-check. Turn: 2 of diamonds. OOP LJ overbets 150% pot with overpairs (AA, KK, QQ) and gutshot bluffs (A-5, A-4). On turn K of spades, LJ bets frequently and small (range advantage, opponent also has kings).
Example
BTN raises, BB calls. K-7-4 two spades flop, check-check. BB probes the turn. On 6 of hearts (range improves with straights/two-pair), bet more frequently with some big and some small bets. On J of diamonds (connects with BTN), bet very polarized and infrequent -- just nuts + high-equity draws.

IP vs OOP: Critical Difference

The maximum percentage of bluffs in your river betting range, based on bet size:

Trap
In practice, bluff slightly less than these theoretical maximums. Your value bets don't always win (opponent has some traps in checking range), and tricky opponents will check-raise you. Against super trappy opponents, bluff even less.

11. When to Bet Small on the River (OOP)

Small river bets from OOP are ideal when you have a massive range advantage but not necessarily the nut advantage. This commonly occurs after check-check on the turn.

Example
BB vs BTN. 9-7-3 flop, BTN bets 2/3, BB calls. Turn 8d, check-check. River 6h. BB has tons of straights, two-pairs, and top pairs. Bet 33% pot with the wide thin-value range (A-9, K-9, two-pairs). Include some nut hands (straights) that can bet-then-reraise. Include some low-equity bluffs (queen-high).

Structuring Your Small Betting Range

  1. Thin value: Top pair, two-pair, medium-strength hands
  2. Traps: Some nut hands (sets, straights) that bet small and re-raise if opponent raises
  3. Bluffs: Lowest equity hands (queen-high, no pair). NOT king-high or ace-high (those have showdown value to check)
Insight
In deep-stacked GTO, the river rarely prefers going smaller than 33% pot from OOP. But exploitatively, against opponents who never raise and call too often (thinking they're getting good odds), going even smaller can work very well.

12. When to Bet Very Large on the River

Bet very large (all-in for 4x+ pot) when you have a big nut advantage -- typically when draws miss and the board doesn't change from turn to river.

Example
200bb deep, LJ vs BB. K-7-4ss flop, bet/call. J turn, overbet 150%/call. Queen of clubs river. LJ has A-10 and 10-9 for straights, plus sets. BB has almost no nuts (would have raised earlier). JAM for 4.2x pot. LJ gains \(\frac{28.8 - 28.07}{1} = 0.77\) bb per hand by having all-in available -- that's 77bb/100 hands lost by capping at 150% pot.

When to All-In

  • Draws all missed on the river: Opponent's range is almost entirely marginal. Your range has either nuts or air
  • Turn nuts preserved on the river: Board didn't change meaningfully
  • Opponent's range is "all medium": They called a big turn bet with bluff-catchers; they have no nuts
Insight
The biggest EV gain from having an all-in size is with the nuts specifically. In one example, A-10 (the nuts) gained 6.16bb per hand by jamming vs capping at 150% pot -- that's 616bb/100. Even if all-in is rare, when it's correct, the EV gain is enormous.

What NOT to Value Bet on the River

When using giant sizes, hands like AK, A-K, or one-pair are NOT value bets. They belong in the checking range. If you bet AK on a board where you can jam, you either get called by better or fold out worse. Those hands are right at the boundary -- check them.

13. Exploitative Check-Raising Strategies

Against Auto-C-Bettors

When opponents c-bet 100% on boards where GTO checks 50%+, punish with check-raises:

Board TypeOpponent's Excess RangeYour Check-Raise
Connected (10-9-7)Too many marginal pairs (9x, 7x)LARGE (pot-sized) to pressure pairs
Uncoordinated (A-7-2)Too much trash (K-high, Q-high)SMALL (30% pot) -- they fold air to anything
Insight
Against auto-c-bettors, you should slow-play less -- not more. Their range is so weak that if you just check-call, they won't put in more money on the turn. Check-raise immediately to build the pot while they still have their "betting" range.

14. Multi-Street Sizing & Player-Type Adjustments

The Pot Growth Principle

Pots grow exponentially in no-limit. A slightly bigger flop bet cascades across streets:

Example
Pocket JJ on 10-8-7 vs straightforward player. By potting it on every street (pot flop, pot turn, all-in river), you get your entire stack in. If instead you bet 1/3 pot on the flop, the pot grows much more slowly across three streets -- leaving massive value on the table against a player who calls any bet.

Adjusting to Player Types

Player TypeYour Sizing Strategy
StraightforwardTheir bets are strong; fold to double-barrels. When they check, bet thin value (they won't bluff-raise). Small river value bets work well.
Loose-AggressiveOverbet-bluff rivers (they fold everything but straights to 2x pot). Check to induce bluffs on boards favoring your range. Call wider on turns.
Calling StationBet big for value (pot to 2x pot) -- they call "because they're getting odds." Never bluff-small (they call that too). Size up value, give up on bluffs.
Tight-PassiveTiny bluffs (20% pot, breakeven at 19%). Small value bets. Fold to their aggression (when they bet big, they have it). Bet wider for thin value since they won't raise.
Trap
Pay close attention to the specific bet sizes opponents use when they're bluffing vs value betting. Many live players use different sizes: some pot it with bluffs and overbet with nuts, some reverse it. If you can identify this pattern, it adds enormously to your win rate.

15. The Complete Decision Framework

Flop Sizing Flowchart

  1. Do I have the nut advantage?
    • YES: Is the board dynamic (draws but no made straight/flush)? -> BET LARGE (66-120% pot)
    • YES: Is there already a made straight/flush? -> BET MEDIUM (33-66% pot)
    • NO: Do I have a range advantage? -> BET SMALL (20-33% pot) frequently
    • NO range or nut advantage? -> CHECK most, bet tiny when you do

Turn Sizing Flowchart (After C-Bet + Call)

  1. Are the flop nuts still the nuts?
    • YES: -> OVERBET 150% with polarized range (nuts + draws)
    • NO: Did a flush/straight complete? -> Bet smaller (60% pot), less polarized
    • NO: Did the turn improve YOUR range (overcard)? -> BET LARGE

River Sizing Flowchart

  1. IP: Value bet at 50%+ pot. Check marginals. Never bet bottom pair
  2. OOP: Thin value at 10-33% pot. Protect checking range with traps
  3. Did draws miss? Do I have a nut advantage?
    • YES: -> ALL-IN (even for 4-11x pot) with nuts + bluffs
    • NO: -> Smaller sizes; both players have nuts
Insight -- The Meta-Principle
Every sizing decision reduces to one question: "Who has the nuts here?" Nut advantage = big bets, infrequently. Range advantage without nut advantage = small bets, frequently. Both = medium bets, frequently. Neither = check.
