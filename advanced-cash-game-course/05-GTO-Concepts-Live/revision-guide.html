1. Defending Your Range vs Aggression

Core Concept: Equity Realization Over Raw Equity

When deciding which hands to continue with facing aggression, the question is not "how much equity do I have right now?" but rather "how well can I realize my equity on future streets?"

Key Factors for Continuation Decisions

  • Nut potential: Can this hand make the nuts on future streets? In deep-stacked cash, this is everything.
  • Backdoor flush draws: Worth enormously more than raw equity suggests (0.5 to 1.2 BB of EV difference).
  • Clean outs: When you improve, are your outs "clean" (no reverse implied odds)?
  • Blocker effects: Blocking bluffs is way more important than blocking value, especially on rivers.

Practical Heuristics

  1. Adjust for bet sizing precisely: 65% pot and 85% pot are NOT the same -- 85% requires significantly tighter continues (8% more folding). Don't bucket sizes as "big."
  2. Adjust for positions/ranges: A hijack raise demands far more folds than a button raise -- the nut advantage is stronger from tighter ranges.
  3. Double flush draw boards: You have so many clean continues (suited hands, nut draws) that you can fold marginal top pair without backdoors.
  4. Jack-Ten offsuit is often a fold: On boards where making two pair puts you against straights, and you block bluff combos (opponent's KT, T9), JTo is a clear fold facing turn aggression.
Worked Example: H-2-5-0, HJ vs BB, T-5-4 Rainbow

Situation: Hijack opens (tight 20% range), you call from the BB. Flop T-5-4 rainbow. Hijack bets 65% pot.

Comparison: K\u2665J\u2665 (backdoor hearts) vs KJ no backdoor. KJ with backdoor hearts is worth +0.67 BB continuing. KJ without backdoor is a marginal fold. On any heart turn you pick up a nut flush draw and have an easy decision. The backdoor alone is worth almost a full big blind in EV -- the single biggest factor distinguishing two otherwise identical hands.

What happens at 85% pot instead? Sixes and sevens (mixed frequency calls at 65%) become pure folds. Ace-Queen offsuit goes from marginal continue to near-pure fold. The hands that survive both sizes are exactly the KJ/KQ with backdoor flush draws -- that is why they are so valuable.

Range position adjustment: If villain is on the button (wider range, ~35-40% hands), you have more equity across all runouts and can continue more top-pair type hands. HJ range (~20%) means they bet turns and rivers at much higher frequency because they hold more nutted combos -- force more folds on the flop accordingly.

Key Insight
Hands that can improve to the nuts over-realize equity massively. Having nut hands in your range lets you build aggressive strategies on future streets. Backdoor draws that develop into nut draws are the primary reason suited hands are worth so much more than offsuit.
Worked Example: Clean vs Dirty Outs -- Q-J-T-K board, Turn barrel

Situation: HJ opens, you call BB. Board Q\u2663J\u26652\u2663T\u2660 on turn. Villain overbets 1.5x pot.

You hold J\u2660T\u2660 (top two pair). Looks strong. But consider what happens when you improve: if you river a Jack, villain has AK, K9 for straights. If you river a Ten, villain has K9, Q9 for straights. If any spade hits, villain has flush draws they were barreling. JT is blocking villain's key bluff combos (KT, T9) -- the very hands you need villain to have for calling to be correct. Blocking bluffs is devastatingly bad for calling decisions.

Compare to K\u26547\u2665 (no pair, heart draw): Has reasonable equity against the whole range. When a heart hits, clean nut flush draw. When a King hits, top pair with no reverse implied odds. Does NOT block KT/T9 bluff combos. This hand calls more often despite being weaker in immediate showdown equity.

EV cost of error: Calling JTo at 1.5x pot when you should fold costs approximately 1.5-2.5 BBs of EV per occurrence. Folding K7h when you should call costs similar. The errors compound across sessions because live players almost never make these adjustments correctly.

2. Nut Advantage and Over-Realizing Equity

Definition
Nut Advantage: When one player's range contains significantly more combinations of the strongest possible hands (sets, straights, flushes, etc.) than the other's. This is the primary driver of who gets to bet big.

Attacking Small Bets (Check-Raise Strategy)

  1. Build robust check-raise ranges: Start with two pair+ for value, add flush draws, straight draws, and hands that wrap future nut cards
  2. Include ace-of-suit-X combos that cover nut flush runouts
  3. Include bottom pair that wraps: hands like 5-3s, 6-3s on K-4-3 boards
  4. Increase frequency against over-c-betters: Take your GTO check-raise strategy and roughly 2x all frequencies
  5. Use big sizes: If your value threshold is two pair+, you want a big check-raise size
Worked Example: Check-Raise Construction -- K\u26434\u26433\u2660, BTN vs BB, 200 BB deep

Situation: Button opens, you call from the BB with K\u26506\u2665 (65s). 200 BB deep. Flop K\u26434\u26433\u2660 rainbow. Button bets small (~25% pot). Justin's actual hand in his training session.

Step 1 -- Value threshold: At 200 BB you do NOT check-raise top pair. King-Queen offsuit goes into check-call. Value hands are K4, K3, 43, sets only. These hands say "I have two pair or better" and justify a big raise size (Justin raised to 125 into ~20 at 200 BB deep).

Step 2 -- Add draws: Spade flush draws (7\u26485\u2648 etc.), open-ended straight draws (75s, 52s that make wheel or 6-high straights).

Step 3 -- Cover nut runouts: A\u2648x hands (A\u26485\u2665, A\u26486\u2665) that wrap the 3 and 4. Without these, on a 6\u2648 turn (flush + straight complete) you have zero bluffs. These hands give you credible bluffs on the worst runouts. 5-3s, 6-3s (bottom pair that wraps) serve the same purpose.

Against an over-c-bettor (~100% frequency vs GTO 60%): Same hand types, but increase all frequencies approximately 2x. If 6-5o was check-raising 30% of the time, now do it 60%. Do not add new hand types -- you will arrive at the turn with unmanageable ranges. The extra folds you generate on the flop means you don't actually face turn spots at higher frequency than normal.

Why the big size? Your value threshold (two pair+) vs their betting range (medium equity hands, top pairs) creates a massive gap at the top of the equity distribution. Big size forces them to fold everything in their middle -- all the King-X single pair hands, all the pocket pairs below two pair.

The One-Track Mind Trap
After check-raising flop and barreling turn, don't default to jamming the river. Adapt: if the nuts change (flush completes), block instead of jam. If nuts stay the same (brick), jam confidently.
Worked Example: Turn Barreling and River Adaptation -- K-4-3, Turn 6, River 7\u2660 vs 2\u2666

Situation continued from above: You check-raised K\u26434\u26433\u2660 to 125 with 6\u26505\u2665. Button calls. Turn is 6\u2663. You have 49% raw equity but a massive nut advantage: you hold all the straights (75s, 52s now complete), all the sets, all the two pairs. The button has mostly single-pair King-X hands.

Turn action (6\u2663): Pot-bet at very high frequency (~75%). The 49% equity does not matter -- the high-end equity gap is enormous. Folding out all their King-X, pocket Jacks, pocket Queens. You have a third of your range as sets or straights in this spot. This is what over-realizing equity looks like: 46% raw equity realizing more than 50% pot share.

River scenario A -- 7\u2660 (flush completes): The opponent called your pot-bet with flush draws they were barreling the flop with. They now have many flushes. Your nut advantage has shifted. Your sets and straights are now sharing the high end with their flushes. Correct play: block bet (not jam). Let them develop a raise range. Jamming when nuts are shared costs you EV because you're now behind a significant portion of their range.

River scenario B -- 2\u2666 (complete brick): Nuts have not changed. You still have all the straights and sets; they cannot have improved. Your nut advantage is preserved from the turn. Correct play: jam. You have about 30% of your range as sets or straights -- the opponent cannot call profitably with any single pair hand. Even with 5x pot behind, jamming is correct.

EV cost of misadapting: Jamming on a flush-completing river when you should block costs roughly 3-6 BB per occurrence because you fold out hands worse than yours (their bluffs fold) and get called/raised only by hands that beat you. Blocking on a brick when you should jam costs roughly 2-4 BB by leaving value on the table.

3. Playing Out of Position (OOP as PFR)

Two Range-Based Rules

  1. The later your RFI position, the more you check: Cutoff opening checks far more than UTG opening
  2. The closer the caller's position, the more you check: Hijack calling your lojack means much tighter defending range

Board Texture Framework

Board TypeStrategyRationale
Ten-high and lowerPure check entire rangeRange disadvantage or very close equities; board changes often
Two high cards (J-T-x, Q-J-x)Develop ~25-33% c-bets smallReasonable connectivity with offsuit combos
Ace-high (A-9-3, A-5-2)Very passive, high check frequencyStatic equities; single pair ace-x rarely gets three streets
One high card (K-3-4)Similar to ace-high, lean passiveEquities static; be careful with overpairs

The Sliding Scale Principle

Key Insight
The more you check, the more aggressive you can be on future streets. Pure check on flop → all nutted hands retained → aggressive check-raises and delayed c-bets with big sizes. Develop flop bets → condensed checking range → very low check-raise frequency.
Worked Example: The Sliding Scale in Action -- T-6-5 LJ vs BTN, Check-Check then Turn

Situation: You open the lojack, button calls. Flop T\u26656\u26655\u2660 (ten-high). You pure-check your entire range -- the rule for ten-high and lower boards.

Why pure check works: You retain ALL overpairs (JJ, QQ, KK, AA), ALL sets (TT, 66, 55), ALL strong draws. These are in your range ready to spring the trap. If instead you had c-bet 33% of your range, your checking range becomes condensed -- JJ/QQ get moved into the betting range -- and you can no longer check-raise aggressively.

After check-check (brick turn, e.g., 2\u2666): You retained everything. Now you pot-bet with a polarized range: QQ-AA for value, 65o/KJo type hands as bluffs. The button, who checked back a ten-high flop, is now forced to fold all their middle equity. They cannot call pot-bets without strong hands. This is the sliding scale payoff.

Compare -- if you had c-bet 33% then checked: Button facing your check now sees a condensed range (you moved many strong hands into your betting range). They should respond by betting big and polarized. Your check-raise frequency on the turn drops to near-zero because you don't have enough nutted hands to support it.

IP's counter-adjustment: Versus your pure-check range on T-6-5, the button should bet small (33-40% pot) with a linear range because you are still fully uncapped. Versus your bet-then-check range, they bet big (65%+) with a polarized range because your range is now condensed. Watch for this as a tell on opponent's c-bet strategy.

Delayed C-Bet Strategy (Check-Check-Bet)

  • Brick turns: Pot-sized bet with polarized range. You have all overpairs, sets, strong draws.
  • High card turns (e.g., King): Smaller sizes, higher frequency. Your offsuit KQ/KJ combos now connect.
  • Flush-completing turns: Simply choose your flush draw hands as barrels.
Worked Example: Delayed C-Bet Turn Choices -- T-6-5 then King turn vs Club turn vs 8 turn

King turn (e.g., T-6-5-K): Now your offsuit region connects -- KQo, KJo are now top pair. Use smaller sizing at higher frequency. The King gave you a range advantage: your KQ/KJ combos connect while the button has fewer of them (they would have raised or re-raised with many KX preflop from the button). Push this equity advantage across a wide range.

Club turn (flush draw completes): Do not reach for random combos as bluffs. Simply choose your club-suited hands as bluffs -- they have equity to make the nut flush on the river. Your 8\u26637\u2663 type hands now have real equity to improve. Non-club hands without sets or overpairs check at high frequency.

Brick turn (e.g., T-6-5-2 off-suit): Pot-size bet with your overpairs (QQ-AA) and bluffs (KJo, QJo without hearts/spades). Aces tend to check at higher frequency because (a) the Ace in your hand unblocks the button's check-back range on the flop -- they are more likely to have checked back KA, QA type hands -- so against their wider range, you want to let them bet. (b) Aces need less protection. Both overpairs and aces in the right combo choose the checking option to gain EV in that specific way.

Common Exploits vs Weaker Players

  • If opponents won't barrel turns and rivers aggressively when checked to:
  • Fast-play more often: Don't protect your checking range as strongly
  • Bet overpairs on ten-high boards: Just put money in directly
  • Check-raise more aggressively: They won't punish your weakened passive range

4. Deuce-Seven Game Strategy

Baseline
The biggest mistake is playing huge pots with 7-2. The bounty is ~4-5 BB per player, so risking hundreds of BBs to win it is -EV.

Pre-Flop Approach

  • Over-limping is often better than raising/3-betting
  • Loose-passive pre-flop strategy keeps pots small
  • Remove 7-2 from aggressive lines (limp-re-raise, cold 4-bet)

Post-Flop Approach

  • Small stabs on favorable flops: Bet ~25% pot to fold out marginal hands cheaply
  • Check-check then block river: Very deceptive; opponents expect aggression with 7-2
  • Give up when caught: Once called on the turn with no equity, don't compound losses

Leveraging for Non-7-2 Hands

The 7-2 game adds ~12 bluff combos to your perceived range. Use this to overbet for value with strong hands (opponents hero-call thinking 7-2).

5. Stand-Up Game Strategy

Mental Model
Think of it as each standing player putting an extra ante in (roughly 1 BB per standing player).
  • See more flops with wider ranges; suited hands, even weak ones (K2s), become profitable calls
  • As players sit down, the ante shrinks -- adjust tightness accordingly
  • Final two dynamics: Massively widen your range; stack off lighter (bounty is huge relative to pot)
  • Limping is fine: Keep pots manageable, especially early
  • Position matters even more: Having the button when 2-3 players remain is enormous

6. Transitioning from Live to Online

Pre-Flop Adjustments

  • Play three-bet or fold from HJ, CO, and SB (no flatting)
  • Develop calling ranges only from BTN and BB
  • Cut bottom opens (no KTo from lojack in raked games)
  • Tighten big blind defense -- rake makes marginal calls unprofitable

Post-Flop Adjustments

  • Defending vs aggression: Fringe continues (mixing-frequency calls) become folds due to rake
  • C-bet less from wider ranges: When opening wider, range advantage decreases -- check more
  • Backdoor draws still crucial: Same deep-stack principles apply, just with tighter ranges

Practical Tips

  • Move down stakes: Online 2/5 is much tougher than live 2/5
  • Limit tables initially: Focus on decision quality over volume
  • Short stackers change your range shape: Against 30-40 BB players, high-card hands gain value over suited connectors
  • Win rate comes from BTN and CO: Maximize volume in late position

7. Key Formulas

Minimum Defense Frequency (MDF)
When facing a bet of size B into pot P:
\(\text{MDF} = \frac{P}{P + B}\)

Worked example: Pot is 20bb, villain bets 15bb (75% pot). MDF = 20 / (20 + 15) = 20/35 = 57%. You must continue with 57% of your range to prevent auto-profit bluffs. If you folded 50%, villain profits by bluffing with any two cards.
Pot Odds for Calling
\(\text{Required equity} = \frac{\text{Call}}{\text{Pot} + \text{Opponent's Bet} + \text{Call}}\)

Worked example: Villain bets 50bb into a 40bb pot. You must call 50bb. Required equity = 50 / (40 + 50 + 50) = 50/140 = 36%. If your hand has more than 36% equity against villain's range, calling is +EV. If less, folding is correct.
Full Situation: Applying MDF Precisely -- HJ vs BB, T-5-4 Rainbow, 65% Pot Bet

Setup: Live 2-5 NL, 100bb. HJ raises (tight ~20% range), BB calls. Pot: ~12bb. Flop: T♣5♥4♠ (rainbow). HJ bets 65% pot (~7.8bb). BB must defend 12 / (12 + 7.8) = 61% of their range to make bluffing unprofitable.

What BB continues with: Any pair, any strong draw, any backdoor flush draw + overcard. Specifically: T-x (top pair), 5-x, 4-x, 9-8, 7-6, 6-3 (straight draws), A-5s, A-4s (pair + wheel draw), K-J with a backdoor flush draw. The backdoor flush draw alone is worth ~0.5-0.7bb in EV -- it decides close cases like KJ with vs KJ without hearts.

What changes at 85% pot instead: MDF = 12 / (12 + 10.2) = 54%. Now 7% fewer hands defend. The hands eliminated are exactly the "mixing" frequency hands -- 7-6 without a flush suit, 6-3 without a flush draw. K-J without any backdoor becomes a near-pure fold at 85% but mixed at 65%. This is why Saliba's point that "65% and 85% are NOT the same" is so actionable: the hands in your range around the fold threshold swing from call to fold, and they make up 8-10% of your total defending range. That's real money.

Equity Realization
Actual pot share vs. raw equity share. OOP under-realizes; having nuts in range over-realizes.

8. Summary: The Three Pillars

  1. Defend with nut potential: Continue hands that can make the nuts, not just hands with raw equity
  2. Push nut advantage aggressively: When you have more nutted combos, use big sizes to fold out equity and over-realize
  3. Play OOP passively then spring the trap: High check frequencies lead to aggressive future actions via check-raises and delayed c-bets
