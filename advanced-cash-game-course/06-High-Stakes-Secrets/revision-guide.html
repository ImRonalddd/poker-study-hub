1. Why You're Thinking About GTO Incorrectly

why-youre-thinking-about-gto-poker-incorrectly
Central Thesis
Frequencies matter more than combos. If your opponent uses the wrong combos but correct frequencies, you gain almost no EV. If your opponent uses the right combos but wrong frequencies (even slightly), you can exploit them massively with pure strategies.

Combo Substitution Demo

Full Situation
HJ opens 3BB, BB calls. 100BB deep, heads-up to the river. Flop checks through, turn checks through. On the river with a pot-sized bet available, the BB must construct a strategy. The BB's value hands are Kx (top pair is strong enough to go for full pot). With a pot-sized bet, the math requires a 2:1 value-to-bluff ratio and the defender calls at 50% (MDF). Chris shows two strategies side by side:

Optimal strategy: BB bluffs with QJ, Q9, J9 -- hands with no showdown value that gain nothing by checking.
Alternative strategy: Exact same betting frequency, but swap those bluffs to AJ, AQ instead.

Result: zero EV loss in the betting line. The opponent cannot exploit the "wrong" combos because the frequencies are identical. The only small loss comes from bluffing AJ/AQ (which had showdown value and could have won at showdown), not from opponent adjustment.

Frequency Error Demo

Same River Spot -- Frequency Deviation
Using the same HJ vs BB river spot, now keep the right combos (QJ, Q9, J9) but add a couple extra bluffs -- say J9 and Q9 appear at higher frequency than optimal. The opponent now over-bluffs by a small amount.

Result: pure call with every bluff catcher. EV of the betting line plummets by 0.7 BB per betting instance. That means every single time the bet is made, it performs 0.7 BB worse than the balanced version. Against a player who spots this, even tiny frequency errors create massive exploits.

Solver Output Sensitivity

Warning
Same river node, two different input ranges: In version one, the in-position player is assigned a standard GTO range (lots of Tx checkbacks on the flop, some Kx bets). In version two, assume a more ABC opponent who checks back Kx too often (trapping) and bets Tx less. Give them slightly more Kx and slightly less Tx arriving on the river.

Result: betting frequency drops by 10% and the value threshold shifts from K3 to K8 -- a massive shift in what qualifies as a value bet. If you lazily assume "standard GTO ranges" without observing what your specific opponent actually does, you will systematically under- or over-value hands. The player who range-traps Kx makes the entire spot play differently.

How to Use Combos in Practice

  • Use combos as an anchor to maintain proper frequencies, not as the end goal
  • Think in hand classes: "I use my gutshots as bluffs" or "I continue all high flush draws"
  • Focus on overall strategy: "Am I bluffing enough here?" and "Is my value-to-bluff ratio reasonable?"
  • Judge opponents on the frequency scale -- are they under-bluffing or over-bluffing overall?

2. GTO vs Exploit: Making Money

gto-vs-exploit-making-in-cash-games

The Math of Bluff Catching

Real Hand: Chris Brewer vs Jason Koon -- Super High Roller, ~10 Players Left
Setup: Small blind (Chris) limps, Koon ISOs from the big blind. ~50BB effective with ICM pressure (both near average stack, full pay). Flop: A-A-3 rainbow. Chris checks, Koon bets 25% pot. Turn: Q of diamonds. Chris checks, Koon bets ~60% pot. River: off-suit brick. Chris checks. Koon jams approximately 360 into 310.

Chris's bluff-catching math:
Required equity for chips = 360 / (310 + 360 + 360) = 360 / 1030 ≈ 35%
With ICM risk premium: roughly 40-47% needed
At 3:2 ratio: for every 3 value combos, Koon needs 2 bluffs to make Chris indifferent

Counting Koon's value range: AQ off (3 combos), A10 off (3 combos), suited AK as an iso call, KJ of diamonds (4 combos, maybe discounted), 6 combos of full houses (e.g. A3s), ~12 flushes (KJ, K9, 87 of diamonds) = roughly 22 value combos
Required bluffs: ~14 combos

Chris holds A8 of diamonds: Ace blocks AQ/A10 (removes value), 8 of diamonds blocks some low diamond flushes (slightly reduces bluff combos). Net: a reasonable bluff catcher. Chris folds in the tournament on instinct despite theory suggesting a call -- he acknowledges it was probably a theory call he talked himself out of.

Combo Counting Process

  1. Identify value hands (full houses, flushes, trips+) given the board and opponent's likely ISO/calling range
  2. Estimate total value combos (this hand: ~22 combos)
  3. Calculate required bluffs at the bet size: at 3:2 ratio, need ~14 bluff combos
  4. Assess: can this opponent physically find those bluffs? What does their preflop range look like? Did they ISO tight or wide?

Equilibrium Fragility

Critical Concept
Under-bluff by even 0.1%: defender should pure fold every bluff catcher.
Over-bluff by any amount: defender should pure call every bluff catcher.
There is no gradual adjustment -- equilibrium is outrageously sensitive.

How Preflop Construction Cascades

Preflop TypeDescriptionPostflop Consequence
Correct ISO rangeIncludes suited trash (72s, 82s, 65s)Bluffing is manageable but requires discipline
Tight ISO rangeKnows to ISO wide but under-does itMuch harder to find bluff combos; AK call worth only 2.5 BB instead of 8 BB
No dustRaises only decent+ holdingsNearly impossible for humans to bluff enough; bluff catchers have zero EV

The Exploit Framework

  1. Identify preflop tendencies -- are they ISO-ing wide enough?
  2. Map what that demands post-flop -- if they ISO too few trash hands, they physically cannot bluff enough
  3. Tight range spots (UTG vs BB on high boards): opponents struggle to bluff enough
  4. Wide range spots (BTN vs BB): opponents tend to over-bluff
Key Insight
Theory and exploits are "one package." You need a baseline understanding to identify where opponents deviate, then exploit accordingly.

3. Rampage Poker's Biggest Hands Part 1

high-stakes-analysis-rampage-pokers-biggest-hands-part-1

Hand 1: A8s 4-Bet Bluff vs Nick (Rating: 6.5-7/10)

Blocker Selection for Deep-Stack 4-Bet Bluffs

Situation: Deep-stacked cash game. Rampage holds A8 suited and 4-bets as a bluff against Nick's 3-bet.

Why A8s is suboptimal: Having an 8 is bad for bluffing because 88 is one of Nick's easiest hands to continue with (he calls a 4-bet with pocket eights at most stack depths). By holding an 8, you remove one combo of 88 from his range -- meaning his remaining range is slightly stronger. You want him to have 88 (a hand he might fold), not block it.

Better bluff selection: A hand with a King (like K5s) blocks KQ and KJs -- hands Nick is on the fence about calling with. When you block his borderline calls, his remaining range is weighted toward folds, increasing your bluff's success rate.

General principle: When bluffing deep, select hands that unblock opponent's folding range (let them have the hands they'd fold) and block their continuing range (remove the hands they'd call with).

Hand 2: KK vs Mariano (~1000 BB Deep)

Full Situation: KK at 1000bb Deep -- Why You Might Fold Kings

Setup: Ultra-high-stakes live cash, ~1000bb effective. Mariano raises from early position. Rampage is in late position with K♥K♣. The question: call or fold to a river jam?

Why KK is "exactly mediocre": At 1000bb, the SPR is enormous. On the river with a significant bet, KK has no interaction with Mariano's value hands -- KK doesn't block AA (the most likely value hand), doesn't block AK (another likely value), doesn't block sets (33, 44, etc. depending on board). The only thing KK does is block K-T and K-9 type hands -- exactly the combos Mariano might use as bluffs. If you hold KK, Mariano is less likely to have K-T as a bluff, which means his range, when he bets, is weighted MORE toward value.

Compare to JT: J-T has positive interaction -- it unblocks J-X and T-X type hands that Mariano might be bluffing with (J9, T8, QT, etc.). JT doesn't block any likely value hands (AA, KK, sets on this board). When you hold JT, Mariano's betting range contains more bluffs because you haven't removed bluff combos from his range.

Lesson: At extreme stack depths, even a strong hand like KK can be a fold or mix-fold if it blocks bluffs and unblocks value. The blocker analysis completely reverses intuition -- "I have a strong hand" is not the same as "I am a good bluff catcher." Good bluff catchers unblock bluffs and block value.

Key Insight
At deep stacks, even strong-looking hands can be clear folds when they block bluffs and unblock value.

Hand 3: A7 vs Queens Bluff (Rating: "Liked a lot")

  • Multi-way pot, A7s with backdoor flush draw. The straight is so common in opponent's range that less than a straight is questionable for value.
  • Queens can maybe value bet, but Jacks cannot. Nine-eight would be "kind of sad" because it doesn't fold to a raise.

Hand 4: Trips vs Trips (Rating: "Really good")

  • Consider opponent's position and range. Straddle has way more suited jacks than small blind would.
  • Put money in when you have a good hand and opponent has a good hand.

4. Rampage Poker's Biggest Hands Part 2

high-stakes-analysis-rampage-pokers-biggest-hands-part-2

Hand 1: AKo Hero Call vs Tilted Nick (Rating: 10/10)

Full Situation: AKo Hero Call, Nick Tilting, ~400bb Deep

Setup: High-stakes live cash, 400-500bb effective. Nick has been 3-betting extremely wide while tilting. Rampage opens in late position. Nick 3-bets from the blinds. Rampage holds A♣K♦ and flats (calls) the 3-bet.

Why flat instead of 4-bet? At 400-500bb deep, OOP 3-bets should be 7-10x the open to charge for positional disadvantage. If Rampage 4-bets, Nick -- on tilt and 3-betting everything -- will 5-bet or call, creating a massive inflated pot where Rampage is OOP to Nick with AKo and ~400bb behind. That is a dangerous situation even with AK: AK has ~57% equity vs QQ and only ~46% vs KK, and deep stacks mean any edge in a bloated pot becomes amplified.

Why AKo is a great call (not fold): (1) Blocks AQ and KQ -- hands Nick uses for value in his 3-bet range. (2) Beats every bluff Nick might 3-bet (97s, 65s, random J-3s while tilting). (3) Has strong postflop realization in position -- you will flop an A or K about 27% of the time, and any ace-high board is a nightmare for Nick's 3-bet bluffs. (4) Even against Nick's real value hands (QQ, JJ), AKo is a flip or ahead of JJ (3 combos).

On an ace-high flop: Rampage now has top pair top kicker and can call down multiple streets. On a low flop (7-4-2), Rampage has ace-high and can fold to continued aggression without having inflated the pot preflop. This flexibility is worth more than 4-betting and bloating the pot.

Hand 2: KK in 5-Bet Pot (Rating: 1.5/10)

Warning
At deep stacks, only 5-bet AA and AKs. Kings should almost always flat a 4-bet. AKs outranks KK for 5-betting because having an Ace blocks opponent's Aces.
  • Even if your 5-bet range is only 1.5% of hands, eliminating it entirely lets opponents 4-bet anything they want
  • KK on AJJ in a 5-bet pot with tight ranges -- you're drawing nearly dead
"Winning poker is not supposed to be very sexy and glamorous. Show up, make reasonable decisions, have well-constructed ranges." -- Chris Brewer

Hand 3: A8 Bluff vs Kings (Deep-Stack Analysis)

  • Should mostly check as PFR when OOP against a condensed button calling range
  • Bluff selection should prioritize having the Ace of the trip suit (blocks quads)
  • Only ~3 bluff combos available, still under-bluffing

Deep-Stack Cash Game Principles

  1. Play tight ranges -- you're supposed to mostly have it
  2. When calling, expect to lose -- majority of bluff-catching calls lose to value. That's correct play.
  3. When betting, mostly have value -- the math demands far fewer bluffs than people think
  4. Over-bluffing is the #1 leak at all stakes in tight-value spots
  5. Suitedness is king deep -- AKs dramatically outranks AKo

5. Chris Brewer's Biggest Hands

high-stakes-analysis-my-biggest-cash-game-hands

Hand 1: KQo River Bluff vs Ult (2018, ~125 BB)

  • Chris had a structured 4-bet strategy: QQ+ always, Jacks mixed, half KJo/A10o, 25% AJ/KQo, A6s as pure 4-bet bluff
  • Key lesson: Having any designed plan is better than winging it. Even an imperfect strategy gives you defined ranges.
  • At the bottom of 4-bet range with only 1-2 allowed bluff combos -- be very disciplined

Hand 2: AKs Geometric Sizing (vs Matt Sue)

  • Don't range bet AX7 boards. Equity split is only 54-45 -- not enough for range betting.
  • Check kings, queens, jacks (way ahead/way behind hands).
  • Turn barrel with geometric sizing: 80-90% pot to set up river jam. On the turn, you have equity disadvantage but nut advantage -- polarize and size up.
  • AK suit matters: Red AK (no flush draw interaction) is always a stack-off. Club/spade AK (interacts with flush draws) is indifferent.
Gap Theory
You need a better hand to call than to bet. On the turn, your range polarizes: bet AJ+ for value or bluff. No more medium hands.

Hand 3: QJo Block Bet and Call

  • What your opponent does on earlier streets completely changes later-street solutions
  • If opponent checks back AK on the turn (common at lower stakes), the river raise becomes much more dangerous

Hand 4: KJs Turn Barrel (vs Andy, ~125 BB 3-bet pot)

Full Situation: KJs Turn Barrel in a 3-Bet Pot, ~125bb

Setup: 3-bet pot, ~125bb effective. Chris c-bets the flop 4♠4♥3♣ at 75% pot. Andy calls. Turn: K♥ (giving Chris K♥J♠, now top pair plus flush draw plus gutshot to AQ type straights). Pot: ~100bb after the turn card.

Why pot-sized barrel on the turn with KJs: (1) Top pair king is strong value against Andy's likely range -- Andy called a 75% pot bet on 4-4-3, so his range is mostly: small pairs (AA-55), Ax hands with some equity, and rare 4x hands. On a king turn, all of Andy's 55-99 range is now behind top pair. (2) The flush draw adds approximately 9 outs. (3) The gutshot adds 3-4 more outs. Combined: ~11-13 clean outs if called. (4) KJ blocks AJ (Andy's possible holdings), making some of his calling range weaker. (5) 100% pot size forces Andy to choose: commit or fold. Many of Andy's pairs from the 4-4-3 flop are now behind and face impossible decisions.

Andy's counter-play (jamming his set): If Andy held 33 (set of threes), he faces a massive SPR situation. By jamming, Andy is saying: "I have a set, you have the king, fold." He's targeting Chris's top pairs and pairs that cannot commit 125bb. Against Chris's specific hand (KJs), Andy's set is actually a ~2:1 favorite -- KJs has top pair + flush draw + gutshot but still loses to a set most of the time at showdown. Chris must evaluate: do I have enough equity to call a jam? With ~35-40% equity, and needing ~40% to call, it's borderline. Chris should have folded (he was slightly wrong to call even with flush draw).

Lesson: A combo draw (top pair + flush draw + gutshot) is not always enough to call a jam vs a range weighted toward sets. Count outs carefully: 9 flush outs + maybe 3 gutshot outs but MINUS any outs that don't improve enough = real outs. When facing a 4-4-3 caller who then jams on a King turn, his range for jamming is mostly sets (44, 33) or occasionally 43s. Against that range, 35% equity is marginal for a call-off.

Balance vs Dominant Strategy

Key Concept
Don't slow-play to "be balanced." GTO is about choosing the dominant EV option. If betting is clearly higher EV than checking, just bet. Mix only when truly indifferent. Backdoor equity drives bluffing frequency: hands without backdoor draws are the most aggressive bluffs.

6. Core Themes Across All Lessons

1. Frequencies Over Combos

  • Wrong combos with right frequencies = almost no exploit available
  • Right combos with wrong frequencies = massive exploit available

2. Preflop Range Construction Cascades

  • Every preflop error compounds through the hand
  • Missing trash in your ISO range = impossible to bluff later streets
  • Too-tight 3-bet range = linear post-flop play (less polarization)

3. Equilibrium Is Outrageously Fragile

  • Even 0.1% deviation from optimal bluff frequency creates a pure strategy response
  • Under-bluff by any amount = opponent should never call a bluff catcher
  • Over-bluff by any amount = opponent should always call a bluff catcher

4. Deep-Stack Adjustments

  • Suitedness is king: AKs >> AKo. Suited connectors rise dramatically.
  • 3-bet and 5-bet ranges get very tight and use very large sizing
  • Play tight ranges, bet big when you have it, and mostly have value

5. Blocker Logic for Hand Selection

  • When bluffing: unblock opponent's folds, block their calls/raises
  • When bluff-catching: block opponent's value, unblock their bluffs
  • Suit blockers matter enormously

6. The Theory-Exploit Continuum

  • Theory and exploits are "one package"
  • Use GTO as a baseline to identify where opponents deviate
  • In tight-range spots: opponents struggle to bluff enough (fold more bluff catchers)
  • In wide-range spots: opponents tend to over-bluff (call more bluff catchers)
