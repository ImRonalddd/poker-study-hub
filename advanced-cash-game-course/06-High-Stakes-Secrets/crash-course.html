1. Frequencies Over Combos

Core Principle Frequencies are more important than specific combos. If your opponent uses the wrong combos but maintains correct frequencies, you gain almost nothing. If their frequencies are off -- even slightly -- you can exploit them with pure strategies.

The Experiment

Chris demonstrates with a HJ vs BB river spot (pot-sized bet, 2:1 value-to-bluff ratio):

ScenarioChangeEV Loss
Optimal combos, optimal frequencyBaseline0 BB
Wrong combos, right frequencySwap QJ/Q9/J9 bluffs for AJ/AQ~0 BB in betting line
Right combos, slightly overbluffingAdd Q9, J9 beyond optimal0.7 BB (massive)
Practical Takeaway Think in hand classes, not individual combos. "I bluff with my gutshots" or "all low flush draws give up" is sufficient. Your goal is no noticeable frequency skew.

Stop Asking "What Did My Hand Play As?"

This promotes a micro, combo-focused mindset. Instead ask:

  • "Am I bluffing enough in this spot?"
  • "Is my value-to-bluff ratio reasonable?"
  • "On a frequency scale, is my opponent over- or under-bluffing?"

2. Solver Sensitivity

Small input changes produce dramatically different outputs:

Example Give opponent slightly more Kx and slightly less Tx on a river node:
  • Betting frequency drops by 10%
  • Value threshold shifts from K3 to K8
If you assume "standard GTO ranges" without considering actual tendencies, you will systematically misvalue hands.

This is why memorizing solver outputs is inferior to understanding the mechanics behind them: what frequencies are demanded, what the nut advantage looks like, and how ranges interact.

3. Equilibrium Fragility

The Nuts vs Air Toy Game

Setup: Aces vs 72s, board 4-4-4-5-5, pot = 300 chips, stack = 100 chips (one-third pot jam).

Bettor's StrategyDefender's ResponseBluff Catcher EV
Optimal (75% of 72s bluff)Call at MDF (50%)0
Under-bluff by 0.1%Pure fold everything0
Over-bluff by any amountPure call everything> 0
Critical Insight There is no gradual adjustment. Equilibrium is an "outrageously sensitive" knife-edge. Any deviation in either direction triggers a pure strategy response. This is why frequency errors are so costly.

Implication for live play: against opponents you believe are well-balanced (like Jason Koon), you cannot confidently deviate. Against opponents with known frequency leaks, you should deviate maximally.

4. Bluff Catching Math

Pot Odds and Required Bluff Frequency

When facing a bet, calculate what percentage of the time you need to be good:

\[\text{Required equity} = \frac{\text{Call Amount}}{\text{Pot} + \text{Bet} + \text{Call}}\]

Example: Chris vs Jason Koon

  • Bet: 360 into pot of 310
  • Required equity for chips: \(\frac{360}{310 + 360 + 360} \approx 35\%\)
  • With ICM risk premium: ~40%
  • At 3:2 ratio (every 3 value combos, need 2 bluffs): need ~14 bluff combos against ~22 value combos

Blocker Logic for Bluff Catching

Your hand's blockers determine its quality as a bluff catcher:

You BlockEffectQuality
Opponent's value hands (AQ, AT)Fewer value combos in their rangeBetter call
Opponent's bluffs (low spades, offsuit draws)Fewer bluff combos in their rangeWorse call
NeitherNeutralAverage
Key Example Chris holds A8 with the 8 of diamonds. The Ace blocks AQ/AT (value) -- good. The 8 of diamonds blocks some flush draws -- slightly bad, but most flushes use higher cards. Net: a reasonable bluff catcher.

5. Preflop Range Cascading

Every preflop decision compounds through the entire hand. Chris demonstrates with three versions of the same spot:

Preflop TypeISO RangeRiver Consequence
CorrectIncludes suited trash (72s, 82s, 65s)Can bluff but must be disciplined; easy to over-do with one-spade hands
Slightly tight50% fewer offsuit bluffsTrips drops from good call to marginal; AK call worth 2.5 BB instead of 8 BB
No dustOnly decent+ handsMust bluff with 76cc, 87dd -- nearly impossible; all bluff catchers have 0 EV
Practical Rule Before reacting to a river bet, ask: "What does this player's preflop range look like?" If they ISO too few trash hands, they physically cannot bluff enough on later streets. If they ISO too wide, they will have too many bluffs.

6. Deep-Stack Principles

Suitedness Is King

At deep stacks (300+ BB), suited hands dramatically outperform offsuit equivalents:

  • AKs >> AKo -- AKs is above KK in effective hand rankings for 5-betting; AKo is equivalent to TT-JJ
  • Having an Ace in your hand blocks opponent's Aces (critical for 5-bet pots)
  • Over-realization of equity at high SPR means suited hands extract far more value

3-Bet and 5-Bet Sizing

Depth3-Bet Size (OOP)Range
100 BB3-4x openStandard polarized
200-400 BB7-10x openVery tight: AA, AKs, KK, some KQs/QJs
1000+ BBMay decrease slightlyMore board coverage, but still tight
Deep-Stack Rules
  1. Play tight ranges -- you are supposed to mostly have it
  2. When calling, expect to lose -- majority of bluff catcher calls lose. That is correct play.
  3. When betting, mostly have value -- with 12 value combos, you get only ~6 bluffs
  4. Over-bluffing is the #1 leak -- one offsuit combo already gives you 12 bluffs (too many!)
  5. "Winning poker is not supposed to be sexy and glamorous"

Geometric Sizing on the Turn

After c-betting the flop, you often have an equity disadvantage but nut advantage on the turn (classic gap theory). The response:

  • Polarize your range: bet strong hands and bluffs, check everything in between
  • Use large sizes (80-133% pot) to force indifference from opponent's medium hands
  • Goal: make opponent's AJ indifferent so you win maximum with AK

7. The Exploit Framework

Chris's Framework Theory and exploits are "one package." You need a baseline understanding of GTO to identify where opponents deviate, then exploit the deviation maximally.

The Frequency Scale

Judge opponents on a scale of under-bluffing to over-bluffing, not on whether they "could be bluffing":

  • "My opponent can always be bluffing" -- true but irrelevant
  • "Can they hit the required bluff frequency?" -- this is the real question
  • Use words like "never" and "always" carefully -- think in percentages

8. Hand Analysis Highlights

AKo Hero Call vs Tilted Opponent

Situation QQ2 flop in 3-bet pot. Opponent checked, then bet turn + river. AKo blocks AQ, KQ (all value), beats every bluff. "Looks like a hero call but you just have a very strong hand."

Blocker Selection When Bluffing Deep

Situation Trips on board, need to select river bluffs. Chris's approach:
  • Prioritize the Ace of the trip suit (blocks quads)
  • Preferred bluffs: A♥J♠, A♥T♠, A♥K♠ -- only 3 combos, still under-bluffing
  • Opponent folding KK is not crazy: KK beats zero value hands in your range

KK as a Fold at 1000 BB Deep

Situation 3-bet pot, unfavorable runout. KK on the river doesn't beat any value hand and only has negative interaction (blocks KT, K9 which are bluffs). JT is a better call because it has positive interaction. "You just gotta fold sometimes."

Balance vs Dominant Strategy

Principle Don't slow-play to "be balanced." GTO is about choosing the dominant EV option. If betting is clearly higher EV, just bet. You mix only when two actions have identical EV.

The Turn Barrel: KJs on 4-4-3-T

Why this is an excellent turn barrel:

  • Blocks opponent's best non-boat hand (jacks)
  • Unblocks the entire folding range (flush draws, sevens, 5-9 pairs)
  • Folds out high-equity draws (KQdd, K9dd, 87hh)
  • 6 outs if called (any King or Jack improves to near-nuts)
  • No interaction with flush draws = good for bluffing
