1. Foundations & Key Concepts

Definition
GTO (Game Theory Optimal): A strategy that cannot be exploited. When you play GTO and your opponent deviates (too loose, too tight, too passive, too aggressive), money flows in your direction. Learning GTO preflop is mandatory.
Definition
RFI (Raise First In): When the action folds to you and you are the first player to put money in the pot (beyond the blinds). No limpers before you.
Definition
Rake: The percentage of the pot taken by the house. A 5% rake capped at 1.5bb is standard online. Live games may have no per-pot rake (timed or entry fee). Rake fundamentally changes preflop strategy -- with rake, you must play tighter and never limp.

Two Formats, Two Systems

Feature8-Max Live6-Max Online
Players8-handed6-handed
Stack Depths100bb and 200bb100bb
RakeNo per-pot rake5% capped at 1.5bb
SB StrategyRaise or limp (no rake)Raise or fold (rake)
Middle Position CallingAllowed3-bet or fold (no flatting)
Insight
Stack depth changes everything. At 100bb, high cards and pairs dominate. At 200bb, suited connectors and suited aces gain significant value because post-flop playability matters more when stacks are deep. You'll 3-bet with more mixed strategies at 200bb and use more polarized strategies at 100bb.

2. Raise First In (RFI)

The No-Limp Rule

From every position except SB in no-rake games: if you're first in, you raise or fold. There are zero limps in GTO play. With rake, even the SB is raise-or-fold.

Example: UTG RFI — 98s vs 65s
Situation: 8-max live cash game, 100bb effective stacks, no rake. You are UTG (first to act). Action folds to you. You look down at 9-8 suited.

Correct play: Fold.

Why 98s is unplayable from UTG: The UTG RFI range is about 11% of hands. You have 7 players left to act who can wake up with premiums. More importantly, 98s has a reverse-implied-odds problem at depth: on a board like Q-J-T (a perfectly reasonable board for a UTG raiser to have hit), your opponent can hold AK for the nut straight while you hold the second-best straight — and you lose your stack. This "top end vs. bottom end" straight problem makes 98s dangerous in any contested pot.

Contrast with 65s: 65s is playable from UTG in many scenarios. When 65s makes the low straight (A-2-3-4-5, the wheel), it actually has the nut-nut scenario against A5s, which makes the identical wheel but is a flush draw counterpart. When 65s makes a straight on a 4-3-2 board, even AK (which has a gutshot) is drawing nearly dead. The hands that beat you (67 for a higher straight) are not in opponents' early-position ranges.

EV cost of the mistake: Raising 98s from UTG in an 8-max game costs roughly 0.05–0.10bb per hand in EV from a combination of: bloating pots OOP against ranges that dominate you, paying rake on limped/contested pots, and losing stack in reverse-implied-odds spots. Over a long session this compounds significantly.

Ranges widen dramatically as position improves:

Trap
Small pairs from EP are weak. Pocket 2s through 5s are not playable from UTG in 8-max. They get 3-bet too often and play poorly OOP. Also, 9-8s and 8-7s are consistently bad from early and middle positions -- do not treat all suited connectors equally.
Insight
200bb deep vs 100bb deep (8-max): The biggest RFI divergence is on the button -- 40% at 100bb vs 44% at 200bb. The extra hands are almost entirely suited: Q2s, J4s, T5s, 85s, 64s, 53s. Suited connectivity and flush potential matter more as stacks deepen.

SB Special: The Limp-or-Raise Decision (No Rake)

In 8-max with no rake, the SB has a unique limp/raise strategy:

  • Raise for value: AKs-ATs, KQs, and similar premiums
  • Raise as bluffs: Junky suited hands (Q2s, J4s, 85s, 53s) + offsuit blockers (A9o, KJo, QTo)
  • Limp: Most pairs (including AA!), medium suited hands, medium offsuit hands
Trap
Never raise all good hands and limp all bad hands from the SB. If you do, your limp range becomes transparently weak and your opponent crushes you by raising your limps aggressively. You MUST mix good hands into your limp range (including aces and kings sometimes) to protect it.

3. Facing a Raise

3-Bet or Call: The Position Factor

Definition
Polarized 3-betting: 3-betting with the best hands (for value) and the worst playable hands (as bluffs), while calling with middle-strength hands. Used primarily when you have position.
Definition
Linear 3-betting: 3-betting with a continuous range of strong hands. Used primarily when out of position (especially from the SB/BB).

In Position (IP)

  • 3-bet polarized: value hands + blocker bluffs
  • Value 3-bets: AA-QQ, AK (not always at 200bb)
  • Bluff 3-bets: Low Ax suited (A5s, A4s), Kx suited on the cusp (K9s-KJs), 65s
  • Calls: QQ-JJ (often!), AQs, KQs, suited connectors, medium pairs
Example: BTN vs UTG Raise — JJ Decision
Situation: 8-max live cash, 100bb effective. UTG opens to 3bb, folds to you on the BTN. You hold J♠ J♣. The SB and BB are unknowns.

Correct play: Call the 3bb open. Do NOT 3-bet.

Why calling is correct: JJ is a strong hand but faces a core problem against a 3-bet: UTG's range is about 11% (AA-TT, AKs-ATs, KQs, etc.), which contains many hands that either crush JJ (AA, KK, QQ) or have significant equity (AK). If you 3-bet to 10bb and get 4-bet to 28bb, you now face a terrible spot — calling 28bb with JJ against a range of QQ+ and AK gives you roughly 43% equity while putting in 28% of your 100bb stack preflop. Instead, by calling the 3bb open, you put in only 3% of your stack with a hand that has ~50%+ equity against UTG's full range.

Post-flop advantage: In position on the BTN, JJ plays extremely well. On a 9-high or T-high board, you have an overpair and can value-bet three streets in position against UTG's c-bet range. On an A or K high board, you can fold cheaply to continued aggression.

The same logic applies to QQ (mostly call at 100bb) and even KK at 200bb deep — deeper stacks mean getting 4-bet with KK and facing a 5-bet all-in is catastrophic. At 200bb, KK wants to see a flop rather than bloat a pot against a range containing AA.

EV cost of auto-3-betting JJ: Against a UTG range that 4-bets ~10% of the time, 3-betting JJ loses approximately 3–5bb per occurrence compared to calling. Over a session with 5–6 such spots, this is a meaningful leak.

Out of Position (OOP)

  • SB vs raise: 3-bet or fold only (no calling). Size: 10-12bb. Super tight.
  • BB vs raise: Can call. 3-bet to 10bb. Calls: pairs, suited aces, suited connectors/gappers
  • 3-bet bluffs OOP: more linear -- good suited broadways + Ax suited + 65s

6-Max: No Flatting from Middle Positions

A critical 6-max rule: from the HJ and CO facing a raise, you have no calling range. The rake makes flatting unprofitable (you pay rake + let people behind you call in position). You 3-bet or fold.

Insight
The "Magical Triangle" of 3-bet bluffs (BB, 6-max): Against late position raisers, the hands in the low-middle region of the hand matrix -- suited connectors like 76s, 87s, 65s, 54s -- are the primary 3-bet bluffs from the BB. These hands flop well and aren't dominated. JL calls this the "magical triangle" on the hand chart.

3-Bet Sizing

Position3-Bet Size (over 2.5bb open)

Out of Position (6-max)10bb
In Position (8-max)10bb

Trap
Do not always 3x the open. Many players make it 3x regardless of position. From OOP in cash games, you should 3-bet larger to deny the in-position caller good odds. Making it only 7.5bb OOP over a 2.5bb open gives the caller too good a price to see a flop in position against you.

4. Facing a 3-Bet

The Brutal Truth: Fold a Lot

When you raise and get 3-bet, the most common correct action is fold. The 3-bettor should have a strong range, especially from early positions.

RULE: When you raise and get 3-bet, fold every offsuit hand except AK in most scenarios.

What Calls a 3-Bet

  • Always: Pairs (for set mining), low suited connectors (65s, 76s, 54s -- they're live)
  • Usually: AKs, AQs (often just calls, doesn't 4-bet), QQ-JJ at 200bb
  • Never: Low Ax suited, Kx suited (even KQs folds vs EP 3-bets), offsuit hands, 98s
Example: UTG vs HJ 3-Bet — KQs Is a Fold
Situation: 8-max live cash, 100bb effective. You open to 3bb from UTG, the HJ 3-bets to 10bb. Action folds back to you. You hold K♥ Q♥.

Correct play: Fold.

Why KQs folds: The HJ's 3-betting range against a UTG open is extremely narrow — roughly: AA, KK, QQ, AK (value), plus a few Ax suited blockers (A5s, A4s) and possibly 65s as bluffs. Against this range, KQs is in terrible shape:
• Against AA: ~30% equity
• Against KK: ~27% equity (dominated)
• Against AK: ~27% equity (crushed on K-high boards)
• Against QQ: ~27% equity (dominated)
Average equity vs the full 3-bet range: roughly 37–40%, but you are OOP for the entire hand and must put in 7 more big blinds to continue.

The domination problem: Even when KQs "hits," it often doesn't win. Flop K-7-2: great for KQs, right? But AK (in HJ's range) has you crushed. Flop Q-8-3: again, AK has you dominated if opponent continuation bets. The hand has a high frequency of "second-best hand" scenarios.

The same applies to AQo, KJs, QJs: These are all hands that look strong but are dominated by or heavily behind the 3-bettor's value range when raised from EP. The correct response is folding all of them.

EV cost of calling: Calling KQs vs a tight 3-bet range costs approximately 2–4bb per occurrence. At 100bb, calling 7bb more to win 13bb with ~37% equity and OOP disadvantage is a losing proposition in expected value.

What 4-Bets

  • 100bb, OOP: Jam AK + KK. Small 4-bet with AA, QQ + blocker bluffs (AJs, KQs for playability)
  • 100bb, IP: Almost never 4-bet. Call instead.
  • 200bb, OOP: Small 5-bet with only AA + Ax suited blockers
  • 200bb, IP: Almost never 4-bet. Call with everything decent.
Insight
100bb vs 200bb 4-bet bluff selection: At 100bb, your 4-bet bluffs are Ax offsuit and Kx suited hands (blocker value matters most). At 200bb, your bluffs shift to Ax suited and suited connectors (playability matters because you'll get called more often and see flops).
Trap
AK offsuit at 200bb deep. Against a tight 3-bet range, AK offsuit should often fold or just call -- not 4-bet. At 200bb, if you 4-bet and face a 5-bet, you're in a disaster. AK offsuit makes top pair but has no flush outs. This is one of the most commonly overplayed hands at deep stacks.

5. Facing 4-Bets and Beyond

vs Non-All-In 4-Bet

You 3-bet, they 4-bet to ~20-24bb. You're getting decent odds (~14 to win 49.5) but against a strong range.

Pattern: What Folds, What Calls

ActionHands
FoldLow Ax suited, ALL Kx suited (even KQ at 200bb), all offsuit (except AK), 98s, QJs, JTs
CallPairs, low suited connectors (65s, 76s, 54s), AKs, QQ (usually), A5s
5-bet/Jam100bb: AK + KK. 200bb: only AA + Ax suited blockers
Insight
Why do low suited connectors call 4-bets but high suited hands fold? High suited hands (KJs, QTs, KQs) are heavily dominated by the 4-bettor's value range. Low suited connectors (65s, 76s) are "live" -- they don't interact with the 4-bettor's range at all. If you flop a straight or flush with 65s, it's almost always good. If you flop top pair with KJs against a 4-bet range, you're often crushed.
Example: vs Non-All-In 4-Bet — AKo at 200bb
Situation: 8-max live cash, 200bb effective. You 3-bet from HJ to 10bb over a UTG 3bb open. UTG 4-bets to 24bb (non-all-in). You hold A♠ K♣.

Correct play at 200bb: Fold AKo. Call AKs.

Why AKo folds at 200bb but calls at 100bb:
At 100bb: The pot is 24bb if you fold, you've invested 10bb. Calling 14bb more to win 49.5bb (the 4-bettor's 24bb + your 10bb + UTG's 3bb + antes) gives you roughly 3.5:1 odds. With ~43% equity against a 4-bet range of AA/KK/QQ/AK, this is a borderline but acceptable call. Additionally, at 100bb, you can jam over the 4-bet with AK+KK and get called by worse (QQ).

At 200bb: The SPR (stack-to-pot ratio) after calling is enormous. If you call 14bb into a 49.5bb pot, the remaining stacks are ~176bb vs ~176bb with a pot of 49.5bb — SPR of roughly 3.5. Now every postflop decision is high leverage. AKo's problem: it makes TPTK but has no flush outs. On a board like K-7-3, you bet and opponent raises — they almost certainly have you beat (sets or KK). On an A-4-2, they have you beat with AK (ties) or better. The hand frequently makes the second-best hand at deep stacks.

If you 4-bet with AKo and face a 5-bet: Now you've invested 52bb into a pot and face a shove of 200bb. Against a 5-bet range of AA/KK (and very few bluffs at 200bb), you're approximately 30% equity. Calling is a disaster. Folding after 4-betting is exploitable. The correct solution is to not 4-bet AKo in the first place at 200bb.

AKs is different: At 200bb, AKs calls the 4-bet because nut flush equity is a game-changer. On a monotone board where both players are committed, AKs can win the entire stack. It also blocks AA (one fewer combo) and sets up premium bluffing situations.

vs All-In 4-Bet / 5-Bet / 6-Bet

RULE: vs 4-bet all-in -- tight ranges: QQ+ and AKs. Wide ranges: TT+ and AK.
RULE: vs 5-bet all-in -- "Tens or better and Ace-King calls."
RULE: vs 6-bet all-in (200bb) -- AA, KK, AKs only.
Trap
Don't auto-call with AKo vs 5-bet all-in from EP. Against early position 5-bet jams (where the range is AA/KK/AKs with tiny bluffs), AK offsuit should fold. AKs calls because it blocks their range better AND has flush potential. This is a subtle but important distinction that costs many players money.

6. Squeezing & Multiway

Raise + Call(s) Before You

Definition
Squeeze: A 3-bet made when there is a raise and one or more callers before you. The goal is to isolate or win the pot immediately by pressuring the original caller(s) who have capped ranges.

Key Principles

  • SB vs raise + call: 3-bet or fold. No cold calling (even getting better odds).
  • BB vs raise + call: Can call. Defend with suited aces, pairs, suited connectors/gappers.
  • GTO squeeze ranges are tighter than you think -- solver assumes perfect post-flop play
  • Exploitative adjustment: Play more suited aces, pairs, and low suited connectors than GTO recommends (opponents don't play well enough to justify the solver's extreme tightness)
Example: BB vs CO Raise + BTN Call — The Suited/Offsuit Divide
Situation: 8-max live cash, 100bb. CO opens to 3bb, BTN calls 3bb, SB folds. You are in the BB with A♦ 6♣ (offsuit). The pot is 7.5bb and you need to call 2bb more to close the action.

Correct play: Fold A6o. Call if suited (A6s). Better yet, call 76s or 55.

Why A6o folds despite getting 3.75:1 pot odds: In multiway pots, you need to make the best hand — not just a good hand. A6o's main source of value is hitting an ace for top pair, but in a 3-way pot:
• The CO (who opened) has many strong aces (AQ, AJ, AT, AK) that beat your kicker
• The BTN (who called) also has many middle aces
• When you flop A-high and bet or call down, you often find yourself losing to a better kicker
This is called "kicker domination" — you have equity but negative expected value because you lose the most in the biggest pots.

Why A6 suited is much better: A6s adds flush equity that is virtually never dominated. If you make a flush, it's almost always the best flush (since you hold the Ace). Flush draws also give you good fold equity when you semi-bluff. The suited version is worth approximately 3–4bb more in expected value per 100bb in multiway pots.

Why 76s and 55 are even better: 76s makes nut straights (not the bottom end), has flush potential, and its equity doesn't interact with opponents' ranges the way A6o does. 55 can flop a set (~12% chance) which is a disguised monster — opponents won't put you on a set on low boards, and you can stack someone with an overpair or two pair.

The universal multiway rule: Offsuit aces, offsuit broadways — fold. Suited aces, low suited connectors, pairs — play. This distinction accounts for the majority of preflop EV leaks in multiway cash game pots.
THE MULTIWAY RULE: Offsuit hands are bad. Suited aces and low suited connectors are good. Pairs are great. This applies to every multiway preflop scenario.

7. Cold 4-Betting

When there's a raise and a 3-bet before you and you have no money invested: 4-bet or fold. Never cold call the 3-bet.

Trap
Cold calling a 3-bet reveals your hand. When you cold call a 3-bet (with no money in the pot), you almost always have a "good but not premium" hand -- something like JJ, AQs, KQs. This narrows your range enormously and makes you easy to play against post-flop. Either 4-bet or fold.

Implementable Cold 4-Bet Ranges

ScenarioCold 4-Bet Range
Tightest (EP raise + EP 3-bet)QQ+, AK, A5s
Medium (EP raise + LP 3-bet)QQ+, AQs+, AK, A5s, KQs
Widest (LP raise + LP 3-bet)TT+, ATs+, AK, AQo, KQs, A5s

8. Blind vs Blind (No Rake)

In 8-max no-rake games, do not chop. Play every blind-vs-blind hand if you're the better player.

Escalation Pattern

As the pot escalates through re-raises in blind-vs-blind, ranges converge rapidly:

StageApprox. Range
SB RFI / BB raiseWide (50-70% of hands)
3-betJJ+, AQ+, KQ + blocker bluffs
4-betTT+, AK + Ax/Kx suited blockers
5-betAA, KK, AK + rare Ax suited bluffs
5-bet all-in / 6-betJJ+ and AK
Insight
"Jacks or better and Ace-King" -- this is the ultimate blind-vs-blind all-in call-off rule. When the money is going in after multiple re-raises, if you have JJ+ or AK, you call. Everything else folds. Memorize this phrase.

9. Universal Rules & Heuristics

The Five Power Rules

1. OFFSUIT HANDS ARE BAD. Beyond RFI, fold all offsuit hands except AKo (and sometimes AQo) in nearly every 3-bet+ scenario.
2. 65s IS THE POWER HAND. It gets in as a bluff in 3-bet, 4-bet, and squeeze spots. It makes the nut straight cleanly (unlike 98s). It smashes A5s on wheel boards. Always 3-bet it.
3. 98s IS A TRAP. Despite looking like a premium suited connector, 98s makes the bad end of straights (Q-J-T = opponent has AK). Fold it in most preflop scenarios. Prefer 65s, 76s, 54s.
4. AK OFFSUIT AT 200bb IS NOT A PREMIUM. It often just calls (not 3-bets), calls (not 4-bets), and sometimes folds to 4-bets. It makes TPTK but has no flush equity. Be cautious.
5. IN POSITION = CALL MORE, 4-BET LESS. Out of position = 4-bet more, call less. IP players want to see flops. OOP players want to either win the pot now or build it with nutted hands.

Exploitative Adjustments

Example: Exploiting Passive Players
Scenario A — Opponent rarely 3-bets:
You are on the BTN in a live 8-max game. The typical player in this pool open-raises from UTG and folds to 3-bets more than 80% of the time. They also rarely re-raise pre-flop from the blinds. GTO BTN RFI is 40–44%. Against this player pool, you can expand to 50%+ by adding: all suited connectors down to 54s, all suited aces, all small pairs, and K7o+. The reason: hands that rely on fold equity (your raise stealing the blinds) become more profitable because opponents yield too much preflop. The EV of each additional BTN raise goes up by ~0.1–0.3bb when opponents concede blinds at a higher rate.

Scenario B — Opponent 3-bets but never 4-bets:
Your CO opens 3bb, BB 3-bets to 9bb. You call. Opponent C-bets flop. You call. Opponent checks turn. Now what? Against opponents who never 4-bet and rarely continuation bet multiple streets, your 3-bet bluffs with A5s, 65s become pure profit. You 3-bet to 9bb, they fold ~60% of the time (pure profit), and when they call and fire one street, you still have implied odds and fold equity on later streets. Add hands like K9s, KTs, A4s–A2s to your 3-bet bluffing range when you know they won't 4-bet.

Scenario C — Loose/passive post-flop opponents:
You call a CO raise from the BTN with 65s (100bb). Flop comes 8-7-2 rainbow. You have an open-ended straight draw. A passive opponent will check-call with top pair and bet-fold draws. Against this player, you can bluff all your draws aggressively because they over-fold to second barrels. Hands like 65s, 76s, A4s gain the most value in these pools — call more of them preflop than GTO suggests.
