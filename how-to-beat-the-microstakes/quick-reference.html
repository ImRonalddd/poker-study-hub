1. Preflop Ranges by Position

Positions: UTG (Under the Gun), MP (Middle Position), HJ (Hijack), CO (Cutoff), BTN (Button), SB (Small Blind), BB (Big Blind).

Position Open-Raise Hands Sizing Notes
UTG AA–TT, AKs, AQs, AKo. Fold everything else including A9o, QJo. 2–2.5x Never open A9o, A6o, QJo UTG. No exceptions.
UTG+1 / MP AA–99, AKs–AJs, AKo–AQo, KQs 2–2.5x Tighten further vs early-position opens.
HJ AA–77, AKs–ATs, KQs–KJs, AKo–AJo 2–2.5x Check 3-bet % of players to left before opening speculative hands.
CO AA–66, Broadways, AXs, suited connectors (98s–54s) 2–2.5x Villain opening CO = you can 3-bet BTN with pairs down to 66.
BTN Wider: 55+, most broadways, suited connectors, suited one-gappers 2–2.5x; size up vs loose BB Check BB 3-bet %. If 0%, open liberally. If short stacks behind, tighten slightly.
SB Raise or fold. Limping marginal. Raise: pairs, broadways, suited connectors. 2.5–3x Limping SB at micros with a big hand is a common trap signal.
BB (vs raise) Defend wide vs loose openers. 3-bet strong hands or bluffs with blockers. 3–4x for 3-bet Calling BB is hardest to profit from. Requires check-raising & leading game.
Trap — Cold-Calling in Early Position

Cold-calling from HJ or earlier with suited connectors and speculative hands is a significant leak. Filter your database by position for cold calls: most players show losses of −40 to −70 bb/100. Only BTN and sometimes CO cold calls are defensible. Fold everything else to a raise + caller.

2. 3-Bet Guidelines

Situation Action Reasoning
Loose villain (VP >45%) opens, you are in position 3-bet wide for value: pairs, AJ+, KQ They call too much preflop; build pot with superior hands in position.
Tight villain (<15% VP) opens early Fold AJo, KQo, even AQo sometimes Dominated more often. Not worth building a pot OOP vs a strong range.
Short stack (<20bb) behind you Raise smaller (2x) or just fold marginal hands Short stacks jam or fold; your 3-bet pricing may force awkward spots.
Multiple short stacks behind Use 2x; encourages them to jam and gives you calling odds At micros, short stacks jam with much wider ranges than at higher stakes.
Villain opens, limper(s) already in Often fold marginal hands (KTo, QJo); risk of squeeze & limper reshove Multi-way pot equity collapses for one-pair hands.
Bluff 3-bet candidate A3o–A5o vs aggressive steal-heavy opponent; ace blocks their 4-bet range Best vs high-3bet opponents who are stealing from late position frequently.
Key Insight — Check 3-Bet % Before Every Raise

Before opening a speculative hand, scan every player to your left for their 3-bet percentage. A 0% 3-bet player is a green light. Any player at 8%+ or with a short re-jam stack (<20bb) is a stop sign. Getting to the big blind — who plays the most hands and the worst range — is the goal.

3. Common Microstakes Opponent Types

Type Stats (VP / PFR / 3B) How They Play Exploit Strategy
The Calling Station 40–60% / 8–14% / 0–3% Calls raises with garbage, check-calls flop & turn, rarely folds a pair or a draw. Value bet large every street. Never bluff. If they raise, they have it. Size up with TPTK on safe boards.
The Nit 10–18% / 8–14% / 0–4% Plays only strong hands. Folds to most pressure. When they fire twice, they have it. Fold to double-barrels. Steal their blinds frequently. Do not bluff-catch — trust their showdowns.
The Maniac 45–70% / 25–40% / 15%+ Opens huge range, fires multiple streets, raises postflop liberally with weak hands. 3-bet for value with wider range. Call down with TPTK or better. Do NOT 3-bet bluff them — they call everything.
The Loose-Passive (Fish) 30–50% / 5–10% / 0–2% Limps frequently, calls raises OOP, checks most flops, won't bet without the nuts. Build pot preflop when in position. Bet every street for thin value. If they check-raise or lead river, fold unless you have a strong hand.
The Passive Reg 18–28% / 12–18% / 4–8% Knows hand selection, bets value hands but rarely bluffs. Position-aware but not exploitative. Respect their double-barrels. Give them credit vs passive lines. Play straightforwardly; don't over-bluff.
Key Insight — Don't Apply Your Logic to Micro Players

Microstakes players are not thinking about balance, MDF, or optimal defense frequencies. A player raising a small c-bet on A♥-Q♦-9♦ may literally have top pair and want more money — not a sophisticated semi-bluff. Read their stats, not their supposed strategy.

Key Insight — The Microstakes C-Bet Edge

At microstakes, a 1/4 pot bet needs to work only 20% of the time. But players here WILL call with any pair, any draw. Use larger bets for value (they still call) and save small bets for semi-bluffs on boards where they fold top pair to pressure. Big value bet + they call = you print money over time.

Trap — C-Bet Sizing That Invites Check-Raises

A tiny bet (1/5 pot) vs micro players on a live board invites "I don't believe you" check-raises. They cannot calculate pot odds — they react emotionally to bet sizes. If your c-bet is so small that calling looks free, expect calls and occasional manic check-raises with middle pair. Adjust sizing to what you want them to do with their weakest holdings.

5. Multi-Way Pot Rules

Scenario Rule
3+ players, you have one pair One pair is usually not good enough to build a big pot. Check to control size unless it is TPTK on a dry board.
Everyone checks the flop, turn blank Beware pot-control traps. Passive players check hands like middle set, flush draws, two pair and wait for you to bet into them.
Villain leads small on the river after checking all streets This is almost always value at microstakes. Rarely a bluff. Fold unless you have two pair or better.
You have a big hand (set, two pair) in multi-way pot Bet now. Do not slow-play. Micro players happily chase draws and pay off immediately.
Pocket pair as a cold call, multi-way Cold-calling with pocket pairs in early/middle position multi-way is a losing play in databases. Fold or 3-bet.

6. Key Formulas & Pot Odds

Pot Odds (% equity needed to call)
\[\text{Equity Needed} = \frac{\text{Call Amount}}{\text{Total Pot After Call}}\]

Example: Pot = 100, Call = 50 → 50 / 150 = 33% equity needed.

Bet Breakeven (fold equity needed)
\[\text{Fold Eq. Needed} = \frac{\text{Bet}}{\text{Pot} + \text{Bet}}\]

1/4 pot bet needs to work 20%. Half pot needs 33%.

Minimum Defense Frequency (MDF)
\[\text{MDF} = 1 - \frac{\text{Bet}}{\text{Pot} + \text{Bet}}\]

If villain bets half pot, they need you folding ≤33% — you must defend 67%.

Key Formula — The Big Call Decision

Before any big call, ask: "Does he do this with X?" where X is the best hand you beat. If the honest answer is "rarely" — fold. If "yes, sometimes, especially at microstakes where they shove wide" — call. Do not chase 100% certainty; 80% is enough.

7. Bankroll Management

$5–$22 MTTs (low stakes) 100 buy-ins 150 buy-ins at next level Below 75 buy-ins
$50–$109 MTTs 80–100 buy-ins Proven win rate over 1,000+ games Below 60 buy-ins
Key Rule — Min Cash First

When 20% of the field is paid, your first objective is to min-cash. 298th and 68th place pay the same. Once in the money, then go for broke. Consistent min-cashes build your bankroll between deep runs.

Key Rule — Table Selection = 90% of the Job

Find the softest sites and tournaments. Crappier software = fewer multi-tablers. Live poker in vacation spots and newly opened markets = recreational players. Soft field + solid play = sustained profit without needing extreme variance.

9. Quick Decision Reference

Almost Always Raise / 3-Bet

  • Premium pairs (AA–JJ) in any position
  • AK / AQs vs aggressive opener
  • Pairs (66+) vs loose late-position opener, you have position
  • AJ+ in position vs loose raiser
  • Suited connectors (98s, 87s) on BTN vs CO open, no 3-bettors behind

Almost Always Fold

  • A9o or worse UTG / MP
  • Suited gappers (3-gap+) facing raise + caller
  • KTo, QJo facing early-position raise
  • Cold-calling with speculative hands HJ or earlier
  • Any hand vs nit's UTG open if you're out of position
  • 89o facing raise + cold call

Fold to Villain's Action When…

  • Nit (<18% VP) double-barrels — they almost always have it
  • Villain leads river after checking all streets — value, not bluff
  • Small c-bet gets raised on a board that hits raising range
  • Short stack limps then re-raises — classic micro-trap

Call / Raise Villain's Action When…

  • Loose villain (45%+ VP) bets after a 3-way checked flop — likely weak
  • You have TPTK or better and villain is a calling station
  • Short stack shoves in micro tournament — widen calling range; they shove anything
  • Villain check-raises a very small c-bet heads-up — often emotional bluff

10. Postflop Tendencies at Microstakes

Definition — Pot-Control Trap

Micro players frequently check strong hands on the flop intending to check-call two streets. When three players check a flop and you consider betting the turn because "nobody has anything" — beware. Someone is pot-controlling a made hand. Do not fire into passive multi-way action without a strong holding.

Micro Player Tendency Exploit
Never folds top pair to any street, any size Value bet every street. No bluffing. Get max chips in before river.
Never leads river after calling two streets Check back river with medium-strength hands; check-call if they do lead (they have it).
Checks all draws hoping to see free cards Bet to deny equity; do not give free cards to flush or straight draws.
Folds all high cards to c-bet, even in position Small c-bet (1/5–1/4 pot) works very often; build fold equity cheaply.
Check-raises only with the nuts (or close to it) When facing a check-raise, especially from a passive player: fold or call only with very strong hands.
Emotionally pot-commits after calling flop Bet large on the turn with strong hands. "He can't fold now" is literally true.
Worked Example — TPTK vs Microstakes Field

Setup: BTN open 2x, BB calls (VP 32%, PFR 9%). Board: J♠-9♦-7♦. BB checks.

Analysis: Bet large (50%+ pot). BB called preflop with 9-5, 7-8, Q-J, T-8 — wide weak range. He is NOT folding a pair of any kind. Make him pay now. If turn is a blank and he checks again, bet large again. If he leads the river, he has two pair or better — fold. Total extracted EV from one hand vs this player profile is 2x higher with aggressive multi-street betting than with check/pot-control.
