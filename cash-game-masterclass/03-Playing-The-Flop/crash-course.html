1. The Four Hand Types

Definition
Every hand on the flop falls into one of four categories that determine your default action: Premium Made Hand, Draw, Marginal Made Hand, or Junk.
TypeWhat It IsDefault Action
Premium MadeStrong top pair+ — happy getting lots of money inBet. Do not fold to a raise.
DrawFlush draw, straight draw, backdoor combosBet. Fold weak draws if raised; continue strong draws.
Marginal MadeWeak top pair, middle pair, ace-high on dry boardsCheck-call. Keep the pot small.
JunkNo pair, no meaningful drawCheck-fold (or bluff in high-advantage spots).
Key Insight
Hand types are relative. Pocket tens is a premium hand on T-7-2 but a marginal hand on K-J-4. Ace-high is marginal on Q-7-2 but junk on Q-9-7 with flush draws. Always recategorize as the board and situation change.

Betting Range Ratios

When you bet on the flop, your range must satisfy these constraints:

  • Betting range: At most 1 premium made hand to 2 draws. Fewer draws is fine.
  • Checking range: At least 70% marginal made hands, at most 30% junk. More marginals is always OK.
Board Example — J♠ 8♥ 5♦

LoJack raises, BB calls. Categorize our range:

  • Premium: JJ+, J9s+, 88, 55 (good jacks and better) — 51 combos (26%)
  • Draws: QTs (gutshot), 76s (OESD), T9s, backdoor flush draws with overcards — 36 combos
  • Marginal: TT-99, AQ (overcards with showdown value) — ~45 combos
  • Junk: K9♣ (no backdoor draw), low unpaired no-draw hands — ~11 combos

Ratio check: 36/51 = 1.4:1 draws to premiums. Under the 2:1 max. We bet ~60% and check ~40% — matches GTO.

2. C-Bet Frequency: Range Advantage

Definition — Range Advantage
The advantage one player's likely range has over their opponent's range on any individual board, measured by overall equity. The preflop raiser usually has the advantage because they start with better hands.
EquityClassificationWhat to Bet (IP)
60%+StrongEverything — premiums, draws, marginals, junk
54-60%ModeratePremiums + draws + some bluffs (40-75% of range)
50-54%WeakPremiums + draws only
<50%NoneAlmost nothing (check nearly everything)
Key Insight
Range advantage is like a superpower. The bigger it is, the more hands you can profitably include in your betting range. A strong advantage lets you bet junk as a bluff because the "weight" of your premiums makes it profitable.

3. C-Bet Sizing: Nut Advantage & Connectivity

Definition — Nut Advantage
A player has the nut advantage when they have more combinations of effective nut hands (two pair and better) than their opponent. You can have range advantage without nut advantage.
Definition — Range Connectivity
How well the opponent's continuing range connects with the board. High connectivity = they have lots of clear pairs and strong hands. Low connectivity = they must continue with king-high, queen-high, backdoor draws.
Sizing Decision Tree Do I have the nut advantage? ├── NO → Bet SMALL (25-33% pot). Always. └── YES → How well does opponent connect? ├── WELL (lots of pairs) → Bet BIG (50-75% pot) └── POORLY (mostly overcards) → Bet SMALL (25-33% pot)
Sizing Comparison

A♠ K♥ J♦ (UTG vs BB): UTG has nut advantage + opponent connects well (any A/K/J isn't folding). Bet BIG.

J♠ 6♥ 6♣ (UTG vs BB): UTG has range advantage but NO nut advantage (BB has the sixes). Bet SMALL.

J♠ 6♣ 2♦ (UTG vs BB): UTG has range + nut advantage, but opponent connects poorly (must call with backdoor draws, overcards). Bet SMALL.

4. Position Effects on C-Betting

In Position (IP)

  • Bet more often across the board
  • Naturally have bigger range advantage (BB's range is wider/weaker)
  • After checking, only face 2 more streets — less pressure on checking range
  • 60%+ equity: bet 75-100%. Moderate: 40-75%. Weak: premiums + draws. None: almost never.

Out of Position (OOP)

  • Bet less often — must protect checking range with strong hands
  • Range advantage is smaller (opponent in position has stronger range)
  • After checking, face up to 3 streets of aggression
  • On low-connected boards: check everything — even when you have decent hands
  • Rare high-frequency OOP boards: King-high (K-J-2, K-9-5)
Common Mistake
C-betting 100% from out of position on every flop. On boards like 8-4-3, 7-5-4, or T-8-6, the GTO strategy OOP is to check your entire range. Betting here makes you exploitable through check-raises.

Board: K♠ K♥ 6♦ (Paired King-high)

Massive nut + range advantage for PFR. Opponent has lots of queen-high junk. Range connectivity is terrible.

Strategy: Bet 100% of range, very small (25% pot). GTO checks only 4% of the time (just a few ace-highs).

Board: T♦ 8♣ 6♥ (Middle-connected)

This board connects well with BB's range (suited connectors, middle pairs). LoJack has ~53% equity IP.

Strategy (IP vs BB): Bet very polarized — good top pairs, sets, some overpairs + junky draws. Check ~70%.

Strategy (OOP vs BTN): Check everything. 0% c-bet frequency.

Board: Q♠ T♠ 5♠ (Monotone)

PFR has best flushes and best flush draws (nut suited hands). BB has fewer high-card flush draw combos.

Key: Bet pairs with a spade more than pairs without. Pocket aces with A♠ always bets; AA without a spade may check at 100bb depth. Use small sizing.

6. Facing a Bet: The Response Flowchart

Definition — Minimum Defense Frequency (MDF)
\[\text{MDF} = 1 - \frac{\text{bet}}{\text{bet} + \text{pot}}\] The minimum percentage of your range you must defend to prevent opponent from profiting with any bluff. From OOP, you should overfold compared to MDF because you under-realize equity.
Bet Response Flowchart What hand type do I have? ├── Junk → FOLD ├── Marginal Made Hand → CALL └── Premium / Draw → Do I have the nut advantage? ├── NO → Mostly CALL │ IP: call almost everything, very few raises │ OOP: still raise best hands + some weak draws └── YES → RAISE more frequently IP: raise to ~2.5x the bet OOP: raise to ~3x the bet (more vs small bets)
Key Insight
IP = lots of calling. OOP = more raising. When you're in position, you can float flop bets and bet when they check the turn. When OOP, you can't — so you must raise weak draws now or fold them. This is why OOP always has a check-raising range on almost every board.

Raise Sizing

  • IP: ~2.5x the bet (unless bet is tiny, then raise more)
  • OOP: ~3x the bet. If they bet 25% pot, raise to ~5x their bet
  • High range connectivity board (K-J-4): raise large (70%+ pot)
  • Low connectivity board (7-5-4): raise smaller (cheaper bluffs)

7. Facing a Check-Raise

When you c-bet and get check-raised, most of your range becomes a bluff catcher.

Key Insight
Most small/medium stakes players do not check-raise aggressively enough. They are overwhelmingly value-heavy. Against these opponents: bet the flop super wide, and if they check-raise, just fold your non-premium hands. It's a huge exploit.

Against Polarized Check-Raisers (GTO-type)

  • Call with best made hands + draws getting correct odds + marginal made hands
  • Slightly overfold compared to MDF
  • Very little 3-betting (you're IP, closing the action)

Against Linear Check-Raisers (thin value)

  • 3-bet with premium hands needing protection (overpairs, bottom two pair) + high-equity draws
  • Call with sets (don't need protection) + medium draws
  • As SPR decreases, increase 3-bet frequency

8. 3-Bet Pots

SPR drops to ~4-5

From ~15 in single-raised pots. Use smaller bet sizes (25-33% pot).

Both ranges are stronger

More clearly defined. PFR still usually has the range advantage (has AA, KK, QQ, AK).

As the 3-Bet Aggressor

  • Same range advantage flowcharts apply with minor adjustments
  • Bet small (30% pot) and frequently on Ace-high and King-high boards
  • More checking on lower boards (T-5-2 type): ~50% check frequency
  • OOP: more checking, more polarized, protect checking range (check some AA/KK)
  • Max draws in betting range: ~60% (down from 67% in SRP)

As the 3-Bet Caller

  • Checked to (IP): Bet very polarized, small. Opponent's check range has traps — don't blast into it.
  • Facing a bet (IP): Mostly call. Raise less than 7% of the time.
  • Facing a bet (OOP): More check-raising with vulnerable good hands.
  • Exploitative: many opponents c-bet 100% in 3-bet pots — defend wider and punish them.

9. 4-Bet Pots

SPR ≈ 1-4

Very shallow. Consider all-in when SPR ≤ 1.

Your Range: AA, KK, AKs + bluffs

They have QQ, JJ, AQ, weaker AXs.

As the 4-Bet Aggressor

  • A-x-x / K-x-x boards: Bet 100% small (25% pot). You have 65-70% equity.
  • Middle-connected (J-T-8, T-7-6): Check more. Bet polarized when you do, sometimes all-in.
  • Slow-play super nuts (top set) — stacks already shallow, hard for opponent to have anything.
  • Without range advantage: check a lot, protect checking range with overpairs.

As the 4-Bet Caller

  • Some counter-intuitive folds are correct (e.g., AJ on KJ3 — opponent's range crushes you)
  • OOP: shove vulnerable but likely-best hands (overpairs, nut flush draws) — pot is huge
  • IP: mostly call, very little raising
  • When checked to: bet small and polarized (opponent's check has traps)
4-Bet Pot: K♥ J♦ 3♣

LoJack 4-bets, CO calls. CO's calling range: QQ, JJ, AQ, AT, A5s, etc.

On KJ3, opponent's entire range is crushed. Queens folds (drawing to 2 outs against KQ/KJ/KT/AK). Even AJ folds — loses to everything in the betting range and blocks the bluffs.

10. Multi-Way Pots

Common Mistake
Playing multi-way pots the same as heads-up. In a 5-way pot on T-9-7, someone will have two pair or better. Your aces are no longer premium — they're marginal. Check-call, don't bet.

Key Adjustments

  • Betting frequency drops dramatically — only very polarized
  • Premium hands get downgraded — overpairs become marginal made hands
  • One-pair hands lose huge value — fold middle/bottom pair to any action
  • MDF splits across all defenders — fold way more when someone bets
  • Draw to the nuts only — non-nut flush draws and bottom-end straights have massive reverse implied odds

Coordinated Boards Multi-Way

Check-call overpairs. Premium = two pair+ or nut draws. If bet + raise happens, fold below two pair.

Uncoordinated Boards Multi-Way

Can bet more (especially 3-way). Top pair top kicker is reasonable. But still check marginal hands and protect checking range.

11. Limped Pots

Key Insight
Rake awareness is critical. If the casino rakes 40%+ of a limped pot, nobody wins except the house. Limping behind is only viable when rake is manageable or there's an ante. When in doubt, raise.
  • Deep SPR — play for implied odds hands (small pairs, suited connectors)
  • No preflop raiser — no one has "announced" strength, no one to check to
  • Bet very polarized when checked to — premium hands + draws only
  • Exploit weakness: If blinds are clearly done with the hand, bet with anything IP
  • Facing a bet: mostly call, rarely raise (no nut advantage typically)
  • Check-raise with sets and strong draws facing small bets
Limped Pot Example

Limp, limp, limp. You limp 3♥ 3♦ on BTN. Flop: Q♠ 5♥ 3♣. Small bet, call.

Raise. You have bottom set on a coordinated enough board with flush draw potential. Facing a tiny bet, raising is mandatory — you need protection and to build the pot. Go to ~$30-45 (small raise) because the tiny bet means you can raise cheap.

12. Common Mistakes

Mistake #1 — Betting Marginal Hands Then Not Folding
You bet K9 on Q-9-7, get raised, and call because "I have a pair." Your marginal hand was supposed to check-call, not bet. If you bet and get raised, you're crushed by everything that continues.
Mistake #2 — C-Betting 100% From OOP
On 8-4-3 out of position vs the button, GTO checks everything. You have <50% equity. Every c-bet is lighting money on fire and making you exploitable through check-raises.
Mistake #3 — Not Protecting Checking Range
If you only check garbage OOP, your opponent will bet the turn every time and print money. Include strong hands (overpairs, sets) in your checking range when you're supposed to check frequently.
Mistake #4 — Raising Marginal Hands Facing a Bet
The flowchart is clear: junk = fold, marginal = call, premium/draw = raise or call based on nut advantage. Many players raise middle pair "to find out where they stand" — this is a massive leak.
Mistake #5 — Drawing to Non-Nut Hands Multi-Way
7-6 on T-9-7 (bottom pair + bad gutshot) looks playable heads-up but is a clear fold multi-way. If you hit the 8 for a straight, someone has the jack. Low flush draws face the same problem.
Mistake #6 — One Bet Size for All Boards
A-K-J demands a big bet (high connectivity). J-6-2 demands a small bet (low connectivity). Using the same size on both boards costs significant EV.
