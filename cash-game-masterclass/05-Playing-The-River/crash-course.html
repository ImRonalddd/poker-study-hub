1. River Foundations: Value vs Bluffs

Definition Value bet: You expect to win at showdown >50% of the time when called.
Bluff: You expect to lose at showdown ~100% of the time when called.

Unlike earlier streets, the river has no draws or semi-bluffs. Your range is purely a mixture of value hands and bluffs. The goal:

  • Value betting: extract maximum from worse hands as often as possible
  • Bluffing: force better hands to fold, while ensuring your value bets get called
Insight Sometimes your value bet gets called by a better hand -- that's okay. Sometimes your bluff gets called -- that's also okay. If your range is balanced, you are unexploitable.

Key Questions Before Every River Bet

  1. How many bet sizes should I use for value?
  2. What should those sizes be?
  3. Which hands go into each size (value and bluff)?
  4. What percentage of bluffs do I need per size?
  5. How did the river card impact my range and equity?

2. Analyzing Your Range

The Two-Bet-Size Framework

On the river, utilize two distinct bet sizes. This strategy lets you:

  • Extract maximum value with premium hands (big bet)
  • Extract some value with marginal winners (small bet)
Bet CategoryTypical SizeComposition
Small bet10%-50% potThin value + few bluffs + some nuts for protection
Large bet67%-500% potPremium hands + proportional bluffs

Hand Spectrum on the River

Classify every hand in your range into one of four categories:

CategoryExamplesDefault Action
Premium valueStraights, sets, strong two-pairBet large
Thin valueTop pair top kicker, weak two-pairBet small
Some showdown valueMiddle pair, Ace-highCheck (or small bluff candidate)
No showdown valueBusted draws, low cardsBluff or give up

Perfect Polarization

Definition Perfect polarization occurs when your betting range contains only hands that always win (nuts) and hands that always lose (bluffs). This puts your opponent in a spot where calling and folding are both break-even.

When perfectly polarized, you win the existing pot on average regardless of what your opponent does.

Bluff Percentage Formula

\[\text{Bluff\%} = \frac{\text{Amount Opponent Calls}}{\text{Pot} + \text{Your Bet} + \text{Opponent's Call}}\]
Bet SizeMax Bluff %Value : Bluff Ratio
33% pot20%4 : 1
50% pot25%3 : 1
75% pot30%2.3 : 1
100% pot33%2 : 1
200% pot40%1.5 : 1
500% pot45%1.2 : 1
Trap These percentages assume perfect polarization. When your value bets don't always win (opponent has traps), reduce your bluff percentage accordingly.

Factors That Increase Opponent's Traps

  • Opponent has a large nut advantage
  • Opponent's range is so weak they check everything (including nuts)
  • You use small bet sizes (random rivered two-pair in their range)
  • Opponent is prone to slow-playing

3. Analyzing Your Exact Hand

Value Betting Decision

  1. Premium hand (65%+ equity when called)? → Bet large if opponent can call large bets
  2. Thin value (51-64% equity when called)? → Bet small
  3. Neither? → Use the Bluffing Decision Flowchart
Insight Always move some premium hands into your small bet range for balance. Otherwise your small bets are all marginal and get demolished by raises.

Bluffing Decision Flowchart

Answer each question. More "yes" answers = more inclined to bluff:

  1. Is my opponent capable of folding? (Calling station? Don't bluff.)
  2. Do I lack showdown value? (10-high = yes, Ace-high = maybe not)
  3. Do I have many value hands in my range? (More value = more bluffs needed)
  4. Are my blockers highly relevant? (Block auto-calls = good)
  5. Do I unblock opponent's auto-folds? (Don't hold cards they'd fold)
  6. Does my bluff tell a credible story? (Would you play your good hands this way?)

Bluff Sizing Flowchart

After deciding to bluff, determine big or small:

Factor"Yes" → Bet Big"No" → Bet Small
Zero showdown value?5-high, complete airBottom pair, Ace-high
Opponent has marginal hands that call small?Many one-pairsMostly busted draws
Block many nut hands?Hold flush/straight blockersNo relevant blockers
Trying to get them off a strong hand?Fold out top pair+Fold out busted draws
Representing a nutted hand?Polarized lineThin value line

Blocker Logic for Bluffing

Example Board: 10-9-4 (two diamonds) -- 9 -- 2. You hold Q♦J♦ (busted flush draw).
Bad bluff: Q-J blocks opponent's K-Q, K-J, A-Q, A-J -- hands they'd auto-fold.
Blocking folds means opponent is more likely to have a pair they won't fold.
Example Same board, you hold 7♦6♦ (busted flush draw).
Better bluff: 7-6 does NOT block any of the high-card hands opponent would fold.
Their range contains more auto-folds → your bluff succeeds more often.

The River is Under-Bluffed

Two critical truths about small/medium stakes:

  1. Most players are afraid to bluff the river (especially overbets)
  2. Most players are afraid to overbet when bluffing
Trap Players who "call with everything" on the flop often overfold the river after being barreled on flop + turn + river. Don't confuse flop stations with river stations.

4. Positional Adjustments

In Position (IP): Betting vs Checking

Betting in position reopens the action -- opponent can check-raise you.

When SPR ≤ 1 (Shallow)

  • Use one bet size only: all-in
  • Do NOT go for thin value with small bets
  • Top pair should check behind (disaster if raised)

When SPR > 1 (Deeper)

  • Two bet sizes: ~50% pot (small) + all-in/overbet (large)
  • IP "small" bet is actually medium (~50% pot, not 10-33% like OOP)

Out of Position (OOP): Four Major Adjustments

Definition OOP thin value betting: From OOP, a bet just needs to be more profitable than checking. This leads to betting very small (10-33% pot) with hands as weak as bottom pair or third pair.
  1. Bet small with very thin value (52-55% equity: third pair, bottom pair for 10-33% pot)
  2. On great rivers (big range advantage): single small size, bet frequently
  3. Check some nuts to protect your checking range (for check-raise value)
  4. Check some weak pairs for check-raise bluffs (block opponent's calls)

OOP Check-Raise Strategy

Check-Raise TypeIdeal HandsWhy
For valueLiteral nuts, second nutsBuild max pot with unbeatable hand
As a bluffBottom pair, weak fourth pairWins if check-check; blocks sets/two-pair calls
Insight Check-raise bluffing the river is something almost no one does at small/medium stakes. This makes it extremely profitable when executed with proper blockers.

5. Facing a River Bet

Bluff Catching

Definition A bluff catcher beats all of opponent's bluffs and loses to all of opponent's value hands. The decision to call depends entirely on whether opponent has enough bluffs.

Bluff Catching Decision Flowchart

  1. Are you beating any value hands? Yes → Just call (not a bluff catcher)
  2. Does opponent's range have enough obvious bluffs? (Busted draws = yes)
  3. Is opponent capable of finding the right bluffs? (Tight rec = no)
  4. Will opponent turn weak showdown value into a bluff?
  5. Does my hand unblock the most obvious bluffs?
Example Board: A-4-4-3-K♦. You hold Q♦J♦ facing a 110% pot overbet from a tight recreational player.
Fold. Your hand blocks diamond flush draws (the most obvious bluffs). Tight player is almost always showing you an ace or a four.
Example Board: K-8-7-5♦-10. You hold Q♦J♦ facing 110% pot overbet from a semi-pro.
Closer to a call. Opponent has many straight draw bluffs (10-9, J-10, 6-5, 5-4). Semi-pros find these bluffs. Your hand doesn't block most of them.

Blocker-Based Calling

When opponent is polarized (nuts or nothing), raw equity matters less than blockers:

  • Block opponent's value hands → more inclined to call
  • Block opponent's bluffs → less inclined to call
Trap A-2 can be a better call than 10-9 (trips!) when opponent's bluffs are J-10 combinations. 10-9 blocks the bluffs; A-2 does not. When opponent is perfectly polarized, all bluff catchers have the same equity.

6. Raising the River (Value & Bluff)

Value Raising

Raise wider for value when:

  • Facing a smaller bet (opponent's range is more marginal)
  • Opponent is laggy (calls raises wider)
  • You have one of the very best hands in your range

Raise tighter for value when:

  • Facing a large bet (opponent is polarized)
  • Opponent folds all non-nut hands to a raise

Bluff Raising

Insight River bluff raising is one of the most under-utilized plays in poker. Use sizes of 2-5x pot to maximize fold equity. If you raise 300% pot, you get to have ~43% bluffs.

Bluff Raise Factors

  1. Facing a small bet? → More inclined to bluff raise
  2. Opponent capable of folding? → More inclined
  3. Your hand has no showdown value? → More inclined
  4. You have many value raise hands? → More inclined
  5. Your blockers block auto-calls? → More inclined
  6. Your bluff tells a credible story? → More inclined
Facing...Raise SizeReasoning
Small bet (33% pot)Raise huge (4-5x)Get marginal made hands to fold
Large bet (100%+ pot)Raise smaller or don't raiseNuts never fold; bluffs always fold anyway

7. When You Bet and Get Raised

Trap FOLD A LOT. This is the single most straightforward spot in poker. Your opponents at small/medium stakes almost never bluff enough when raising the river.

OOP: You Bet, IP Raises

  • Fold all bluffs and bluff catchers
  • Fold most marginal made hands (unless they block opponent's value)
  • Call only with your very best hands

IP: You Bet, OOP Check-Raises

Your Bet SizeResponse to Raise
Large (polarized)Overfold. Opponent is never bluffing enough.
Small (thin value)Defend closer to MDF. Small bet may have induced bluffs.

Key question: "Did I induce my opponent to bluff me?" If yes, call wider. If no (normal spot), overfold.

8. Betting IP: Solver Patterns

Pattern 1: Bet Flop + Bet Turn Big → Jam or Check River

Example BTN raises, BB calls. A♠-J♥-5♠ flop. Bet 67% flop, overbet turn on 2♥, BB calls.
River (8♦): Jam all-in (~1.5x pot) or check. Almost no small betting range.
Value: A-J, sets, two-pair, A-5. Bluffs: hands with 3 or 4 blockers (block straights).
~33% bluffs in the jamming range.

Pattern 2: Bet Flop + Check Turn → Small/Medium Bets Only

Example LJ raises, BB calls. J♠-6♥-6♣-4♠ (bet flop small, check-check turn).
Ace river: Bet ~50% pot. Value = pocket aces, pocket jacks, aces. No weak ace value bets.
No overbets because you lack super nuts after check-check turn.

Pattern 3: Check Flop + Overbet Turn → Cautious River

Example LJ raises, BTN calls. 10♦-8♣-6♥ (check-check flop, overbet Q♥ turn, call).
Ace river: Sets overbet; bluffs with 9-8, 5-4 (low pairs). Q-10 two-pair does NOT value bet.
Opponent called an overbet = their range is strong. Be very cautious with thin value.
Trap When you overbet the turn and opponent calls, their range is condensed and strong. Do NOT thin-value-bet top pair on the river in these spots -- check it behind.

9. Betting OOP: Solver Patterns

Pattern: Range Advantage → Bet Frequently, Small

Example BB vs BTN. 9♦-7♣-5♠-2♥ (check-check flop, BB overbets turn, BTN calls).
8 river (great for BB): Bet frequently (~67%), mostly 25% pot. 9s bet for thin value.
Straights overbet. Check J-10 sometimes for check-raise trap.
9 river (bad for BB): Bet infrequently. When betting, use 4x pot all-in (super polarized).
Bluffs: K-x, Q-x, J-x that block opponent's 9s. ~41% bluffs when jamming.

Pattern: Paired Board OOP

Example BB vs LJ. K♠-K♥-6♦-2♠ (check, LJ bets flop, check-check turn).
Ace river: Bet infrequently (25%), large (120% pot). Value = kings. Bluffs = ace blockers (A-Q, A-J) + low pairs (9-8, 8-7 blocking draws).
Low rivers: Bet very frequently, small. Range advantage. Almost no bluffs in small bet range.

OOP Key Rules

  • Always protect checking range with some nuts (for check-raise)
  • Give up with highest showdown value busted draws (K-Q checks, Q-J bluffs)
  • Bigger bet sizes OOP = more polarized range
  • Sets sometimes check OOP to protect check-raising range

10. Facing Bets: Solver Patterns

IP Facing a Bet

Facing a small bet (25% pot)

  • Raise with nuts + hands that block opponent's auto-calls
  • Bluff raises: every hand containing J or 10 (blocks J-10 for nuts) on a 9-7-5-2-8 board
  • Call with aces, kings that don't block bluffs
  • Even ace-high can call facing small bets

Facing a large all-in (3x+ pot)

  • Fold ~79% of hands
  • Call with trips that DON'T block opponent's bluffs
  • A-2 can be a better call than 10-9 (trips) because blockers matter more than raw hand strength
Insight Against polarized opponents, all bluff catchers have roughly the same equity. The only thing that differentiates them is blockers. Always ask: "Do I block bluffs (bad) or value (good)?"

OOP Facing a Bet

After calling flop + turn bets (strong range)

  • You typically have a range advantage but lack the nut advantage
  • Facing overbet all-in: low aces (A-3, A-4) are better calls than A-10, A-J
  • High aces block opponent's bluffs (10-9 type straight draws)

When flush completes on the river

  • Call with all flushes
  • One-pair + flush blocker (spade) = call
  • One-pair without flush blocker = fold

Summary: Universal River Principles

Definition Minimum Defense Frequency (MDF): The minimum % of your range you must continue with to prevent opponent from profitably bluffing with any two cards.
\(\text{MDF} = \frac{\text{Pot}}{\text{Pot} + \text{Bet}}\)

The Five Pillars of River Play

  1. The river is under-bluffed -- exploit by over-folding to aggression; self-correct by bluffing more
  2. Blockers are everything -- when bluffing: block calls, unblock folds. When calling: block value, unblock bluffs
  3. Bigger bets = more polarized = more bluffs allowed
  4. Protect every bet size with nuts -- small bets, large bets, and checking ranges all need some nut hands
  5. When raised on the river: fold a lot -- this is the most under-bluffed spot in all of poker
