1. Introduction to the River

Lesson: introduction-to-the-river
Value Bets vs Bluffs
  • Value bet: you expect to win at showdown >50% of the time when called
  • Bluff: you expect to lose at showdown ~100% of the time when called
  • Goal of value betting: extract maximum from worse hands
  • Goal of bluffing: force better hands to fold, while making your value bets get called

2. Analyzing Your Range

Lesson: analyzing-your-range

Two-Bet-Size Strategy

On the river, use two distinct bet sizes:

  • Small bet: 10%-50% pot (thin value + few bluffs + protective nuts)
  • Large bet: 67%-500% pot (premium hands + proportional bluffs)

Hand Spectrum Classification

Impact of Traps on Bluff %

Reduce bluffs when opponent has more traps. Traps increase when:

  • Opponent has a large nut advantage
  • Opponent's range is so weak they must check everything (including nuts)
  • You use small bet sizes (opponent has random rivered two-pair)
  • Opponent is prone to slow-playing

3. Analyzing Your Exact Hand

Lesson: analyzing-your-exact-hand

Value Betting Decision Tree

  1. Premium hand (65%+ equity when called)? Bet large (if opponent can call large bets)
  2. Thin value hand (51%+ equity when called)? Bet small
  3. Neither? Use bluffing decision flowchart

Bluffing Decision Flowchart

Answer each question -- more "yes" answers = more inclined to bluff:

6 Questions to Ask
  1. Is my opponent capable of folding?
  2. Do I lack showdown value?
  3. Do I have many value hands in my range?
  4. Are my blockers highly relevant?
  5. Do I unblock my opponent's auto-folds?
  6. Does my bluff tell a credible story?

Bluff Sizing Flowchart

  • Bet big when: zero showdown value, opponent has marginal hands that call small, you block nut hands, representing the nuts
  • Bet small when: some showdown value, opponent has weak hands that fold to any bet, not blocking nuts, representing thin value
Key Blocker Concepts
  • Blocking auto-folds is BAD for bluffing: if you hold cards opponent would fold, they're more likely to have calling hands
  • Blocking auto-calls is GOOD for bluffing: if you hold cards opponent would call with, they're more likely to fold
  • Blockers matter more with larger bet sizes
  • Blockers must remove realistically held hands (not theoretical ones)
Key Insight

River is notoriously under-bluffed at small/medium stakes. Most players are afraid to bluff river, especially afraid to overbet bluff. Exploit: over-fold to river aggression against most opponents.

4. Positional Adjustments

Lesson: positional-adjustments

In Position (IP) -- Betting vs Checking

SPR-Based IP Strategy
  • SPR <= 1 (shallow): Use one bet size only: all-in. Do NOT go for thin value with small bets. Top pair should just check behind.
  • SPR > 1 (deeper): Two bet sizes: ~50% pot (small) + all-in/overbet (large). Small bet IP is medium-sized (not tiny like OOP).

Out of Position (OOP) -- Four Major Adjustments

  1. Value bet small with very thin value (52-55% equity hands: third pair, bottom pair for 10-33% pot)
  2. On great rivers with big range advantage: use a single small size frequently
  3. Checking range must contain check-raise value hands (the absolute nuts)
  4. Checking range must contain check-raise bluffs (weak pairs that block opponent's calls)

Check-Raise Strategy from OOP

Check-Raise for Value
  • Check-raise with your absolute best hands (literal nuts, second nuts)
  • These hands are so strong they benefit from building a bigger pot
  • Check-raise wider as opponent bets more frequently
Check-Raise as Bluff
  • Best hands: bottom pair or weak pair (can win if check-check, blocks opponent's sets/two-pair)
  • Hand too weak to call a bet but has blockers to calling range
  • Opponent's range is capped (top pair max)

5. Deciding to Call on the River

Lesson: deciding-to-call-on-the-river
Bluff Catcher Definition

A bluff catcher is a hand that beats all of opponent's bluffs and loses to all of opponent's value hands.

Bluff Catching Decision Flowchart

  1. Are you beating any value hands? If YES → just call (not a bluff catcher)
  2. Does opponent's range have enough obvious bluffs? (busted draws, etc.)
  3. Is opponent capable of finding the right number of bluffs?
  4. Will opponent turn weak showdown hands into bluffs?
  5. Does my hand unblock the most obvious bluffs?
Key Insight: Tight Players Under-Bluff
  • Against tight recreational players: over-fold to river aggression
  • Against semi-professionals: they find more bluffs, call more
  • Against maniacs: call with all bluff catchers

6. Deciding to Raise for Value on the River

Lesson: deciding-to-raise-for-value-on-the-river

Raise Wider for Value When:

  • Facing a smaller bet size (opponent's range is more marginal)
  • Opponent is laggy / battling with you (they call raises wider)
  • You have one of the very best hands in your range

Raise Tighter for Value When:

  • Facing a large bet (opponent's range is polarized/strong)
  • Opponent folds all non-nut hands to a raise
  • Opponent's value hands are all premium

7. Deciding to Raise as a Bluff on the River

Lesson: deciding-to-raise-as-a-bluff-on-the-river
Key Insight

River bluff-raising is a notoriously under-bluffed spot. Use large sizes (2-5x pot) to maximize fold equity.

Factors that Increase Ability to Bluff Raise

  1. Facing a small river bet (marginal made range)
  2. Opponent is capable of folding
  3. Your hand has no showdown value
  4. You have many value hands in your range
  5. Your blockers are highly relevant (block auto-calls)
  6. Your bluff tells a credible story
Facing...Raise SizeReasoning
Small betRaise huge (4-5x)Get marginal hands to fold
Large betRaise smaller or don't raiseThey fold bluffs anyway, nuts never fold

8. When You Bet and Get Raised on the River

Lesson: when-you-bet-and-get-raised-on-the-river
Universal Rule: FOLD A LOT

This is the most straightforward spot in poker -- opponents almost never bluff enough here.

Out of Position (You Bet, IP Raises)

  • Fold all bluffs and bluff catchers
  • Fold most marginal made hands
  • Call only with your best hands

In Position (You Bet, OOP Check-Raises)

  • If you bet large and get raised: fold almost everything. Opponent is super-polarized.
  • If you bet small and get raised: defend closer to MDF (small bet may induce bluff raises)
Exploitative Approach

In small stakes: opponents NEVER bluff enough when raising the river. When raised on the river: overfold against basically everyone. Exception: if opponent is known to be maniacal.

9. Betting the River In Position (IP Examples)

Turn ActionRiver Strategy
Bet flop + bet turn bigJam or check (very polarized)
Bet flop + check turnSmall/medium bets only (no overbets)
Check flop + overbet turnCautious river (opponent called = strong)
Examples Highlights
  • A-J-5, 2 turn: After bet-bet, jam or check on most rivers. ~1.5x pot shoves with nuts + 33% bluffs. Bluffs use hands with 4 or 3 blockers.
  • J-6-6, 4 turn: After flop bet + check-check turn: no overbets (you lack super nuts). Small/medium bets only (~50% pot).
  • 10-8-6, Q turn: After check-check flop + overbet turn: opponent's range is strong after calling. Non-flush rivers: jam with sets. Flush rivers: medium bets only.

11. Facing a Bet on the River IP

Lesson: facing-a-bet-on-the-river-ip-examples
Blocker-Based Decisions
  • When calling: do NOT block opponent's bluffs; DO block their value
  • When raising: block opponent's auto-calls (nuts/strong hands)
  • Bluff raises: all hands should block the nuts
Key Example: 9-7-5-2, 8 River

A-2 (random pair) calls > 10-9 (trips) when facing an all-in. Why: A-2 doesn't block bluffs (J-10), while 10-9 blocks them heavily. Raw equity matters less than blockers when opponent is polarized.

12. Facing a Bet on the River OOP

Lesson: facing-a-bet-on-the-river-oop-examples
  • After calling flop + turn bets: you typically have range advantage but lack nut advantage
  • Opponent's river bet is very polarized (nuts or nothing)
  • Low aces (A-3, A-4) are better bluff catchers than high aces (A-10, A-J) because they don't block bluffs
Flush Blocker Strategy
  • When flush completes: call with flush, call with one-pair + spade blocker
  • Without spade blocker: fold one-pair hands
  • A spade in your hand blocks a huge portion of opponent's value range

Universal River Principles

The River is Under-Bluffed
  1. You should over-fold to river aggression from most opponents
  2. You should learn to bluff the river yourself in a balanced way
  3. You can overbluff against opponents who over-fold
Blockers Are Everything on the River
  • When bluffing: block opponent's calling range, unblock their folding range
  • When calling: block opponent's value range, unblock their bluffs
  • When raising: block opponent's auto-calls

Bet Size → Polarization Relationship

$$\text{Bigger bet} \rightarrow \text{More polarized range} \rightarrow \text{More bluffs allowed}$$
