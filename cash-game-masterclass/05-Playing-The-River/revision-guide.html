1. Introduction to the River

Lesson: introduction-to-the-river
Value Bets vs Bluffs
  • Value bet: you expect to win at showdown >50% of the time when called
  • Bluff: you expect to lose at showdown ~100% of the time when called
  • Goal of value betting: extract maximum from worse hands
  • Goal of bluffing: force better hands to fold, while making your value bets get called

2. Analyzing Your Range

Lesson: analyzing-your-range

Two-Bet-Size Strategy

On the river, use two distinct bet sizes:

  • Small bet: 10%-50% pot (thin value + few bluffs + protective nuts)
  • Large bet: 67%-500% pot (premium hands + proportional bluffs)

Hand Spectrum Classification

CategoryExamplesAction
Premium valueStraights, sets, top two pairBet large
Thin valueTop pair good kicker, weak two pairBet small
Some showdown valueMiddle pair, Ace-highUsually check
No showdown valueBusted draws, low cardsBluff candidate

Bluff Percentage Formula

Formula \[\text{Bluff\%} = \frac{\text{Call Amount}}{\text{Pot} + \text{Bet} + \text{Call}}\]
Bet SizeBluff % Allowed
33% pot20%
50% pot25%
75% pot30%
100% pot33%
200% pot40%
300% pot43%
500% pot45%

Impact of Traps on Bluff %

Reduce bluffs when opponent has more traps. Traps increase when:

  • Opponent has a large nut advantage
  • Opponent's range is so weak they must check everything (including nuts)
  • You use small bet sizes (opponent has random rivered two-pair)
  • Opponent is prone to slow-playing

River Card Impact on Strategy

River TypeEffect on Your Strategy

Good + equity bump onlyBet more frequently, small size
Bad + opponent gains equityCheck more, bet polarized when you do
Bad + opponent gains trapsBluff less often

3. Analyzing Your Exact Hand

Lesson: analyzing-your-exact-hand

Value Betting Decision Tree

  1. Premium hand (65%+ equity when called)? Bet large (if opponent can call large bets)
  2. Thin value hand (51%+ equity when called)? Bet small
  3. Neither? Use bluffing decision flowchart

Bluffing Decision Flowchart

Answer each question -- more "yes" answers = more inclined to bluff:

6 Questions to Ask
  1. Is my opponent capable of folding?
  2. Do I lack showdown value?
  3. Do I have many value hands in my range?
  4. Are my blockers highly relevant?
  5. Do I unblock my opponent's auto-folds?
  6. Does my bluff tell a credible story?

Bluff Sizing Flowchart

  • Bet big when: zero showdown value, opponent has marginal hands that call small, you block nut hands, representing the nuts
  • Bet small when: some showdown value, opponent has weak hands that fold to any bet, not blocking nuts, representing thin value
Key Blocker Concepts
  • Blocking auto-folds is BAD for bluffing: if you hold cards opponent would fold, they're more likely to have calling hands
  • Blocking auto-calls is GOOD for bluffing: if you hold cards opponent would call with, they're more likely to fold
  • Blockers matter more with larger bet sizes
  • Blockers must remove realistically held hands (not theoretical ones)
Key Insight

River is notoriously under-bluffed at small/medium stakes. Most players are afraid to bluff river, especially afraid to overbet bluff. Exploit: over-fold to river aggression against most opponents.

4. Positional Adjustments

Lesson: positional-adjustments

In Position (IP) -- Betting vs Checking

SPR-Based IP Strategy
  • SPR <= 1 (shallow): Use one bet size only: all-in. Do NOT go for thin value with small bets. Top pair should just check behind.
  • SPR > 1 (deeper): Two bet sizes: ~50% pot (small) + all-in/overbet (large). Small bet IP is medium-sized (not tiny like OOP).

Out of Position (OOP) -- Four Major Adjustments

  1. Value bet small with very thin value (52-55% equity hands: third pair, bottom pair for 10-33% pot)
  2. On great rivers with big range advantage: use a single small size frequently
  3. Checking range must contain check-raise value hands (the absolute nuts)
  4. Checking range must contain check-raise bluffs (weak pairs that block opponent's calls)

Check-Raise Strategy from OOP

Example: OOP Check-Raise for Value -- Flopped Nut Straight

Situation: $2/$5 cash game, 100bb effective ($500). You are in the Big Blind and call a Button raise with \(J\diamondsuit T\clubsuit\). Pot: $12. Board runs: \(Q\heartsuit 9\spadesuit 8\clubsuit\) (flop -- you have the nut straight) -- \(2\diamondsuit\) (turn) -- \(K\heartsuit\) (river).

River action: You check. Button bets $40 into an $80 pot (50% pot). You have the nut straight (J-T-Q-K = No -- wait: the nut straight on Q-9-8-2-K is J-T for J-T-Q-K... actually Q-9-8-2-K gives the straight 9-T-J-Q-K for anyone with JT, or 8-9-T-J-Q for JT... let us use the correct straight: with \(J\diamondsuit T\clubsuit\) on \(Q\heartsuit 9\spadesuit 8\clubsuit 2\diamondsuit K\heartsuit\), you have K-Q-J-T-9 -- the King-high straight. This is the nuts.)

The check-raise: Raise to $130 (3.25x their $40). Why raise? Your hand is the literal nuts -- you cannot be beaten. More importantly, the King on the river improves many hands in the Button's range. The Button raised preflop with hands like A-Q, K-Q, K-J, K-T -- on the K river, K-Q made two pair, K-J made a pair of kings, A-K made top pair. These hands bet the river for value and are not folding to a raise. By check-raising to $130, you extract maximum value from their now-improved hands. Calling $40 only extracts $40; raising to $130 extracts $90 more from hands that call.

EV of check-raise vs call: If the Button calls the raise 60% of the time: EV = 0.60 x $90 extra + call: $40 (guaranteed) = $40 + 0.60 x $90 = $40 + $54 = $94 extracted. Call only: $40 extracted. Check-raise earns $54 more per occurrence. Against a reg who bets the river at 50% pot on a wet board, this is a clear check-raise situation.

Example: OOP Check-Raise as Bluff -- Blocking Sets and Two-Pair

Situation: $1/$2 cash game, 100bb effective ($200). You are in the Big Blind and called a Cutoff raise with \(6\spadesuit 6\heartsuit\). Pot: $9. Board runs: \(A\clubsuit K\diamondsuit 7\spadesuit\) (flop) -- \(T\heartsuit\) (turn) -- \(2\diamondsuit\) (river).

Line so far: You check-called the flop, check-called the turn. River: You check. Cutoff bets $25 into a $45 pot (56% pot).

Your holding on the river: Pocket sixes, a pair of 6s on A-K-7-T-2. This has showdown value (beats bluffs and air) but loses to all of opponent's value hands (any pair of aces, kings, or better). It is a borderline call -- not great, not terrible. However, there is a better option: check-raise bluff to $85.

Why 66 is an ideal check-raise bluff: (1) Your sixes are below the natural "call" threshold against a half-pot river bet. (2) Holding \(6\spadesuit 6\heartsuit\), you block sets of sixes (66 = the only possible set of 6s, and you have it -- meaning opponent cannot have a set of 6s). You also block anyone with a 6 in their two-pair hand (76, 67). (3) If the check-raise goes wrong and they call, you occasionally win at showdown against pure air. (4) The bluff represents a flopped slow-played set (AA, KK, 77 are all plausible in a BB defend range) that came in quietly on the river.

EV of check-raise bluff vs calling: If they fold 60% of the time to the raise: EV of bluff = 0.60 x $70 (pot) - 0.40 x $60 (raise cost after call) = $42 - $24 = +$18. EV of calling: approximately +$3 if they bluff 30% of the time. The check-raise bluff earns $15 more per occurrence when opponent is capable of folding top pair to aggression.

5. Deciding to Call on the River

Lesson: deciding-to-call-on-the-river
Bluff Catcher Definition

A bluff catcher is a hand that beats all of opponent's bluffs and loses to all of opponent's value hands.

Bluff Catching Decision Flowchart

  1. Are you beating any value hands? If YES → just call (not a bluff catcher)
  2. Does opponent's range have enough obvious bluffs? (busted draws, etc.)
  3. Is opponent capable of finding the right number of bluffs?
  4. Will opponent turn weak showdown hands into bluffs?
  5. Does my hand unblock the most obvious bluffs?
Key Insight: Tight Players Under-Bluff
  • Against tight recreational players: over-fold to river aggression
  • Against semi-professionals: they find more bluffs, call more
  • Against maniacs: call with all bluff catchers

6. Deciding to Raise for Value on the River

Lesson: deciding-to-raise-for-value-on-the-river

Raise Wider for Value When:

  • Facing a smaller bet size (opponent's range is more marginal)
  • Opponent is laggy / battling with you (they call raises wider)
  • You have one of the very best hands in your range

Raise Tighter for Value When:

  • Facing a large bet (opponent's range is polarized/strong)
  • Opponent folds all non-nut hands to a raise
  • Opponent's value hands are all premium
Example: River Value Raise with Two Pair Facing Small Bet

Situation: $2/$5 cash game, 100bb effective ($500). You are on the Button and called a Big Blind 3-bet with \(A\spadesuit J\heartsuit\). Pot: $65. Board: \(A\clubsuit J\diamondsuit 4\heartsuit\) (flop, you bet and they called) -- \(8\spadesuit\) (turn, you bet and they called) -- \(2\clubsuit\) (river). River pot: $190. The Big Blind leads for $35 (18% pot -- a very small bet).

Your holding: \(A\spadesuit J\heartsuit\) -- top two pair (aces and jacks).

The raise: Raise to $110. Why raise for value? The BB's small river lead (18% pot) signals a marginal made hand -- most likely top pair weak kicker (A9, A8, A7), second pair (JT, J9), or a thin value bet with a hand like 44 (set of fours) or 88 (pair turned two-pair). They are NOT polarized (they'd bet bigger with the nuts or air). Against a marginal betting range, your top two pair is nearly the nuts. Raising to $110 extracts $75 more than calling the $35. Hands like A9, 88, J9 that bet $35 will call $110 because they think they have value.

Calculating the raise value: Against a range where they call a raise 50% of the time and fold 50%: EV of raise = 0.50 x $75 extra + call: $35 (guaranteed) = $35 + $37.50 = $72.50. EV of calling: $35. Raising earns $37.50 more. Against a "never fold top pair" player (call frequency 80%): EV = $35 + 0.80 x $75 = $35 + $60 = $95. Calling: $35. The raise value scales dramatically with how wide their calling range is.

When NOT to raise for value: If the BB had bet $120 (63% pot) instead of $35, their range is more polarized (strong hands and bluffs, fewer marginals). Your two pair is still the best non-nut hand, but raising $120 risks them having AA (unlikely given preflop 3-bet) or a flopped set they slow-played. Against a large bet, just call with top two pair.

7. Deciding to Raise as a Bluff on the River

Lesson: deciding-to-raise-as-a-bluff-on-the-river
Key Insight

River bluff-raising is a notoriously under-bluffed spot. Use large sizes (2-5x pot) to maximize fold equity.

Factors that Increase Ability to Bluff Raise

  1. Facing a small river bet (marginal made range)
  2. Opponent is capable of folding
  3. Your hand has no showdown value
  4. You have many value hands in your range
  5. Your blockers are highly relevant (block auto-calls)
  6. Your bluff tells a credible story

Large betRaise smaller or don't raiseThey fold bluffs anyway, nuts never fold

8. When You Bet and Get Raised on the River

Lesson: when-you-bet-and-get-raised-on-the-river
Universal Rule: FOLD A LOT

This is the most straightforward spot in poker -- opponents almost never bluff enough here.

Out of Position (You Bet, IP Raises)

  • Fold all bluffs and bluff catchers
  • Fold most marginal made hands
  • Call only with your best hands

In Position (You Bet, OOP Check-Raises)

  • If you bet large and get raised: fold almost everything. Opponent is super-polarized.
  • If you bet small and get raised: defend closer to MDF (small bet may induce bluff raises)
Exploitative Approach

In small stakes: opponents NEVER bluff enough when raising the river. When raised on the river: overfold against basically everyone. Exception: if opponent is known to be maniacal.

9. Betting the River In Position (IP Examples)

Turn ActionRiver Strategy
Bet flop + bet turn bigJam or check (very polarized)
Bet flop + check turnSmall/medium bets only (no overbets)
Check flop + overbet turnCautious river (opponent called = strong)
Example 1: A♠-J♥-5♠, 2♥ turn -- Bet-Bet Line IP (BTN vs BB, 100bb)

Full situation: You raise BTN to 3bb, BB calls. Pot: 6.5bb. Flop A♠-J♥-5♠. BB checks, you bet 4bb (62% pot). BB calls. Pot: 14.5bb. Turn 2♥. BB checks, you overbet ~16bb (110% pot). BB calls. Pot: ~46bb. You have ~54bb behind. SPR on river: ~1.2. River arrives (e.g., 8♦).

River strategy -- jam or check, nothing else: With only 1.2x pot remaining, the solver uses exactly two actions: jam all-in (~140% of the remaining pot) or check. Half-pot and two-thirds-pot bets do not appear. Why? Your line (bet-bet big) announces a polarized range -- you either have a strong hand (AJ, AA, JJ, 55, A5s, AK) or a draw that missed. A medium river bet with a polarized range is dominated: your value hands want max money, and your bluffs need to maximize fold equity.

Value hands that jam: AJ (top two pair), AA/JJ/55 (sets), A5s (two pair), AK and AQ (strong top pairs that are near-premium here). These are the super nuts on this board and all want max value.

Bluffs that jam: ~33% of the jamming range. Prefer hands that hold a 3 or 4 (block the gutshot 3-4 straight that BB might call with), or hands holding a 10/9 (block A-10, A-9 hero calls). For example, K4s with a busted backdoor flush draw is a strong bluff candidate -- it blocks straights and has no showdown value.

Hands that check: Thin value hands like K-10, weak aces (A8, A7), middle pairs -- these cannot jam profitably and would be destroyed by a BB check-raise jam. Also give up with some bluffs you cannot profitably jam (K7, K6 off-suit that don't hold relevant blockers).

EV cost of medium-bet mistake: If you bet 50% pot instead of jamming with a set of Aces, you allow BB to call with weaker Aces and pocket pairs for a fraction of the EV you would capture by jamming. Against a range that contains A-x calling hands, the correct jam extracts full expected value. Small-betting the nuts here could easily cost 8-15bb per occurrence.

Example 2: J♠-6♥-6♣-4♠, river -- Flop Bet + Check-Check Turn IP (LJ vs BB, 100bb)

Full situation: You raise LJ to 3bb, BB calls. Pot: 6.5bb. Flop J♠-6♥-6♣. BB checks, you bet small (~2.5bb, 38% pot). BB calls. Pot: ~11.5bb. Turn 4♠. BB checks, you check back. Pot: ~11.5bb. River arrives (e.g., A♣).

Why you checked the turn: On J-6-6, the Jack is a frequent check-back on the turn because if you bet and BB check-raises, you are often dominated by a 6. Most of your "good" turn hands (aces, jacks, overpairs) prefer the cautious check-back to avoid being blown off their equity by BB's trips.

Range damage from turn check: After check-check on the turn, your range is capped at overpairs and jacks. You would have bet the turn with trips, full houses, and your best premium hands. So on the river, you have: almost no nuts. The preferred bet size is ~50% pot; overbets (80%+) do not appear in the solver output because you lack the super nuts to support them credibly.

Ace river strategy: The Ace is actually a good river for you. BB is not calling the flop with a lot of ace-high combinations (they fold many to even a small flop bet), and you now have a range full of aces, which are strong here. Value bet ~50% pot with pocket aces (which are the effective nuts), pocket jacks (near-nuts -- full house), and strong ace-x (A-K, A-Q). Bluff occasionally with hands like K-Q, K-9 (blocking the opponent's Jacks and Aces that might thin-value-call). Do NOT bet weak aces (A-4, A-3) for thin value -- if BB check-raises, you are in a terrible spot.

Six river strategy: The 6 is another good river. Since you would have bet the turn with sixes, BB's range is also lacking sixes (they would have called any bet). You now hold all your pairs and aces, plus BB's range is capped at Jacks and overpairs. Bet ~50% pot with almost everything (aces, kings, jacks, strong overpairs). Fewer bluffs needed because your range is dense with good hands.

Nine-or-lower river (e.g., 9♣): A low blank is actually bad for you because BB checked the turn with hands like 9-8, 8-7, 7-6 that now have strong pairs. You cannot bet thin value often. Bet only your best hands (aces, jacks) and check back with medium hands to avoid getting check-raised by BB's well-disguised pairs and trips.

EV cost of overbetting without nuts: If you overbet jam the Ace river in this spot, BB's checking range contains many kings and sixes that called the flop and turn. When you jam, BB calls you with 6-x trips and crushes you. Sticking to 50% pot avoids this catastrophe and still extracts value from BB's weaker jacks and aces.

Example 3: 10♦-8♣-6♥-Q♥, river -- Check-Check Flop + Overbet Turn IP (LJ vs BTN, 100bb)

Full situation: LJ raises, BTN (you) calls. Pot: ~6.5bb. Flop 10♦-8♣-6♥. LJ checks, you check back (uncapped checking range -- you have straights, sets, overpairs all in your checking range here). Turn Q♥. LJ checks, you overbet ~130% pot. LJ calls. Pot: large. River arrives.

What LJ's range looks like after calling: By calling your turn overbet on Q, LJ has announced a strong range. They folded all their garbage (under pairs, backdoor draw hands). Their calling range consists of: Q-x top pair (Q-J, Q-10, Q-9), 10-8 / 10-6 two pair, sets (TT, 88, 66), straights (9-7, J-9), and some strong flush draws. This range is condensed and dangerous.

Ace river (e.g., A♥ arrives): Bet infrequently, but with two distinct sizes. Overbet/jam with: sets (10-10, 8-8, 6-6), straights (9-7, J-9 if you have them), K-J (the nuts here). Use 50% pot bet with: A-Q (strong two pair -- but NOT a clear overbet candidate because LJ can have K-J for the nuts). Check behind: A-K (thin -- LJ has many Aces in their calling range from the turn), Q-10 (two pair -- looks strong but LJ's range is too good after the overbet call), A-Q non-nut two pairs. The critical insight: even Q-10 (two pair) does NOT value bet here in solver output. Opponent called an overbet -- respect their strength.

Bluffs when betting big: 9-8 (blocks set of 8s and two pair 10-8, 9-7), 5-4 (blocks the straight 9-7 if relevant), low pairs with spades (block flush draws LJ might have called turn with). ~35% bluffs in the overbet range because you are not perfectly polarized -- LJ can have better hands like KJ or 9-7s.

Flush-completing river (e.g., 5♥ -- heart completes): Dramatic change. LJ's turn-calling range includes many heart flush draws (Q♥-x, 10♥-x, J♥-x). They now have completed flushes you do not have (you checked the flop, so your heart holdings are limited). Do NOT use all-in sizing. Use 50-66% pot bets with flushes you do hold and sets. Check two-pair hands. ~35% bluffs in the bet-big range, but reduce overall bet frequency compared to a brick river. Key: the opponent having the flush advantage means their calling range is very strong -- don't bet thin.

EV cost of thin value after turn overbet: Betting top pair (A-K, Q-J) on the river after opponent called your turn overbet costs you heavily when they check-raise jam with their sets or two pairs. In solver output, even two pairs like Q-10 check back. The mistake of thin value betting here is one of the most common and costly errors made by players moving up from small stakes.

10. Betting the River Out of Position (OOP Examples)

Your Range StateRiver Approach
Range advantage, no nut advantageBet frequently, small
Nut advantage presentOverbet with nuts, bluff with blockers
Opponent has nut advantageCheck a lot, bet polarized when you do
Checking rangeInclude some nuts for check-raise, plus weak-pair bluffs
Example: 9-7-5, 2 turn -- OOP as BB After Check-Raise

Situation: CO raised, you called from BB. Flop 9-7-5. CO bet, you check-raised, CO called. Turn 2 (brick). You bet pot, CO called. Now the river arrives.

Good rivers (8 or 6 -- completing straights): You have tons of straights (T6s, 86s, 64s, T8s) that CO cannot hold. Bet frequently and mostly small (33-50% pot) for thin value. Your opponent's range is mostly overpairs and top pairs that will call small bets but fold to huge ones.

Bad rivers (9 -- pairing the top card): This card is bad for you because CO's range includes 99 (top set), A9, K9, and trip 9s. Bet infrequently. When you do bet, overbet massively (4x pot) with your absolute nuts (straights, sets of 7s/5s) plus select bluffs. The overbet works because CO's range is polarized between trips/full houses (which call anything) and everything else (which folds to the huge sizing). Check JT sometimes as a check-raise trap -- it has a straight and can extract maximum value by trapping CO's bets.

Example: K-K-6, 2 turn -- OOP River Decisions

Situation: You 3-bet from BB, BTN called. Flop K-K-6. You bet small, BTN called. Turn 2. You bet again, BTN called.

Ace river: The Ace is a scary card. Your opponent's range includes AK (now a full house), AQ, AJ (just made a strong pair). Bet infrequently -- maybe 30% of the time. When you do bet, use a big size (120% pot) with your best Kings (KQ, KJ -- full houses) plus blocker bluffs (hands with an Ace that block AK). Most of the time, check and accept the showdown.

Low rivers (3, 4, 5): Nothing changed. Your Kings are still the best hand. Bet very frequently (70%+) and small (33% pot) for thin value. BTN's range after calling two streets includes pocket pairs (QQ, JJ, TT) and random sixes that will pay off a small bet. Include your weakest hands as bluffs to maintain balance.

11. Facing a Bet on the River IP

Lesson: facing-a-bet-on-the-river-ip-examples
Blocker-Based Decisions
  • When calling: do NOT block opponent's bluffs; DO block their value
  • When raising: block opponent's auto-calls (nuts/strong hands)
  • Bluff raises: all hands should block the nuts
Key Example: Why A-2 Calls Better Than T-9 on 9-7-5-2-8

Situation: BB check-raised the flop on 9-7-5, bet the turn on the 2, and now jams all-in on the 8 river. You are IP and must decide whether to call.

The counterintuitive result: A-2 (a random small pair of deuces) is a better call than T-9 (trip nines). T-9 looks like a much stronger hand, but blocker effects dominate on polarized rivers:

  • T-9 blocks bluffs heavily: BB's primary bluffs are JT and T6 (missed straight draws). By holding a Ten and a Nine, you remove many of those bluff combos from BB's range. This means when BB jams, their remaining range is weighted much more toward value (straights, sets).
  • A-2 unblocks bluffs: A-2 doesn't block JT, T6, or any of BB's natural bluff combos. So when BB jams, their full range of bluffs is still possible. This means BB is more likely to be bluffing when you hold A-2.

The lesson: When your opponent's range is polarized (nuts or nothing), raw hand strength matters far less than which of their combos you block. Always ask: "Does my hand remove bluffs from their range (bad for calling) or remove value hands (good for calling)?"

12. Facing a Bet on the River OOP

Lesson: facing-a-bet-on-the-river-oop-examples
  • After calling flop + turn bets: you typically have range advantage but lack nut advantage
  • Opponent's river bet is very polarized (nuts or nothing)
  • Low aces (A-3, A-4) are better bluff catchers than high aces (A-10, A-J) because they don't block bluffs
Flush Blocker Strategy
  • When flush completes: call with flush, call with one-pair + spade blocker
  • Without spade blocker: fold one-pair hands
  • A spade in your hand blocks a huge portion of opponent's value range

Universal River Principles

The River is Under-Bluffed
  1. You should over-fold to river aggression from most opponents
  2. You should learn to bluff the river yourself in a balanced way
  3. You can overbluff against opponents who over-fold
Blockers Are Everything on the River
  • When bluffing: block opponent's calling range, unblock their folding range
  • When calling: block opponent's value range, unblock their bluffs
  • When raising: block opponent's auto-calls

Bet Size → Polarization Relationship

\[\text{Bigger bet} \rightarrow \text{More polarized range} \rightarrow \text{More bluffs allowed}\]
