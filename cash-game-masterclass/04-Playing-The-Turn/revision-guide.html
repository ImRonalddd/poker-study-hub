Foundational Principle: Bluff-to-Value Ratios

Key Insight
StreetMax Ratio (Premium : Bluffs)
Flop1 : 2
Turn1 : 1
River2 : 1 (at pot-sized bets)

Fewer cards to come means draws complete less often, so you need fewer bluffs to remain balanced.

What to Check More on the Turn (vs Flop)

  • Premium hands downgraded to marginal (e.g., top pair on a scary turn)
  • Draws that got worse or picked up no additional equity
  • Draws with reverse implied odds (completing could still lose)
  • Draws that turned into marginal made hands (e.g., open-ender turns a pair)

1. Turn Continuation Betting

Lesson: turn-continuation-betting
Why Ranges Are Closer on the Turn

When opponent check-calls the flop, they lose all their junk (folded it). But you still have some junk (bad draws, air that you bet). This makes equities converge, forcing a polarized strategy on the turn.

Turn Card Classification

Turn TypeExamplesYour Strategy
Good (overcards, aces)K-8-4-A, 8-5-2-KBet very frequently (60%+), sometimes 100%
Neutral (blanks, top card pairs)K-K-T-6, A-J-5-2Mix betting and checking; bet polarized
Bad (middle/bottom pairs, straights)J-7-3-7, 9-7-5-8Check very frequently; bet only super nuts + best draws

Sizing on the Turn

  • Have nut advantage + coordinated board → Bet big (66%-125% pot)
  • Have nut advantage + dry board → Bet smaller (33%-66% pot)
  • Lack nut advantage → Bet small (25%-33% pot)

Which Draws to Bet vs Check

Pattern

On the turn, you either bet draws that are good enough to call a raise or bad enough that folding doesn't hurt. Avoid betting medium-strength draws.

  • Bet: Best draws that can call a raise (straight + flush draws, overcards + flush draws)
  • Check: Medium draws you'd hate to fold if raised (flush draw + marginal pair)
  • Bet: Worst draws that are fine folding to a raise (pure air, no showdown value)

2. Facing a Turn Continuation Bet

Lesson: facing-a-turn-continuation-bet
Hand TypeAction
JunkFold

Premium hands + draws (no nut advantage)Mostly call
Premium hands + draws (have nut advantage)Raise sometimes

Facing Different Bet Sizes

Opponent's SizeYour Raise FrequencyYour Fold Frequency
Small (25-33% pot)Raise sometimesFold rarely
Medium (50-66% pot)Raise rarelyFold near MDF
Large (100%+ pot / overbet)Basically never raiseOverfold slightly vs MDF
Critical Pattern

Against overbets, never raise from a GTO perspective. The opponent is announcing extreme polarization.

Protecting Your Calling Range
  • Never raise all your flushes -- keep some in your calling range
  • Keep some strong hands as calls so your range stays protected for the river
  • Against good opponents, if you raise every nut hand, your calling range becomes weak and exploitable

3. Delayed Continuation Bets

Lesson: delayed-continuation-bets-2

Why You Often Have an Advantage

  • In position: Opponent checked flop and turn -- their range is weak/marginal
  • Out of position: You checked everything on flop, opponent checked behind -- they lack premium hands
  • In both cases, you retain some nut hands from protecting your flop checking range

Sizing Principles

  • Worse the two factors (bad flop for you, bad turn for you) → Bet less often and large (polarized)
  • Better the two factors (good flop for you, good turn for you) → Bet more often and small (range bet)
Common Pattern: OOP Delayed C-Bet

When you check 100% OOP (e.g., on T-8-6), you retain your entire range including all premium hands. Opponent's check-back means they lack premiums. On turn:

  • Good turn card (overcard like K): Bet frequently and small
  • Neutral/bad turn: Bet infrequently and big (polarized with nuts + draws)

4. Facing a Delayed Continuation Bet

Lesson: facing-a-delayed-continuation-bet-2

After check-check on flop, you check the turn and opponent bets. Your range is somewhat capped because you've checked twice.

  • Junk → Fold
  • Marginal made hands → Call
  • Premium hands + draws → Mostly call (you almost never have nut advantage here)
  • As opponent bets bigger → Fold more, raise less
  • As opponent bets smaller → Call more, raise more
Common Mistake

Many players always bet all their strong hands when they have them, leaving their check-check range as pure trash. Protect your checking range by including some strong hands.

5. Turn Probes

Lesson: turn-probes-2
Definition

An out-of-position bet made after the preflop aggressor checked behind on the flop. You called preflop, check-check on flop, now you bet the turn.

Probe Strategy Grid

Flop RangeTurn CardProbe Strategy
Strong disadvantage (Q-T-7)Bad (K)Rarely bet, overbet when you do
Strong disadvantage (Q-T-7)Good (low card)Bet big, pretty infrequent
Weak disadvantage (9-7-5)Bad (K)Rarely bet, medium size
Weak disadvantage (9-7-5)Good (6 or 8)Bet frequently and small
Special Case: Four-Card Straight Boards

On boards like 9-7-5-8 or 9-7-5-6, BB has a massive nut advantage (tons of straights). Bet small and very frequently.

6. Facing Turn Probes

Lesson: facing-a-turn-probe-2

You raised preflop, checked back the flop, opponent bets the turn. Your range is relatively capped.

Probe SizeYour Raising Frequency
25% pot (small probe)Raise with nuts + some bluffs
60% pot (medium probe)Raise very rarely
100%+ pot (overbet probe)Basically never raise
Protecting Your Checkback Range

If you always c-bet your good hands, your check-back range is trash and you bleed equity against competent opponents who probe. Always protect your flop checkback range with some strong hands.

7. Flop Caller IP When Checked To on Turn

Lesson: as-the-flop-caller-ip-when-checked-to-on-the-turn
Range Comparison
  • Opponent: Mostly marginal made hands + junk (premium hands would have bet again)
  • You: Well-protected range including slow-played premiums + marginal hands + draws

You typically have a big range advantage in this spot:

  • Neutral-ish turn → Bet polarized (big, with best hands + draws)
  • Good turn for you → Bet frequently and small
  • If equities are close → Bet less often, still polarized

Aim for the 50/50 premium-to-draw ratio when betting big.

8. Multi-Way Pots on the Turn

Lesson: playing-multi-way-on-the-turn

When Lots of Action Goes In

  • Check-fold most hands except strongest made hands + best draws
  • Be hyper-aware of position (closing action = can call looser)
  • When multiple players call a flop bet + turn bet, they all have something -- tighten up

When the Flop Checks Through

  • Preflop aggressor's range is capped
  • OOP players (blinds) often end up with range + nut advantage
  • This is a prime spot to bluff from the BB on middle/low connected boards
  • If it checks to you in position and everyone seems uninterested, steal with a big bet
Live Poker Exploit

When everyone checks twice and goes back to watching TV / playing on phones -- it's your pot. Most small/medium stakes players don't protect their multi-way checking ranges. Bet the turn and river, and the pot is usually yours.

9. 3-Bet and 4-Bet Pots on the Turn

Lesson: 3-bet-4-bet-pots-on-the-turn
Key Differences from Single-Raised Pots
  • Ranges are much tighter pre-flop → fewer hand types at each stage
  • SPR is lower → jam the turn more often
  • Less overbetting -- pot is already large, 66% pot is often the "big" size
  • In 4-bet pots, your range is often just AA/KK/AK → you can often just jam everything

4-Bet Pot Specifics

  • Ranges are super condensed (AA, KK, QQ, AK, maybe JJ)
  • Protection is paramount -- hands like KK need to jam on most turns
  • On the best turn card (usually K), bet frequently and small
  • On all other turns, mostly jam or check -- very binary decision
  • AA can slow-play sometimes (needs less protection than KK)

Key Recurring Patterns Across All Turn Spots

The Range Advantage / Nut Advantage Framework

Range Advantage?Nut Advantage?Strategy
YesYesBet frequently, small to medium size
YesNoBet frequently, small size
NoYesBet medium frequency, big size (polarized)
NoNoBet infrequently, medium size

Turn Card Effect Heuristic

  • Overcards / Aces: Usually good for preflop/flop aggressor
  • Low blanks: Whoever had the nut advantage keeps it
  • Middle/bottom card pairs: Good for the flop caller
  • Draw completions: Good for the player with more draws in range
  • Flush completions: Good for the player with more suited combos
The Overbet-Raise Relationship

When your opponent overbets → You never raise (they are maximally polarized). When opponent bets small → You raise more (their range is wider/weaker). This holds in every single turn spot without exception.

Protecting Your Checking/Calling Range

This is the single most important theme on the turn:

  1. On the flop: Don't bet all your strong hands -- keep some to protect your check
  2. On the turn: Don't raise all your nuts -- keep some in your calling range
  3. When checked to: Don't bet all your nut hands -- slow-play some for protection
  4. The cost of having an unprotected range far exceeds the cost of occasionally missing thin value

Reliable Turn Betting Patterns

  • Pair + straight draw = almost always bet (e.g., 9-8 on a 9-7-5 board getting a 6 turn)
  • Under-pairs as bluffs: GTO frequently uses small pocket pairs as bluffs on the turn when logical draws are scarce. These have ~2 outs to the nuts (set) and don't block opponent's folding range.
