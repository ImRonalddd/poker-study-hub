Playing the Turn

Cash Game Masterclass -- Chapter 4 Crash Course

1. The Turn Bluffing Ratio

Definition
On the turn, you can have at most a 1:1 ratio of premium made hands to draws/bluffs. This is down from the flop's 1:2 ratio because with one fewer card to come, draws complete less often.

As bet size changes, so does the ratio:

  • Overbetting (125%+ pot): Approximately 1:1 ratio (maximum bluffs)
  • Pot-sized bet: Roughly 1:1
  • Half-pot bet: Fewer bluffs allowed (opponent gets better odds)
  • Quarter-pot bet: Very few bluffs
Insight
The key shift from flop to turn: you must find additional hands to check. These are draws that got worse, premium hands that got downgraded, and medium-strength draws you'd hate to fold if raised.

2. The Range/Nut Advantage Framework

Every turn decision starts with two questions:

  1. Do I have the range advantage? (More overall equity)
  2. Do I have the nut advantage? (More very strong hands)
Range Adv?Nut Adv?Strategy
YesYesBet frequently, small-medium size
YesNoBet frequently, small size
NoYesMedium frequency, big size (polarized)
NoNoBet infrequently, medium size
Example
A-J-5 flop, 6 turn (LJ vs BB, you bet flop, they called): You have no range advantage (opponent's check-call range is strong), but you have the nut advantage (AK, sets, two-pair). Strategy: bet medium amount using a big size (125% pot), very polarized.

3. Turn Card Classification

Good Turns for the Flop Aggressor

  • Overcards (especially aces): Your bluffs were ace-high, king-high -- they just became top pair
  • Cards that complete YOUR draws but not opponent's: When opponent check-raised their draws on flop, they don't have draws left
  • High cards matching your preflop range: K, Q, J often improve your overcards to pairs

Neutral Turns

  • Top card pairs (e.g., K-8-4 then K): You both have some trips; mix betting and checking
  • Complete blanks (e.g., A-J-5-2): Whoever was ahead stays ahead; bet polarized

Bad Turns for the Flop Aggressor

  • Middle/bottom card pairs (J-7-3-7): Opponent check-called with all their 7s; you checked yours
  • Cards completing opponent's draws (9-7-5-8): Opponent's range has tons of 6s and 8s
Trap
Don't assume all low cards are bad for you. On dry boards like K-7-5, a low blank (2 or 3) actually preserves your nut advantage (overcards are still premium). Low cards are bad primarily on connected boards where they complete opponent's straights.

4. Turn Continuation Betting

Definition
A turn continuation bet is specifically when the flop aggressor continues betting on the turn. It is NOT a delayed c-bet or a probe.

Why Opponent's Range Is Stronger

When they check-call the flop, they shed all junk and some of their best hands (would have raised). Their range is now marginal made hands + draws + some premium. This forces you to be more selective.

Which Draws to Barrel

  • Barrel: Best draws that can call a raise (nut flush draws, big combo draws)
  • Barrel: Worst draws happy folding to a raise (pure air with overcards)
  • Check: Medium draws you'd hate to fold (flush draw + pair, non-nut flush draw)
Insight
This "barbell" pattern with draws is unique to the turn. On the flop you can bet any draw. On the turn, with shallower SPR, you risk getting check-shoved. Bet draws that either happily continue or happily fold to a raise.

Pair + Straight Draw = Almost Always Bet

Across every example board (J-6-2, A-J-5, 9-7-5, K-7-5, K-K-6, Q-T-5), hands with a pair plus a straight draw consistently bet. Examples: 9-8 on 9-7-5, 6-5 on J-6-2-3, J-8 on 9-7-5-J. This is one of the most reliable turn patterns.

5. Facing a Turn Continuation Bet

Core Principle

Your opponent's turn c-bet is saying: "I'm polarized. I have nuts or air." Against a polarized range, you do a lot of calling and very little raising.

The Overbet Rule

Trap
When your opponent overbets the turn (100%+ pot), never raise from a GTO perspective. They are maximally polarized. Just call your strong hands and fold your junk. Many players raise their best hands against overbets -- this weakens their calling range catastrophically.

Minimum Defense Frequency

\[\text{MDF} = 1 - \frac{\text{Bet Size}}{\text{Bet} + \text{Pot}}\]
Bet SizeMDF (must defend)
25% pot80%
33% pot75%
50% pot67%
66% pot60%
100% pot50%
125% pot45%

In position, call more than MDF (you realize equity well). Out of position, call less than MDF (you under-realize equity).

Protect Your Calling Range

  • Don't raise every flush -- keep some as calls for river protection
  • Don't raise every two-pair -- mix calls and raises
  • If you raise all nuts, your calling range becomes marginal-only and collapses to river pressure

6. Delayed Continuation Bets

When You Have the Advantage After Check-Check

If you protected your flop checking range (kept some strong hands), you arrive at the turn with:

  • Some nut hands (sets, top pair that you checked for deception)
  • Marginal made hands (underpairs, middle pair)
  • Opponent's range is condensed (they would have bet their best hands and draws on the flop)
Example
T-8-6 flop (OOP), check-check, K turn: The King is the best card in the deck for you. Opponent would have bet most king-high on the flop as a bluff. When they didn't, they lack kings. You have all kings. Bet frequently and small (25% pot) -- checking only ~14% of range.

Key Pattern

When opponent checks back the flop with a condensed range, and the turn is good for you, your top pairs become effective nuts because opponent lacks top pairs. This lets you value bet thinly with a small sizing.

7. Turn Probes

Definition
A probe bet is an OOP bet after the preflop aggressor checked behind on the flop. You (the caller) are betting into the raiser who declined to c-bet.

Two Critical Factors

  1. Your flop range disadvantage: How well does the board favor BB vs the raiser?
  2. The turn card: Does it improve your range or your opponent's?

The 9-7-5 Pattern

On low connected boards, BB has many straights and two-pairs. When a 6 or 8 comes:

  • BB has a massive nut advantage (opponent would have bet their 6s and 8s on the flop)
  • Bet small and frequently (25% pot, 70% of range)
  • Similar to when the flop aggressor has a big range advantage and bets small
Insight
When your opponent would have bet a card on the flop but didn't, and that card arrives on the turn, it's paradoxically great for you. The 8 on 9-7-5 is the most profitable probe card because opponent literally cannot have 8s (they would have bet them).

8. Facing Turn Probes

Protect Your Checkback Range

This is the #1 lesson. If you always c-bet your good hands, your check-back range is pure garbage and you bleed money against competent probers.

Response by Probe Size

  • Small probe (25% pot): Raise with best hands + bluffs; call very wide (80%)
  • Medium probe (60% pot): Raise rarely; call near MDF
  • Large probe (overbet): Never raise; call or fold
Example: Raising a Turn Probe on a Completed Board

Setup: $2/$5 cash game, 6-max, 100bb effective ($500). UTG opens to $15. You call on the Button with \(J\heartsuit T\heartsuit\). Big Blind calls. Pot: $47.

Flop: \(9\clubsuit 7\diamondsuit 5\spadesuit\). BB checks, UTG checks, you check back. Pot: $47.

Turn: \(8\heartsuit\). BB probes $12 (~25% pot). UTG folds.

Action: You hold JT — a flopped open-ended straight draw that just got there (J-T on 9-7-5-8 = nut straight). Raise to $40.

Why raising is correct:

  • The 8 is the best possible card for you. The BB probed small, advertising a wide range — pairs, weak draws, two-pair-type hands.
  • BB literally cannot have 8-x (they would have bet the flop with a pair + OESD). Your straight is essentially the nuts here.
  • Calling lets BB see a free river and possibly check back, losing value from all the pairs in their range.
  • Raising charges pair hands max. If they fold: fine, you win $59. If they call: pot grows to ~$127, and you can pot or jam the river (~$400 left, 3x pot) for maximum value.

EV of raise vs call: If BB folds to raise 55% of the time: EV(raise $40) = 0.55 × $59 + 0.45 × (future value on river) = $32.45 + ~$30 future = ~$62. EV(call) = $47 pot × river realized value = ~$45 expected. Raising wins ~$17 more.

Bluff-raise candidates in the same spot (with 10-x, 6-x draws): A raise with \(T\clubsuit 6\clubsuit\) (flopped OESD, turn gives open-ender to gutshot only) works because: BB's small probe announces weakness, you represent the same straights, and if they fold you show a profit immediately. You pick up the pot 55%+ of the time and have 9 outs when called.

9. Flop Caller IP: Checked To on Turn

Someone raised, you called IP. They bet flop, you called. They check the turn.

Insight
When opponent bets flop and then checks turn, they're announcing a marginal range. You retained a well-protected range (you don't raise much IP). You now have a big range advantage.

Strategy

  • You're usually betting small and frequently (25% pot, ~60% of range)
  • Unless equities are very close, in which case you bet big and polarized
  • Protect your checking range -- don't bet every good hand
  • Even hands like underpairs can bet small for thin value (opponent's range is weak)

10. Multi-Way Pots

When Money Goes In Multi-Way

  • Tighten up dramatically -- multiple callers all have something
  • Fold hands you'd normally continue with heads-up (e.g., fold T-9 with a pair + gutshot when facing a bet + call multi-way)
  • Position is even more important (closing action = can call looser)

When Flop Checks Through Multi-Way

  • Preflop raiser is capped (would have bet strong hands)
  • OOP players (blinds) retain entire range including nuts
  • BB should attack on low/connected turn cards with medium-sized bets
  • If in position and everyone looks uninterested, make a big bet and steal
Trap
Don't assume multi-way pots play like heads-up. A hand like top pair no kicker that you'd happily call heads-up might need to be folded when there's a bet and a call in front of you. Each additional caller drastically narrows what they can have.

11. 3-Bet and 4-Bet Pots

Key Differences

  • Tighter ranges: Fewer hand types, easier to categorize
  • Lower SPR: Jamming the turn is common and correct
  • Less overbetting: 66% pot is often the "large" size; pot is already big
  • Protection is paramount: Over pairs on coordinated boards should jam

Slow-Playing the Super Nuts

In 3-bet pots, you should start checking back top set on the turn sometimes. Example: A-9-3-8 in a 3-bet pot -- check back pocket aces to protect your checking range. Bet AK/A9/A8 as value instead.

4-Bet Pot Simplification

Your range is essentially AA, KK, AK (maybe QQ, JJ). On most turns:

  • Best turn card (usually K): Bet frequently and small
  • All other turns: Mostly jam or check -- very binary
  • KK needs more protection than AA, so KK jams more often

12. Universal Turn Principles

The Five Commandments

  1. Protect your checking/calling range -- the single most important theme
  2. Never raise against overbets -- the opponent is maximally polarized
  3. Bet small and frequently with range advantage -- nudge up equity across all hands
  4. Bet big and polarized without range advantage -- use only nuts + draws
  5. Always consider how the turn card changes equities -- reclassify your hand every street

The Hand Reclassification Process

Every turn card requires you to re-evaluate:

  • Did my premium hand get downgraded to marginal? (Check it)
  • Did my draw improve to premium? (Bet it)
  • Did my draw get worse? (Move to junk, check-fold)
  • Did my marginal hand become junk? (Fold to aggression)
Final Insight
The turn is where implementable strategy and GTO start to diverge most significantly. Don't try to perfectly replicate solver output. Instead, master the range advantage + nut advantage framework, keep your ratios reasonable, and always protect your checking range. If you do these three things, you'll be far ahead of the vast majority of opponents.
