1. Stack Depth Matters

Lesson: stack-depth-matters-2
Core Principle

You always play the effective stack -- the shortest stack involved in the hand.

Deep-Stacked Adjustments (200bb+)

  • Go up in value: Suited aces, suited connectors, small/medium pairs (big implied odds)
  • Go down in value: Offsuit broadways (KJo, QTo), unimproved pocket pairs
  • Reason: Deep stacks mean more money behind. If you make top pair marginal kicker and opponent wants to put in 200bb, you are in trouble.
Example: Why KJo Is Dangerous at 200bb

Situation: 6-max, 200bb effective. You raise to 3.5bb from CO with K♠J♦. BTN calls. Pot: 8.5bb. Flop: K♥9♦3♣ (top pair, second kicker).

You bet 5bb. BTN raises to 16bb. You are now facing a raise with top pair second kicker, 200bb deep. If you call and BTN fires 3 streets on a blank turn and river, you will be faced with calling roughly 80bb more into a pot that grew from 40bb. At 200bb, the stacks behind after the flop raise are ~184bb -- your opponent has room to represent AK, KQ, KK, or a flopped set.

Why this is a problem: KJo only beats bluffs and weaker top pairs. When a competent opponent raises the flop for 200bb deep, their range is weighted toward hands that crush you (KK, KQ, AK, K9s, 99, 33). You are making a marginal hand in exactly the spot where marginal hands are most costly. At 100bb, a flop raise might be a fold. At 200bb, even calling once sets up a disastrous turn/river situation.

Compare to 87s: With 87s on a 9♦6♣5♠ flop at 200bb, you have the nut straight. When the money goes in, you are the one with the dominant hand. This is why implied odds hands skyrocket at 200bb.

Shallow-Stacked Adjustments (20-30bb)

  • All pairs and big cards become powerful (one pair is often enough)
  • Going all-in over initial raise is strong when you have ~10x the raise or fewer
  • If a raise puts in >30% of your stack, just go all-in instead (you're pot-committed anyway)
Example: Shoving 25bb with Pocket 9s

Situation: Cash game, 25bb effective stacks. CO raises to 3bb. You are on the BTN with 9♥9♦. Action folds to you.

A 3bb raise is 12% of your 25bb stack. A re-raise to ~8bb would be 32% of your stack -- past the 30% threshold. The correct play is to shove all-in for 25bb.

Why shoving is right: (1) CO must fold hands like ATo, KJs, QTs that have 35-40% equity against 99 but can't profitably call 25bb. (2) When called, you are not in terrible shape -- 99 vs AK is roughly 56/44. (3) If you just call, the SB and BB may squeeze, and you see a flop where an overcard often comes (A or K ~70% of flops). Shoving eliminates this variance and captures fold equity.

EV of calling vs shoving: By just calling 3bb and seeing a flop, you may fold to a c-bet on an ace-high board having invested 3bb for nothing. By shoving, you often win 5.5bb uncontested (the raise + blinds), and when called you have equity to fight back.

2. Position

Lesson: position-2

Why Position Is Vital

  1. Information: See opponent's action before you decide
  2. Pot control: Can always add one more bet (raise on river with nuts)
  3. Equity realization: IP realizes more equity because you see more streets cheaply
Position Hierarchy

BTN > CO > HJ > LJ > BB > SB

Key Rules

  • From early position: play very tightly (strong hands only)
  • From BTN: play substantially wider (43%+ of hands at 100bb)
  • From blinds: positional disadvantage is permanent (flop/turn/river OOP)
  • BB closing action preflop does NOT justify playing any two cards
  • Positional disadvantage grows as stacks get deeper

3. Preflop Bet Sizing

Lesson: preflop-bet-sizing-2
Universal Formula: Pot-Sized Raise \[\text{Pot-Sized Raise} = 3 \times \text{Last Bet} + \text{Dead Money in Pot}\]

Dead money = everything already in the pot excluding the last bet (i.e., the blinds, antes, and any previous callers).

Worked Example: Raising Over 3 Limpers

Situation: 6-max, 100bb effective. You are on the button. The HJ limps (1bb), the CO limps (1bb), and the BTN folds. SB (0.5bb) and BB (1bb) are already in. The action is on you in the button seat.

Formula: \(3 \times 1\text{bb} + (1 + 1 + 0.5 + 1) = 3 + 3.5 = 6.5\text{bb}\)

Raise to 6.5bb. Not 3bb (too small -- limpers get excellent odds to call and dominate you multi-way). Not 20bb (only your best hands get action; speculative limpers correctly fold, so you only get paid when you're crushed).

Why this size? A 6.5bb raise denies the limpers a profitable price to outdraw you, while keeping the pot manageable when they do call. With a hand like A9s, you want to play heads-up or at most 2-way -- not 4-way against random limping ranges that hit unexpected two-pairs and straights.

Worked Example: 3-Betting a Raise + Caller

Situation: 6-max, 100bb effective. CO raises to 4bb. BTN calls. You are in the SB with QQ and want to 3-bet.

Formula (IP version): \(3 \times 4\text{bb} + (4 + 0.5 + 1) = 12 + 5.5 = 17.5\text{bb}\)

OOP adjustment: You are in the SB (out of position for the entire hand), so add ~1 unit (4bb): raise to approximately 21.5bb.

Why bigger from OOP? Playing out of position with QQ multi-way is dangerous -- your positional disadvantage grows with each opponent. The larger 3-bet discourages the BTN from calling (they need excellent odds to continue), and if they do call, the stack-to-pot ratio becomes shallower, partially offsetting your positional disadvantage. If both call 21.5bb, the pot is ~66bb with ~78bb behind -- now any set or top-pair-top-kicker hand can comfortably get all-in on the flop.

Trap: Raising to >30% of Your Stack

Situation: You have 30bb. The BTN raises to 2bb. You have KK in the CO. You consider re-raising to 8bb.

Problem: You've put in 8/30 = 27% of your stack. If BTN shoves for 30bb, you must call 22bb more to win a pot of ~62bb -- roughly 2:1 odds. Since almost every hand wins at least 33% of the time against even the tightest 5-bet range, you are pot-committed and must call. So you gave your opponent the option to fold by not going all-in -- that fold equity is wasted if you're calling a shove anyway.

Correct play: Shove all-in for 30bb. Now your opponent must fold a large portion of their range (they cannot profitably call with hands like A9s or 77), and when called you still have strong equity with KK.

Recommended Sizes (100bb+)

ScenarioIn PositionOut of Position
When folded to3.5bb3.5bb
Over 1 limper5bb5bb
Over N limpers5 + 1bb per extra limper+1bb more
Facing a raise3.3x the raise4.3x the raise
Facing a 3-bet (you raised)~3x the 3-bet~3.5x the 3-bet
Key Sizing Rules
  • As stacks get shallower, all raise sizes decrease
  • If raise puts in >30% of your stack, go all-in instead
  • With ~10x the initial raise or fewer chips, all-in over initial raise is usually best
  • With >10x the initial raise, use a non-all-in re-raise

Exploitative Sizing

  • Against strong players: same size with all hands (don't give away information)
  • Against calling stations: raise bigger with premiums, smaller with speculative hands
  • Against players who fold to big raises: raise big as a bluff over limpers

4. Range Composition

Lesson: preflop-range-composition-2

Three Types of Preflop Ranges

TypeDefinitionWhen Used
LinearBest hands in descending order (AA, KK, QQ, AK, AQ...)Open-raising; 3-betting OOP; vs weak/tight opponents
PolarizedBest hands + bluffs, skipping middle3-betting IP; 4-betting; 5-betting
CondensedMedium-strength hands only (no premiums, no junk)Calling a raise (callers are almost always condensed)

Capped vs Uncapped Ranges

  • Capped: Range missing nut hands (e.g., caller who would have 3-bet all aces)
  • Uncapped: Range contains nut hands
  • Pre-flop caller is usually capped on high-card boards
  • UTG raiser is usually capped on low, connected boards (e.g., 7-6-5)

5. Implied Odds & Reverse Implied Odds

Lesson: implied-odds-and-reverse-implied-odds
Implied Odds Formula \[\text{Implied Odds} = \frac{\text{Remaining Stacks} + \text{Current Pot}}{\text{Amount to Call}}\]

Thresholds for Continuing

Hand TypeRequired Implied OddsNotes
Small pairs (22-66)10:1Make set ~1 in 9 times
Suited connectors & suited aces20:1Need nutted hands to get paid

Key Adjustments

  • In position: Accept slightly worse implied odds (better equity realization)
  • Out of position: Need the full 20:1 for suited connectors
  • You will never fully realize implied odds -- discount accordingly
  • Against maniacs: realize more IO (they'll pay off)
  • Against tight players: realize more IO (they only have premiums to pay you with)
Example: Implied Odds Calculation for Small Pair vs Set-Mining

Situation: $1/$2 cash game, 6-max. You are on the Button with \(5\heartsuit 5\spadesuit\). The UTG+1 raises to $6. Action folds to you.

Calculating implied odds: Stack behind UTG+1: $195 (97.5bb). Stack behind you: $200. Effective stack: $195. Current pot: $6 + $1 + $2 = $9. You must call $6.

\[\text{Implied Odds} = \frac{\text{Remaining Stacks} + \text{Current Pot}}{\text{Amount to Call}} = \frac{195 + 9}{6} = \frac{204}{6} = 34:1\]

Threshold for pairs: 10:1 required. You have 34:1. Calling $6 with 55 is a very profitable call.

Why the math works: You flop a set approximately 1 in 8.5 times (12%). When you miss, you lose $6. When you hit, you want to win the remaining $195. Expected value of calling:

\[\text{EV} = 0.12 \times \text{(Amount Won when set hits)} - 0.88 \times \$6\]

If you win the full $195 stack when you hit a set: EV = 0.12 x $195 - 0.88 x $6 = $23.40 - $5.28 = +$18.12 per call. Even if you only win 50% of the remaining stack when you hit (you never guarantee full stack extraction): 0.12 x $97.50 - $5.28 = $11.70 - $5.28 = +$6.42 per call. Still highly profitable.

EV cost of folding: Folding 55 here costs you $6.42-$18.12 in expectation every time this spot arises. Over a session where this spot occurs 10 times, that is $64-$181 left on the table. Set-mining is free money against passive opponents who pay off sets.

Contrast with 76s (suited connector, 20:1 required): Same situation, but you hold \(7\diamondsuit 6\diamondsuit\). Implied odds are still 34:1, exceeding the 20:1 threshold. However, 76s hits "nutted hands" (straights and flushes) less often than pairs hit sets. The required 20:1 accounts for this lower hit rate. At 34:1 you still call 76s in position comfortably -- but if the effective stack were only $120 (IO = 21.5:1), 76s becomes borderline while 55 remains a clear call (21.5 > 10).

Reverse Implied Odds

Hands that tend to be dominated when they hit (top pair bad kicker).

  • Offsuit broadways (KJo, QTo, ATo): top pair + opponent money in = often behind
  • Suited hands inherently have better IO than offsuit (can make flushes)
  • As stacks get deeper, reverse implied odds hands get worse
  • As stacks get shallower, reverse implied odds hands get better
Example: Reverse Implied Odds -- KJo in a 3-Bet Pot

Situation: $2/$5 cash game, 100bb effective ($500). You are on the Button and raise to $15 with \(K\diamondsuit J\heartsuit\). The Big Blind 3-bets to $50. You call. Pot: $102. Effective stacks: $450 behind.

Flop: \(K\spadesuit 9\clubsuit 3\diamondsuit\). You have top pair second kicker. Big Blind bets $65 into $102 (64% pot).

The reverse implied odds problem: KJo flopped exactly what it "wanted" -- top pair. But the Big Blind's 3-betting range is heavy with hands that dominate you: AK (makes top pair with ace kicker), KK (makes a set), KQ (makes top pair better kicker), AA (overpair). Against this range, your KJ is behind most of the time when money goes in, and ahead only against bluffs (Axs, K9s-type bluffs), 99 (which you're behind to), and a few weaker holdings.

Equity analysis: KJo vs BB's typical 3-bet range containing AK, KK, QQ, JJ, TT, AQs, and bluffs (A5s, K9s): your equity is approximately 34%. Required to call 3-street committal: roughly 40%+. You are well below threshold. Calling the flop and then facing a large turn bet puts you in an even worse spot. The correct play is call once (gather information) and fold to continued aggression, OR fold immediately.

EV cost of playing too committed with KJo: If you call flop ($65), turn ($130), and river ($200) = $395 invested. Against a range that beats you 66% of the time: EV = 0.34 x $892 - 0.66 x $395 = $303.28 - $260.70 = +$42.58... but this assumes you win 34% of the time, which you don't -- you fold many turns and rivers when scared. In practice, calling three streets with KJo in this spot loses money because you fold to pressure on a percentage of streets. The play leaks approximately $80-120 vs an optimal player.

6. Minimum Defense Frequency (MDF)

Lesson: minimum-defense-frequency-2
Formula \[\text{MDF} = 1 - \frac{\text{Opponent's Bet}}{\text{Opponent's Bet} + \text{Pot}}\]

Key Points

  • MDF tells you the minimum % of hands to continue with to prevent opponent from auto-profiting with bluffs
  • Before the flop: strive to over-defend compared to MDF
  • MDF is divided among all players remaining in the pot
  • MDF does NOT always apply post-flop (especially OOP deep-stacked where you under-realize equity)
  • Typical MDF facing a 3-bet: ~30-40%
Worked Example: MDF vs a 3-Bet

Situation: 6-max, 100bb effective. You raise to 3bb from the Lojack. The Hijack 3-bets to 10bb. It folds to you.

Calculating MDF: The pot before the 3-bet contains your 3bb + the SB (0.5bb) + the BB (1bb) = 4.5bb. The HJ's 3-bet is 10bb. MDF = 1 - 10/(10 + 4.5) = 1 - 10/14.5 = 1 - 0.69 = 31%.

What this means: You need to continue with at least 31% of your opening range to prevent the HJ from auto-profiting with any two cards. If you opened ~17% of hands from LJ, 31% of that means continuing with roughly the top 5.3% of all hands. In practice: 4-bet for value with AA, KK, QQ, AKs, AKo; 4-bet bluff with A5s or K9s; call with pairs like JJ-77, suited connectors like JTs and T9s, and suited aces like AJs.

Important caveat: Before the flop, you should strive to over-defend compared to MDF, because preflop equity runs so close that folding too much is a bigger leak than calling slightly too wide.

9. When Your Raise Gets 3-Bet (100bb)

Lesson: when-your-raise-gets-3bet

Default Strategy

  • 4-bet with: Premium hands (AA, KK, QQ, AKs, AKo for value) + blocker bluffs (A5s, K9s)
  • Call with: Hands that flop well (suited connectors, medium pairs, suited aces)
  • Fold: Offsuit broadways (AJo, KQo, KJo) -- reverse implied odds

Position Matters

  • In position: Call more, 4-bet less (realize equity better)
  • Out of position: Fold more, 4-bet bluff more (can't realize equity well by calling)
Implementable 4-Bet Ranges (vs CO 3-bet)

Situation: You open-raise from the Hijack to 3.5bb at 100bb effective. The Cutoff 3-bets to ~10bb. Everyone else folds. You are in position for the rest of the hand.

  • 4-bet for value (to ~22bb): AA, KK, QQ, JJ, TT, AKs, AKo -- these hands have enough equity to happily get it in if villain 5-bet shoves. With JJ and TT you 4-bet and call a 5-bet jam because at 100bb you need ~38% equity and these hands have ~40%+ vs a reasonable 5-bet range.
  • 4-bet as bluff (to ~22bb, fold to 5-bet): AQo (blocks AA and QQ, fold to 5-bet jam since AQo only has ~34% vs a tight 5-bet range), A5s (blocks AA, has good post-flop playability if called, wheel potential), K9s (blocks KK, unblocks opponent's folding range like AJs, ATs). These bluffs fold to a 5-bet and accept losing the 22bb.
  • Call (see a flop in position): 99-22 (set-mining, need ~10:1 implied odds -- calling 10bb to potentially win 100bb = 10:1), 76s-JTs (suited connectors with excellent post-flop playability), AJs and KQs (strong but not enough to 4-bet for value), A4s-A3s (nut flush potential, cheap call)
  • Fold: AJo, KQo, KJo (offsuit broadways with reverse implied odds -- when they hit top pair they're often dominated by AK, AQ in villain's range), weak kings like K7s-K5s (dominated by AK in 3-bet range)

10. When You Call and Get 3-Bet (100bb)

Lesson: when-you-call-and-get-3bet

Decision Factors

  1. Are you closing the action? If yes, call more freely
  2. How many players? More players = better for IO hands
  3. 3-bet size? Bigger = fold more; smaller = call more
  4. Risk of back 4-bet? If someone can still 4-bet behind, fold weaker IO hands

Default Approach

  • Your calling range is mostly implied odds hands -- you generally want to see a flop
  • With pairs, suited connectors, suited aces: call (unless 3-bet is very large)
  • With offsuit broadways (KQo, AJo): fold unless getting excellent odds heads-up

Lesson: facing-limpers

TypeStrategy Against
Tricky (limp with premiums to trap)Limp behind with IO hands; raise offsuit broadways (fold to limp-reraise)

Warning: Limp-Reraise

A limp-reraise from a tricky limper almost always means the nuts (AA, KK). Do not call with IO hands if you might get back-raised before seeing the flop.

13. Facing a 4-Bet (100bb)

Lesson: facing-a-4bet
Key Insight

Most players 4-bet way too tightly and way too large. Against most opponents: no calling range at 100bb (fold or 5-bet). 5-bet only: AA, KK (maybe QQ, AKs).

Common Mistake
  • AQo facing a 4-bet: fold (even IP in most games)
  • King-X suited: bad -- dominated by AK in 4-bet range
  • Big offsuit hands: fold

14. 200bb Deep Adjustments -- Summary

Universal Principle
  • Implied odds hands UP: Suited aces, suited connectors, pairs
  • Reverse implied odds hands DOWN: Offsuit broadways, unimproved pairs

When Facing a 3-Bet (200bb)

  • IP: Call much wider -- 5-4s, pocket 2s, Q8s, 97s all become playable
  • OOP: 4-bet bluffs come from suited connectors (87s, 76s) more often
  • More linear 3-betting OOP (hands with post-flop playability)
  • King-X suited still bad vs 4-bets (dominated by AK)

Facing a 4-Bet (200bb)

  • Call more IP with IO hands (getting 10-12:1 implied odds)
  • 5-bet very rarely: only AA + a few Axs blocker bluffs
  • Pairs: can usually call in position

Quick-Reference Decision Trees

Everyone Folds to You

Folded to you?
  +-- Good hand (in range for position)? -> RAISE to 3.5bb
  +-- Bad hand? -> FOLD (never open-limp)

Someone Raised

Someone raised -> Check your position
  +-- EP/MP (not BTN): -> 3-bet or fold only (no calling with rake)
  +-- BTN: -> 3-bet strong/bluffs + call IO hands
  +-- SB: -> 3-bet or fold (worst relative position)
  +-- BB: -> 3-bet linear + call wide (closing action)

Your Raise Gets 3-Bet

You raised, got 3-bet
  +-- In position? -> Call wider (pairs, suited connectors, suited aces)
  +-- Out of position? -> 4-bet or fold (less calling)
  +-- Big offsuit hand (AJo, KQo)? -> FOLD (reverse IO)
  +-- Small pair? -> CALL (set-mining)

Facing Limpers

Facing limpers
  +-- First limper tricky? -> Limp behind IO hands; raise big offsuit for value
  +-- First limper straightforward? -> Raise normal range; limp behind IO hands
  +-- Multiple limpers, all straightforward? -> Big raise strong hands; bluff occasionally

Common Errors to Avoid

Top 10 Preflop Mistakes
  1. Open-limping when folded to you (especially with rake)
  2. Calling too wide from the blinds with offsuit junk (K9o, Q8o, 87o)
  3. Calling 3-bets with offsuit broadways (AJo, KQo) -- reverse implied odds
  4. Not 3-betting enough from SB (playing 3-way OOP is a disaster)
  5. Copying small-stakes players' sizing (raising to 8bb because "they'll call anything")
  6. Attacking tricky limpers who limp-reraise with the nuts
  7. Never bluffing over limpers (miss free pots)
  8. Calling cold 4-bets at 100bb (just 5-bet or fold)
  9. 4-betting King-X suited facing a 4-bet (dominated by AK)
  10. Not adjusting for stack depth (playing 200bb strategy at 100bb or vice versa)
