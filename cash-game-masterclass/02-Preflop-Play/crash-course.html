1. Stack Depth

You always play the effective stack -- the shortest stack in the hand. This course primarily covers 100bb play, with adjustments for 200bb.

Definition: Effective Stack
The effective stack is the smaller of your stack and your opponent's stack. If you have 500bb and they have 25bb, you are playing 25bb poker.
Key Insight
Deep stacked (200bb+): Suited aces, suited connectors, and small pairs go up in value (big implied odds). Offsuit broadways (KJo, QTo) and unimproved pocket pairs go down in value.

Shallow stacked (20-30bb): All pairs and big cards become powerful. Top pair is often good enough to stack off happily.
Common Trap
Playing deep-stacked as if you were at 100bb. If you have KJo and make top pair when 200bb deep, and your opponent wants to put all the money in -- you are in serious trouble. Deep stacks demand nutted hands.

2. Position

Position determines how many hands you can profitably play. Being last to act gives you information and pot control.

PositionAdvantageRough RFI %
BTNBest -- always last post-flop~43%
COGreat -- only BTN behind~28%
HJDecent~21%
LJ (UTG 6-max)Worst of openers~17%
BBClosing action preflop onlyDefend ~40-65%
SBWorst -- OOP entire hand~43% (raise/fold)
Common Trap
Thinking BB closing action preflop justifies playing any two cards. You are OOP on every post-flop street. Against a 3-4bb raise, you are NOT getting good enough odds to play 72o.

3. Bet Sizing

Definition: Pot-Sized Raise Formula
$$\text{Pot-Sized Raise} = 3 \times \text{Last Bet} + \text{Dead Money}$$ Dead money = everything already in the pot excluding the last bet.
Worked Example: Facing 3 Limpers
Three players limp for 1bb each. You want to raise.
$3 \times 1\text{bb} + (1 + 1 + 0.5 + 1) = 3 + 3.5 = 6.5\text{bb}$
Raise to 6.5bb. Not 3bb. Not 20bb.
Worked Example: 3-Betting
Opponent raises to 4bb, one caller. You want to 3-bet in position.
$3 \times 4\text{bb} + (4 + 0.5 + 1) = 12 + 5.5 = 17.5\text{bb}$
Add ~1 unit (4bb) if you are out of position: ~21.5bb.

Quick-Reference Sizes (100bb+)

SituationIP SizeOOP Size
Folded to you3-3.5bb
Over 1 limper~5bb
Over N limpers5 + 1bb per extra limper
3-bet vs raise3.3x raise4.3x raise
4-bet vs 3-bet~3x 3-bet~3.5x 3-bet
Key Insight
If your raise puts in >30% of your stack, just go all-in. You are pot-committed anyway (getting ~2:1 odds if opponent shoves).

4. Range Composition

Definition: Linear Range
The best hands in descending order. Used when open-raising and when 3-betting OOP. Example: AA, KK, QQ, AK, AQ, JJ, KQ, AJ...
Definition: Polarized Range
The best hands (for value) + weak hands (as bluffs), skipping medium hands. Used when 3-betting IP and 4-betting. Example: AA, KK, AKs for value + A5s, K9s as bluffs.
Definition: Condensed Range
Medium-strength hands only -- no premiums and no junk. This is what callers almost always have. Callers would have 3-bet their best hands, so their range is capped.
Key Insight: Capped vs Uncapped
A caller's range is capped (missing nut hands they would have re-raised). On broadway-heavy boards (K-Q-7), the raiser is uncapped and has a huge advantage. On low connected boards (7-6-5), the caller is uncapped with all the straights and sets the raiser doesn't have.

5. Implied Odds & Reverse Implied Odds

Definition: Implied Odds
$$\text{Implied Odds} = \frac{\text{Remaining Stacks} + \text{Current Pot}}{\text{Call Amount}}$$ How much you can potentially win relative to what you must invest.
Worked Example
Opponent raises to 3bb (100bb effective). You're on BTN calling 3bb.
Pot after call = 7.5bb. Remaining stacks = 97bb.
$\text{IO} = \frac{97 + 7.5}{3} = 34.8:1$
More than enough for any speculative hand.

Continuation Thresholds

Hand TypeRequired IOHit Rate
Small pairs (22-66)10:1Flop set ~1 in 8.5
Suited connectors / suited aces20:1Need nut hands to get paid
Key Insight: Position Discount
In position: Accept slightly worse implied odds (you realize equity better).
Out of position: Need the full 20:1 for suited connectors. This is why OOP you fold suited aces to a 3-bet but IP you call.
Definition: Reverse Implied Odds
Hands that are often dominated when they hit. KJo makes top pair, but if opponent puts in lots of money, you're usually behind. Suited hands have better IO because they can make flushes. Offsuit hands have reverse IO.
Common Trap: Offsuit Broadways Deep
Playing KTo, QTo, ATo deep-stacked and then making top pair -- your opponent only puts in big money when they have you beat. These hands should be the first to fold to 3-bets, not the last.

6. Minimum Defense Frequency

$$\text{MDF} = 1 - \frac{\text{Opponent's Bet}}{\text{Opponent's Bet} + \text{Pot}}$$
Worked Example
You raise to 3bb from LJ. HJ 3-bets to 10bb.
$\text{MDF} = 1 - \frac{10}{10 + 3 + 0.5 + 1} = 1 - \frac{10}{14.5} = 31\%$
You must continue with at least 31% of your range.

Key caveats:

  • Preflop: strive to over-defend compared to MDF
  • MDF is divided among all remaining players -- multi-way, each player defends less
  • MDF does NOT always apply post-flop (OOP you under-realize equity)
  • Against a strong range, fold more than MDF suggests. Against a weak range, defend wider.

Key Insight
Never open-limp. When folded to you, raise or fold. Limping with rake costs you money -- the house takes a cut of every pot you see. By raising, sometimes everyone folds and you win the blinds rake-free.

8. Facing a Raise

From HJ/CO (not BTN or blinds): 3-bet or fold

With rake and competent opponents, there is no profitable calling range from middle positions. If you call, the BTN can overcall, someone can squeeze, and you're stuck OOP multi-way. Instead: 3-bet a strong linear range or fold.

From BTN: 3-bet + call

The button is the main position where you develop a calling range. You're closing the action and will be IP throughout the hand. Call with implied odds hands (pairs, suited connectors, suited aces). 3-bet with premiums + blocker bluffs (AJo, KQo as bluffs).

From SB: 3-bet or fold (almost always)

Worst relative position in the game. If you call, BB gets good odds to come along, making it 3-way with you first to act on every street. 3-bet to win the pot or get heads-up.

From BB: 3-bet linear + call wide

You're closing the action and getting good odds. 3-bet with a strong linear range (not polarized -- your opponent is incentivized to call you because they have position). Call with suited connectors, suited broadways, pairs, and broadway offsuit.

Common Trap: Calling Offsuit Junk on the Button
T9o, 98o, 87o on the button facing a raise = torching money. Suited versions are fine because they can make flushes. Offsuit versions cannot and have reverse implied odds.

9. When Your Raise Gets 3-Bet

In Position

  • 4-bet for value: AA, KK, QQ, JJ (sometimes TT), AKs, AKo
  • 4-bet as bluff: AQo (fold to 5-bet), A5s, K9s (blockers)
  • Call: Pairs 22-99, suited connectors (76s-JTs), suited aces, KQs, AJs
  • Fold: AJo, KQo, KJo, ATo, all weak offsuit hands

Out of Position

  • Call less, 4-bet bluff more (can't realize equity by calling OOP)
  • Fold suited connectors and suited aces that you'd call IP
  • Fold more dominated hands -- they under-realize equity OOP
Key Insight
76s is often better to call than QJs when facing a 3-bet. Why? 76s is far less likely to be dominated. QJs runs into AQ, KQ, AJ, KJ constantly. When 76s makes a hand (straight, flush), it's usually the nuts.

10. Facing Limpers

Two Types of Limpers

TypeTellYour Strategy
TrickyLimps rarely, especially from EPLimp behind with IO hands. Raise big offsuit for thin value (fold to limp-reraise).
StraightforwardLimps frequently, would have raised good handsRaise your normal range. C-bet frequently post-flop.
Common Trap: Raising Tricky Limpers with 88
If the tricky limper limp-reraises, you're in a disaster. They almost certainly have AA or KK. Even with 10:1 implied odds, if there's a risk of further action before the flop (back-raises), your implied odds evaporate because you might not see a flop.
Worked Example: Bluffing Over Limpers
HJ limps (straightforward), CO limps, BTN limps, SB limps, you're in BB with J2o.
Normally: check and lose. But if you know all limpers are straightforward with weak hands, raise to 12-14bb. They all fold. You collect 4-5bb for free. Don't do this every orbit -- once every few hours is plenty.

11. Cold 4-Bets & Facing 4-Bets

Cold 4-Betting (Raise + 3-bet before you act)

At 100bb: 4-bet or fold only. No calling range. If you call, the initial raiser will 4-bet often enough to destroy your implied odds.

ScenarioGet It InBluff (fold to 5-bet)
EP raise + EP 3-betQQ+, AKKQs
Late raise + late 3-betJJ+, AK, AQsAQo, KJs, A5s

Facing a 4-Bet

Most opponents 4-bet way too tightly. Exploitatively: have no calling range at 100bb. Just 5-bet AA-KK or fold.

Key Insight
AQo facing a 4-bet = fold. Even in position. This is one of the biggest leaks in small-stakes games -- people cannot release AQ to heavy action. If they want to 4-bet you, let them have it.

12. 200bb Deep Adjustments

Everything from 100bb applies, with these modifications:

Hands That Go Up

  • Suited aces (A2s-A9s)
  • Suited connectors (54s-JTs, plus gappers like 86s, T8s)
  • Small/medium pairs

Hands That Go Down

  • Offsuit broadways (KJo, QTo, ATo)
  • Unimproved overpairs (hard to play for 200bb)
  • KXs facing 4-bets (dominated by AK)

Strategy Shifts

  • BTN RFI: ~46% (vs ~41% at 100bb), adding suited connectors and gappers
  • SB RFI: slightly fewer hands (positional disadvantage bigger when deep)
  • Facing 3-bets IP: call much wider -- 54s, 22, Q8s all become playable
  • 4-bet bluffs: come from suited connectors (87s, 76s) instead of just blockers
  • Facing 4-bets: more calling IP (getting good IO), but KXs still bad
  • 5-betting: only AA + a few Axs blocker bluffs
Key Insight
The deeper you go, the more linear your 3-betting becomes from OOP. At 100bb you 3-bet polarized from the CO. At 200bb you 3-bet more linearly with hands that have post-flop playability (suited aces, good suited connectors), because your opponent is more incentivized to call.
