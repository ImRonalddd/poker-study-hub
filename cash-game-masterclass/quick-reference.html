Foundations

Key Formulas

Pot Odds
Call / (Pot + Call)
E.g., call $50 into $150 pot = 50/200 = 25%. You need 25%+ equity to call.
Equity
Wins / (Wins + Losses)
Your share of the pot if all remaining cards were dealt out.
Expected Value (EV)
(Win% x WinAmt) - (Lose% x LoseAmt)
Positive = profitable long-run. Always compare EV of all options.
Minimum Defense Frequency
1 - Bet / (Pot + Bet)
How often you must continue to prevent opponent from profiting with any two cards.
Stack-to-Pot Ratio (SPR)
Effective Stack / Pot on Flop
Low SPR (<4): commit with top pair. High SPR (>10): need strong hands or draws.
Combinations
Pairs: 6 combos | Suited: 4 | Offsuit: 12
Remove combos for known/dead cards. Blockers matter most on later streets.

Position-Based Strategy

Position Open-Raise Range Key Adjustments
UTG / UTG+1 ~12-15% — Premium pairs, strong broadways, suited aces Tightest range. Fold marginal hands. Must play well OOP postflop.
MP / LJ ~16-20% — Add suited connectors, more broadways Slightly wider. Still cautious — multiple players left to act.
HJ ~22-26% — Add suited one-gappers, lower pocket pairs Transition zone. Start stealing but respect CO/BTN 3-bets.
CO ~28-33% — Wide range including suited Kx, suited connectors Premium steal position. 3-bet wider vs. earlier opens.
BTN ~40-50% — Very wide. Most hands with playability Widest open. Guaranteed position postflop. Steal aggressively.
SB ~35-45% (vs. folds to you) or limp/raise range Raise or fold vs. limps. Against opens: tight 3-bet or fold. Worst postflop position.
BB Defend ~40-55% vs. opens (getting best pot odds) Close pot odds justify wide defense. Check-raise strong hands and draws.

3-Bet Guidelines

  • Value 3-bets: QQ+, AKs, AKo (always), JJ, AQs (usually)
  • Bluff 3-bets: A2s-A5s (blocker + nut potential), suited connectors from position
  • 3-bet sizing: 3x IP, 4x OOP, +1x per caller
  • 4-bet range: KK+/AKs for value; A5s/A4s as bluffs (use sparingly)
Flop

Board Texture Classification

Texture Example C-Bet Strategy
Dry / Static K-7-2 rainbow Small c-bet (25-33%), high frequency. Board changes little on turn. Range advantage usually with raiser.
Semi-Wet Q-J-5 one suit Medium c-bet (50-66%), medium frequency. Some draws possible. Protect equity more.
Wet / Dynamic 9-8-7 two-tone Polarized: large bet (66-75%) or check. Many draws and two-pair combos. Board changes drastically.
Monotone T-6-3 all spades Check often unless you have the nut flush draw. Caller's range has many flush draws too.
Paired J-J-4 rainbow Small c-bet, high frequency. Few trips in anyone's range. Overcards dominate.
High / Broadway A-K-Q rainbow Check more often — both ranges connect. Aggressor advantage is smaller on these boards.
Low / Disconnected 7-3-2 rainbow Small c-bet, very high frequency. Misses caller's range almost entirely. Pure range bet.

Range Advantage vs. Nut Advantage

  • Range advantage: Your overall range is stronger (more high cards, overpairs). Use small bets at high frequency.
  • Nut advantage: You have more of the very strongest hands (sets, two-pair, straights). Use larger bets, more polarized.
  • The preflop aggressor typically has range advantage on A/K-high boards. The caller may have nut advantage on low, connected boards.
Turn

Turn Strategy Essentials

Card Categories for Turn Decisions

Turn Card Type Effect Adjustment
Brick / Blank Changes nothing — no draws complete, no new draws Continue previous street's plan. Barrel for value or as a bluff if you c-bet flop.
Draw-completing Flush or straight completes If you have it, bet for value. If not, consider checking — opponent may have gotten there.
Overcard New highest card appears Good barrel card for aggressor (represents new top pair). Bad for caller holding middle pair.
Pairing card Pairs the board Reduces straights/flushes. Good for overpairs. Check if opponent called with that card's pair.

Delayed C-Bet

  • Check flop, bet turn. Use on boards where flop checking is standard (wet boards, bad range advantage).
  • Effective when turn improves your range (e.g., overcard to board that hits your checking range).
  • Size: typically 50-66% pot. Represents strength since you chose to check and then bet.
River

River Decision Framework

The River Checklist

  1. Narrow opponent's range — What hands called/raised each street? What folded?
  2. Categorize your hand — Is it value, bluff-catcher, or clear bluff?
  3. Choose an action — Value bet (if called by worse 50%+), check (bluff-catcher), or bluff (if opponent folds enough).

Bluff-to-Value Ratio

Bet Size (% Pot) Opponent Must Defend Your Bluff Frequency
33% 75% ~20% bluffs (1 bluff per 4 value bets)
50% 67% ~25% bluffs (1 bluff per 3 value bets)
66% 60% ~29% bluffs (2 bluffs per 5 value bets)
75% 57% ~30% bluffs (3 bluffs per 7 value bets)
100% 50% ~33% bluffs (1 bluff per 2 value bets)

Bluff Catching Rules

  • If you beat zero value hands but lose to zero bluff hands, you are a pure bluff-catcher.
  • Call if you think opponent bluffs more than the required frequency (based on pot odds).
  • Good bluff-catchers: hands that block opponent's value (e.g., holding a flush blocker).
  • Bad bluff-catchers: hands that block opponent's bluffs (e.g., holding missed draw cards they would bluff).
All Streets

Bet Sizing Guidelines

Size % of Pot When to Use
Small 25-33% Dry boards with range advantage. Range c-bets. When you want few folds (thin value).
Medium 50-66% Standard sizing for most spots. Semi-wet boards. Turn barrels. Balanced default.
Large 75-100% Wet/dynamic boards. When you want to charge draws. Polarized ranges. River value/bluffs.
Overbet 120-200%+ Nut advantage spots. When opponent's range is capped. River polarization. Requires careful balance.

Sizing Principles

  • Bigger when polarized — nuts or air. You want max value from strong hands and max fold equity with bluffs.
  • Smaller when merged — many medium-strength hands. You want calls from worse but can't handle raises.
  • Match your sizing to your range, not just your hand. If your range is polarized, bet big with your whole range.
  • Increase sizing with more streets left — pot geometry means small flop bets grow into big river pots.
Summary

Chapter-by-Chapter Key Takeaways

Ch 1 — Introduction

  • Every poker decision is an EV calculation. If the math says call, call — regardless of results.
  • Pot odds tell you the minimum equity needed. Compare pot odds to hand equity before every call.
  • GTO is the baseline strategy that cannot be exploited. Deviate from GTO only when you have a read.

Ch 2 — Preflop Play

  • Position is the single most important factor in preflop decisions. Play tighter OOP, wider IP.
  • Lock in your ranges and play them consistently. Unpredictable deviations cost money.
  • 3-bet for value and as a bluff. A linear 3-bet range works at low stakes; polarized at higher stakes.

Ch 3 — Playing the Flop

  • C-bet strategy depends on board texture and range advantage, not your specific hand.
  • Small bets at high frequency on dry boards. Larger bets at lower frequency on wet boards.
  • When you check, have a plan: check-call, check-raise, or check-fold based on your hand category.

Ch 4 — Playing the Turn

  • The turn is where the pot gets big. Only barrel when your range supports it.
  • Board runout matters enormously. Re-evaluate your range advantage on every new card.
  • Delayed c-bets are a powerful weapon — especially when the turn card favors your range.

Ch 5 — Playing the River

  • The river is pure math. No more draws — you either have the best hand or you don't.
  • Value bet thin when you beat more than 50% of your opponent's calling range.
  • Choose bluffs with good blocker properties: block opponent's calls, unblock their folds.

Ch 6 — Additional Topics

  • Shorthanded: widen all ranges proportionally. Aggression and positional awareness increase.
  • Straddle pots play like lower-SPR poker — tighten up slightly and commit with less relative strength.
  • Live tells and timing are extra information layers. Use them to deviate from GTO.

Ch 7 — Hand Reviews

  • Walk through each street asking: what is my range, what is their range, what changed?
  • Focus on process, not results. A good decision with a bad outcome is still a good decision.
  • Review your own hands regularly. Study where you deviated from your plan and why.
Avoid

Common Mistakes

  • X Playing too many hands preflop. Entering with marginal hands OOP is the most expensive leak. Tight is right, especially from early position.
  • X C-betting every flop. Not all boards favor the aggressor. Check boards where the caller has nut or range advantage (e.g., low connected boards vs. BB).
  • X Using the same bet size for every situation. Your sizing communicates your range. Match size to board texture and your strategic goal.
  • X Ignoring position postflop. Being IP lets you control pot size, realize equity, and make better decisions. Value it heavily.
  • X Calling river bets with bluff-catchers too often. Most low-stakes players underbluff on the river. Fold your marginal hands unless you have a specific read.
  • X Never bluff-raising. If your range is always value-heavy when you raise, observant opponents will overfold. Include some bluffs with good blockers.
  • X Failing to adjust to opponents. GTO is a baseline, not a religion. When villains have clear tendencies, exploit them.
  • X Chasing draws without odds. Always compare implied odds and pot odds to your equity. Calling "because it might hit" loses money.
  • X Playing results-oriented. A bad beat does not make the call wrong. Evaluate decisions on process and expected value, not single outcomes.
  • X Not reviewing your sessions. You cannot improve what you do not examine. Mark interesting hands, review them away from the table.
