Hand Review #1

Hand 1: A7s -- Nut Flush on Monotone Board

Full Situation and Key Takeaways

Setup: $1/$1/$3 live cash game, 100bb effective stacks (~$300). You are on the Button holding \(A\clubsuit 7\clubsuit\). UTG limps, MP limps, CO calls, and you call. Both blinds complete. 5-way pot. Pot: $15.

Flop: \(K\clubsuit 9\clubsuit 3\clubsuit\) -- rainbow of clubs. You flopped the nut flush. One opponent is visibly stuck (down $400 in a $300 game, playing frustrated). UTG leads for $10 into $15. Action folds to you on the Button.

  1. Bet small with the nuts multiway: Just call $10. Do NOT raise yet. The key question is "how much will a J♣ with a random card call?" In a 1/1/3 game, an opponent with any club calls $10-20 every time without hesitation. But they might fold to $80. Calling the small donk also keeps the tilting player in -- if you raise, the sticky opener might fold and the tilting player (who only called preflop) might also fold. Just call. Pot: $35. Effective stacks: $280 behind. The UTG is committed now; let them dig deeper.
  2. When bet into again on turn, call again: Turn brings \(2\diamondsuit\) -- a brick. UTG bets $25 into $35. Just call again. Pot: $85. Effective stacks: $255 behind. Your nut flush is invulnerable. Every caller on the flop with K-x, 9-x, 3-x, or any club is drawing dead or drawing to two outs at best. You are extracting maximum value by keeping all their money in the pot.
  3. Against stuck opponents, bet small on the river: River brings \(J\spadesuit\). UTG checks. Bet $50-70 into the $85 pot -- about 65-80% pot. This is "small" relative to the stacks. The tilting opponent has been calling $10 and $25. A $70 bet gets snap-called by anything with a club and frustrated hero-calls from pairs. A $250 all-in folds everything except a better flush (impossible -- you have the nuts) and a well-timed fold from their side. Give them the sizing they cannot resist. EV of $70 vs $250 bet: If two players each call $70, you win $140. If they both fold a pot-sized shove, you win $0 on top of the $85 pot. Clearly $70 extracts more.
  4. Pot odds for hero calls: If the tilting opponent check-raises you on the river to $200 into the $85 pot (a donk-raise): you call $130 to win ($85 + $200 + $130) = $415 total. Required equity: 130/415 = 31.3%. You have the nut flush -- you win 100% of the time against their range. This is an automatic call. EV = $285. But the correct action is to re-raise all-in: $255 more. Their range is all flushes below yours (which lose) plus random bluffs. Either they fold or they pay you $255 more. Re-raising maximizes value once they are committed.

EV cost of raising the flop: If you raise to $50 on the flop, most opponents fold their weak clubs (J-high, T-high flush draws). You collect $35 instead of potentially $250+ over three streets. Slow-playing with the nuts multiway extracts 3-5x more value in this spot.

"Whenever you have someone who is stuck piles, you want to do everything you can to give them a chance to make a blunder."

Hand 2: AQs -- 3-Bet Pot on T-6-4

Full Situation and Key Takeaways

Setup: $1/$1/$3 live cash game, 100bb effective ($300). You are in the Cutoff holding \(A\heartsuit Q\heartsuit\). UTG opens to $10. MP calls. You 3-bet to $32 in position. UTG calls, MP calls. 3-way pot. Pot: $97. Effective stacks: ~$268 behind.

Flop: \(T\diamondsuit 6\clubsuit 4\spadesuit\) -- connected, two low cards. SPR: $268/$97 ≈ 2.75. Both opponents check to you. You have two overcards and a backdoor flush draw. Nothing else.

  1. Bet small (25-33% pot), not big: Bet $25 into $97 (26%). Why small? Your hand is AQ-high -- you want to apply pressure without committing too much. More importantly, in a 3-bet pot with an SPR of 2.75, a small bet still puts enormous pressure on their ranges. UTG and MP both have hands like KJ, KQ, 87, 98, 55, 77 -- none of which connect well with T-6-4. Your 3-bet range has AA, KK, QQ, JJ, TT (which hit this board). Your small bet simulates the full 3-bet range, not just your AQ overcards. A big bet is wasteful -- it folds marginal hands but doesn't extract more from the hands that call anyway.
  2. If check-raised: FOLD. If UTG check-raises from $25 to $80, and MP calls, you are facing a bet-raise-call sequence on T-6-4. The check-raiser almost certainly has a set (TT, 66, 44), two pair (T6, T4, 64), or a strong draw (87, 75). MP calling the raise means they have their own strong hand or draw. Your AQ has approximately 22% equity against two decent hands. You need (cost to call) / (total pot after call) equity. Call: $55 to win ($97 + $25 + $80 + $80 + $55) = $337. Required: 55/337 = 16.3%. Your 22% equity barely justifies the call -- and only if both opponents are bluffing at some frequency. Against two opponents on a connected board, neither is bluffing. Fold the AQ.
  3. Turn 5 (making 3-4-5-6-7 straight possible): Even if your flop c-bet was called and no one raised, when the turn brings a 5, do not barrel. The 5 completes 7-8 for a straight (hands both callers could have) and gives 3-7 a straight too. Your AQ hasn't improved and the board is now extremely draw-heavy. Check back and evaluate. Bluffing into two callers on a completed straight board with AQ is costly.
  4. River pot odds for a call: If somehow heads-up at the river and facing a half-pot bet with AQ-high (pure bluff-catcher): need to be good (1/pot_size) / (1 + 1/pot_size). Example: pot $150, bet $75. Need to win 75/300 = 25% of the time. AQ beats all bluffs and some air. If you estimate opponent bluffs 35% of the time in this spot, calling is profitable. EV of call = 0.35 x $225 - 0.65 x $75 = $78.75 - $48.75 = +$30. The "getting good odds" principle applies here.

Hand 3: Pocket 5s -- Set Mining Multiway

Key Takeaways
  1. Call preflop with pairs in position: Especially when you don't expect 3-bets.
  2. When an opponent leads small with a set, JUST CALL: Keep other players in. Don't raise and isolate.
  3. Sometimes the button raises for you: Let others build the pot, then spring the trap.
  4. Getting set-over-set is part of the game. Don't play scared because of it.
"You need to make sure you're properly bankrolled. Sometimes playing well implies that you're going to get stacked."

Hand 4: Straddle with AA

  • Against straddlers who attack limps: consider limping with premium hands to induce their aggression
  • Default play is still to raise 4-5 big blinds unless you have a specific read
  • Against shallow straddlers (~50bb): getting it all in preflop is perfectly fine

Hand Review #2

Hand 5: Q7s -- Flopping Two Pair in a Limped Pot

Full Situation and Key Takeaways

Setup: $1/$1/$3 live cash game. Multiple limpers to the Button. You limp with \(Q\heartsuit 7\heartsuit\) on the Button. 5-way limped pot. Pot: $15. Effective stacks: $300 (100bb at $3).

Flop: \(Q\clubsuit 7\diamondsuit 3\spadesuit\) -- you flopped top two pair. UTG checks, others check to you.

  1. Limping with IO hands is correct in limped-pot situations: Q7s in the CO or Button against multiple limpers is a limp-behind, not a raise. Why? Raising to $15 from the Button with 5 limpers risks taking down $15 without seeing a flop, but also risks building a big pot out of position with a marginal hand. In limped pots, your implied odds are enormous (deep SPR) -- calling $3 to potentially win $300 is 100:1 implied odds, far exceeding the 20:1 threshold for suited hands. Limping wins you more when you flop two pair or better.
  2. Raise the flop immediately on Q-7-3: Bet $12 into $15. Q7s on Q-7-3 is the near-nuts (only Q7 exactly ties with you, sets beat you). Why raise now after limping? The board is dry but you have two pair -- vulnerable to overcards, running straights, and hearts. Charging draws is critical. Against 4 other limpers, at least one has a queen (Qx with poor kicker) or a seven (7-6s, 7-5s). These pay off a raise all day. A $12 bet into $15 gets called by Q6, Q5, 76, 57, and paired boards. Two callers: pot grows to $51.
  3. Turn and river thin value: Turn brings \(J\heartsuit\) -- a near-blank (puts a possible straight draw but doesn't complete anything). Bet $30 into $51. River: \(4\clubsuit\). Bet $60-80 into $111. Against a calling station who called flop and turn with a queen: they call $80 with QJ, Q9, Q6 -- hands that have two pair now but are still below your Q7. Shove for thin value on the river when you have two pair and are likely best. EV: opponent calls $80 with 60% frequency (calling station). EV of river bet = 0.60 x $80 + 0 = $48. EV of checking = $0. Over 100 rivers: $4,800 extra profit from the thin value bet.
  4. Know your opponent type: Against a calling station (pays off small bets with middle pair), bet small on all three streets and go for thin value. Against a tight nit (folds everything below top pair), bet big on the flop to charge their draws and check back the river (they won't call without a strong hand). Against an aggressive maniac (raises with air), bet small and call their raise -- they're bluffing. Q7s on Q-7-3 plays very differently against each type.

Hand 6: AJs -- Multiway Facing a 3-Bet

Full Situation and Warning

Setup: $1/$1/$3 live cash game, 100bb effective ($300). You raise to $10 from the Cutoff with \(A\heartsuit J\heartsuit\). The Button calls $10. The Small Blind 3-bets to $35. The Big Blind calls $35. Action back to you.

The Situation: FOLD AJs. Before you act, let's count what has already happened: there is a 3-bet (strong range) plus a cold-call (also strong -- Big Blind closing action calls $35, meaning they have a hand). You are facing both a 3-bet and a call. The 3-bettor's range: typically AA, KK, QQ, JJ, AK, AQ, maybe TT in a live 1/1/3 game. The cold-caller in BB has a range that beats or dominates much of AJs (they'd fold marginal hands to a 3-bet + potential squeeze). The Button is still behind you and may also act.

  • AJs facing a 3-bet + cold call = FOLD: Your AJs has approximately 34% equity against just the 3-bettor's range (QQ+, AK, AQ). Against the combined ranges of the 3-bettor AND the cold-caller (who is also strong), AJs has about 26% equity. You need to call $25 more into a pot that will be ~$110. Required equity: 25/135 = 18.5%. You have 26% equity... BUT that is raw equity, not realized equity. OOP in a 3-way pot, your equity realization is maybe 60-70% of raw equity. Effective realized equity: 26% x 0.65 = 16.9% -- BELOW the required 18.5%. This is a fold.
  • Suited broadways are not automatic continues: AJs looks powerful but suffers from classic reverse implied odds. When you hit top pair with the Jack, hands like AK, AQ, AA, KK, QQ all have you crushed. The very hands most likely to be in the 3-bettor's range dominate your top pair. You cannot profitably call here.
  • Why not shove as a bluff? Shoving $300 over a 3-bet and a call makes no sense without blockers. The 3-bettor likely has AA, KK, or AK -- your AJ blocks aces to some degree, but not enough. You need the nuts-blocking power of A5s or KJ for a shove to make sense, not AJ which partially blocks the hands that call you off.

EV cost of calling: Calling $25 and playing 3-way OOP with reverse IO: estimated EV of calling ≈ -$5 to -$8 per occurrence. Multiply by hundreds of similar spots = significant annual leak.

"When someone bets and someone calls, you should be very quick to get out of the way."

Hand 7: Review Your Bluffs Too

Meta-Lesson

When submitting hands for coaching, always include bluffs and losses -- not just big pots won. Selection bias in hand review leads to incomplete feedback.

Hand Review #3

Hand 8: AJ on Button -- Flatting a Laggy Player

Full Situation and Key Takeaways

Setup: $1/$1/$3 live cash game, 100bb effective ($300). You are on the Button holding \(A\spadesuit J\clubsuit\). A laggy player (plays many hands, bets frequently) in the Cutoff raises to $10. You call. Heads-up. Pot: $22. Effective stacks: $290 behind.

Flop: \(J\heartsuit 7\diamondsuit 4\clubsuit\). You have top pair, top kicker. The laggy player bets $7 into $22 (32% pot).

  1. Against laggy opponents who bet small, just CALL (do not raise): A small bet from a laggy player on J-7-4 almost always indicates a marginal made hand -- middle pair (77 or 44), weak top pair (J-9, J-8), or a backdoor draw (K-T, Q-T with overcards). This is NOT a polarized range. If you raise to $25, they fold all their bluffs and marginal hands. If you call $7, they continue bluffing the turn with all their air and backdoor draws. Over three streets, you extract $7 + $20 + $45 = $72 from their bluffing range. If you raise the flop, you extract $7 once and they fold. EV of calling vs raising: approximately +$65 more per occurrence.
  2. Turn play: Turn: \(Q\diamondsuit\). They bet $15 into $36 (42%). Again, call. Their range still contains many weak hands. Q-T just got a pair; K-T has a gutshot; 8-9 is drawing. Just calling keeps their entire range in -- including hands that will bluff big on the river when they miss.
  3. River: \(8\clubsuit\) arrives. This is a bad card. Hands like 9-T just made a straight (J-7-4-Q-8 run-out: the straight is 7-8-9-T-J). Also, 6-9 made a straight. Against a laggy player's preflop range, J-9 (straight) and T-9 (straight) are absolutely in their range. When they now bet $50 into $66 (76%), this size shift from small (32%, 42%) to medium-large (76%) signals they made something. Check behind if you were checked to, or fold to a big bet. The EV cost of calling a large river bet on a straight-completing card with only one pair: if they're strong 70% of the time, EV of call = 0.30 x $116 - 0.70 x $50 = $34.80 - $35 = -$0.20. Barely break-even -- lean toward fold.

Hand 9: A8s -- Trips vs. KK

  • When an opponent leads small, consider raising: it often indicates a marginal hand
  • Don't use bet sizes containing the trip card (if you have trip 8s, don't bet 80 -- bet 90 instead)
  • When opponents jam and you have trips, assess: do you beat overvalued hands? Are bluffs available?

Hand 10: 5-3s -- Two Pair from Cheap Flop

  • Raising with junky suited hands on the button is fine for image
  • When opponents check-call then lead the turn: they usually have something okay
  • Ask "what am I trying to get called by?" when sizing raises

Hand 11: A9 -- Flop Top Two, Get Raised

Key Insight

When shallow-stacked with near-nuts, just jam. Don't try to be fancy with small raises. Running into the 5 combos of sets happens -- feel worse if T7o gets there because you let them stay in cheaply.

Hand 12: Q3s -- Facing Min-Click Reraise

  • When you need to be good only 27% of the time, lean toward calling
  • Min-click reraises from bad players are unpredictable -- don't assume they always mean the nuts
  • Promotions (royal flush, bad beat) change opponent behavior

Hand 13: QQ vs. 3-Bet All-In (KK)

Full Situation and Warning

Setup: $1/$1/$3 live cash game, 100bb effective ($300). You raise to $10 from the Hijack with \(Q\heartsuit Q\spadesuit\). A very tight, passive player in the Big Blind -- someone who has barely played a hand in 3 hours -- 3-bets to $30. This is their first 3-bet of the session. Action folds back to you.

The Situation: This reads as extremely strong. A tight, straightforward player's 3-bet range in a $1/$1/$3 game after 3 hours of passive play could easily be: AA, KK, and literally nothing else. At most: AA, KK, QQ.

  • The default play is call, not shove: Against their range of QQ+ (6 combos of QQ, 6 combos of KK, 6 combos of AA), your QQ has approximately 35% equity (you tie vs QQ, are a small underdog vs KK, and are a massive underdog vs AA). The call of $20 more into $43 pot: required equity = 20/63 = 31.7%. Your actual equity is 35% vs this range -- barely a call. Against QQ+, AK (adding AK): equity rises to 41% -- a clear call.
  • The exploitative fold: If their range is genuinely AA/KK only (highly specific player read), your equity is approximately 18%. Required: 31.7%. This is a fold. Consider folding QQ only when you have a very specific read that their 3-bet range cannot include QQ or AK. Against most opponents, call.
  • Why never shove over their small 4-bet: Suppose you call the 3-bet ($30) and flop comes \(J\spadesuit 7\diamondsuit 2\clubsuit\). They bet $45 all-in into a $63 pot. You call -- you are ahead of JJ and have two outs against KK/AA. But if the flop is \(K\heartsuit 9\clubsuit 3\diamondsuit\) and they bet, you can fold QQ, saving $245. Shoving preflop locks you into the full $300. Calling the 3-bet and folding on scary boards saves your stack in key spots. EV cost of preflop shove vs call: If their range is 60% AA/KK and 40% QQ/AK, shoving preflop has EV = 0.40 x $600 x 0.52 + 0.60 x $600 x 0.18 - $300 = $124.80 + $64.80 - $300 = -$110.40. Calling has significantly better EV because you can escape on bad flops.

Hand Review #4

Hand 14: QQ -- Facing a Straddle Donk-Bet

Full Situation

Setup: $1/$1/$3 live game with a $6 UTG straddle. Effective stacks ~$600 (200bb at the $3 BB, or ~100bb at the $6 straddle). You are in the Hijack holding \(Q\diamondsuit Q\clubsuit\). Action folds to you. You raise to $20. Button folds, Small Blind folds, Big Blind folds. The straddler (a wild, aggressive player) re-raises to $50.

Decision point: The straddler's 3-bet from straddle position is extremely wide -- they are raising because they got to act last preflop and feel entitled to attack. Their range includes all pocket pairs, suited connectors, and many one-gap hands. Against this wide 3-bet range, QQ has approximately 65% equity. Pot odds: call $30 more into $71 pot. Required: 30/101 = 29.7%. You have 65% equity -- overwhelming call, and actually a 4-bet is superior.

4-bet to $120. SPR if they call: $480 effective / ($240 pot) = 2.0. This is key: with SPR 2, any top pair or better gets it in. You are inviting them to shove with hands like A-K, TT, JJ which you dominate. Against their wide range, this is highly profitable. Jamming all $600 preflop folds their bluffs and garbage -- bad outcome. The 4-bet to $120 keeps their wide range in and builds the pot optimally.

  • Against big polarized flop donk-bets, just call and proceed cautiously
  • On safe turns, jam: draws get free equity, jacks call a shove, flush draws fold (which is fine)

Hand 15: AK -- Flush Draw, Check the Turn

Warning

Don't check the turn with the nut flush. Bet to get value from sets, kings, queens, and one-pair hands. Small-stakes opponents rarely bluff into multiple players on the river.

Hand 16: 86c -- Triple Barrel Bluff

Key Insight
  • When bluffing, bet SMALL on the flop to keep ranges wide, then barrel bigger on turns and rivers for maximum fold equity.
  • Big flop bets strengthen opponent's calling range, making subsequent barrels less effective.
  • Set up stack-to-pot ratios for a river jam: plan sizing across all three streets.

Hand 18: AJ -- Check-Raise Missed Opportunity

  • From OOP with one of your best hands, CHECK-RAISE the flop: AJ on A-Q-J is one of your best possible hands
  • Not check-raising lets opponents off the hook -- they'll bet one street and check back

Hand 19: 65 -- Flush Draw, Bet the Flop

  • In limped pots, bet your good draws from OOP -- it's likely to check through otherwise
  • Bet and call a raise with flush draws: you have equity and can improve
  • When you make your hand, go for thin value on the river

Hand 20: KK vs. AK -- Triple Barrel Bluff by the Numbers

Key Insight

When you have no bluffs in your range, take the ones you have. If your hand can't win at showdown and you have some equity/blockers, barrel off. The best bluffs are the ones you "shouldn't" have.

Universal Principles from All Reviews

Bet Sizing Framework

  1. Ask "what am I trying to get called by?" before choosing a size
  2. In small stakes games: weak players call with anything for $20 but start folding at $80
  3. Set up stack-to-pot ratios across streets -- don't end up with awkward remaining stacks

Reading Opponents

Tag Opponents Specifically

"Laggy reg," "action fish," "tight passive" -- not just "fish." Know what type of player you're facing and adjust your strategy accordingly.

The "Gift" Economy of Small Stakes

Small stakes opponents constantly gift you pots through terrible play. This inflates your win rate; don't attribute it to skill alone. As you move up, gifts become rarer, and your win rate drops. This is normal -- game select to find the softest games at every stake.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Top 5 Mistakes from Hand Reviews
  1. Betting too small when trying to get value -- always ask "how much will X call?"
  2. Not check-raising from OOP with premium hands
  3. Shoving over 4-bets with QQ instead of calling and playing postflop
  4. Not folding two-pair multiway when the raise range is heavily weighted toward flushes
  5. Inconsistent raise sizes (min-raising one hand, 5x another) -- can telegraph hand strength
