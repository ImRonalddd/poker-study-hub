Theme 1: Bet Sizing Is Everything

The single most common improvement area across all reviewed hands. The framework is simple:

The Sizing Question
Before every bet, ask: "What am I trying to get called by, and how much will that hand call?"
TargetSizeLogic
Middle pair or drawSmall (1/4 - 1/3 pot)They call any small amount but fold to 2/3 pot
Top pairMedium (1/2 - 3/4 pot)Top pair rarely folds, so charge them
Overpair or betterBig (3/4 pot - pot)They're committed; maximize value
Force a fold (bluff)Set up multi-street sizingSmall flop, medium turn, jam river
Full Situation: A7s Nut Flush — Sizing for Max Value
$1/$1/$3 live cash game, 100bb effective (~$300). Hero on BTN with \(A\clubsuit 7\clubsuit\).

Preflop: Multiple limpers. Hero raises to $20 on the Button. 3 players call. Pot: ~$85.

Flop: \(Q\clubsuit J\clubsuit 4\clubsuit\). Checks to Hero. Hero holds the nut flush.

The question: Bet $50 (~60% pot) or bet $20 (~24% pot)?

Answer: Bet $20. Ask what hands will call and for how much:

  • \(J\clubsuit x\) or \(K\clubsuit x\) (lower flush draws): calls $20 every time, folds to $50 half the time
  • Qx (top pair, no club): calls $20 comfortably, folds to $50 when scared of the flush
  • Anyone with a pair: calls $20, folds $50

Betting $20 into $85 keeps 3 players in. Betting $50 likely gets one caller. Expected callers × call amount = EV:

  • $20 bet, 2-3 callers = $40-60 added to pot → pot grows to $125-145, giving great setup for a larger turn bet
  • $50 bet, 1 caller = $50 added → pot grows to $135, but you've lost 2 players who would've stacked off on the turn

EV cost of overbetting: You fold out 2 drawing opponents who each have ~15% equity but will call 3 streets adding ~$100+ each to your win. Overbetting costs ~$100-200 in long-run EV from this multiway spot.

Example: 5-3s Two Pair
Board: Q-3-2-K. Opponent leads turn and river.

What are you trying to get called by? If mostly kings, go big (~$100). If mostly queens, go smaller (~$60). The proportion of their range that's kings vs. queens determines your sizing.

Common Mistake
Betting amounts that create awkward stack-to-pot ratios. Plan your sizing across all three streets. If you want to jam the river, work backward from that to determine flop and turn bets.

Theme 2: Give Bad Players Room to Blunder

Principle
"Whenever you have someone who is stuck piles, you want to do everything you can to give them a chance to make a blunder."

Small River Bets Induce Action

With the nut flush on a paired board (A7s hand), the river bet should be small ($70-100) rather than all-in. Why?

  • A hand like J♣x may bluff-raise over a small bet but fold to an all-in
  • Random aces or tens may hero-call a small bet but fold to a big one
  • You give tilting opponents maximum room to make a mistake

Against Laggy Players, Just Call

Example: AJ vs. Laggy Player
Board: J-7-6. Laggy opponent bets small on the turn.

Just call. If you raise, they fold all their bluffs. If you call, they continue bluffing the river with Q-T, K-8, random air. Preserve their bluffing range.

You beat maniacs by letting them bluff you. You do NOT beat them by forcing them to play straightforwardly.

Theme 3: Reading Bet Patterns

Small Bets = Marginal Made Hands
When opponents bet 1/4 to 1/3 pot, they almost always have a marginal made hand (middle pair, weak top pair). This is NOT a polarized range. Adjust:
  • Raise for thin value with your medium-strength hands
  • Don't fear the nuts behind this sizing
Big Donk Bets = Polarized
When straddlers or aggressive players bet big into you, their range is polarized: very strong hands and draws. Against this:
  • Call with your medium-strength hands and proceed cautiously
  • Don't raise unless you can handle getting it all in
Abnormal Lines Are Unbalanced
Check-call, check-call, then lead the river? Check-raise the flop small, then check the turn? These unusual sequences are almost always heavily weighted toward one end of the spectrum.

Theme 4: Pot Odds Drive Hero Calls

Multiple hands came down to pot odds calculations:

The Formula
Required equity = Amount to call / (Pot + Amount to call)

If you need $150 to win $800 total: 150/800 = 18.75%. You only need to be right less than 1 in 5 times.

When the math says you need to be good 18-27% of the time, ask:

  1. Could the opponent be overvaluing a worse hand? (Even 10% of the time counts)
  2. Could the opponent be bluffing? (Even occasionally counts)
  3. Are there full house blockers that make the nuts less likely?
When to Override Pot Odds
Fold despite good odds when:
  • Multiple opponents are in the pot (both could have you beat)
  • The opponent's range is almost entirely hands that beat you
  • The opponent is known to be tight, straightforward, and never bluffs

Theme 5: Check-Raising From Out of Position

Full Situation: AJ on A-Q-J — Missed Check-Raise
$1/$1/$3 live cash game, 100bb effective (~$300). Hero in BB with \(A\diamondsuit J\spadesuit\).

Preflop: CO raises to $10. Hero 3-bets to $35 from the BB. CO calls. Pot: $71.

Flop: \(A\clubsuit Q\heartsuit J\diamondsuit\). Hero checks. CO bets $25 (~35% pot). Hero calls. Pot: $121.

Turn: \(4\spadesuit\). Hero checks. CO checks back.

River: \(7\heartsuit\). Hero bets $50. CO calls. Hero wins ~$221.

What went wrong: Hero won the hand but extracted far less than optimal. After 3-betting preflop, Hero's range excludes AA, KK, JJ (4-bet hands) and AK/AQ (likely 4-bet or 4-bet bluff). This means AJ (two pair) and QT (straight) are Hero's absolute best hands on this flop. Hero must check-raise with these.

Why check-raise the flop to $75:

  • CO's $25 small bet into $71 = weak marginal hand (Qx, Jx, KT, maybe AK). They're not betting big with top pair because Hero 3-bet.
  • A call gives CO a free turn to improve or shut down. A check-raise builds the pot with the best hand.
  • Check-raise to $75: pot grows to $221. Turn bet ~$130, pot ~$481. River jam ~$95 remaining. Total extracted: $300 (stacks).
  • Hero's actual line extracted ~$110 in net profit. Check-raise line extracts full stacks: ~$265 net profit.

EV cost of calling instead of check-raising: ~$155 left on the table. Over a session with two such spots: $310 of lost value from passive play with the nuts.

Why Check-Raising Matters
  • If you just call, opponents bet one street and check back — you leave value on the table
  • Check-raising builds the pot geometrically: a $50 check-raise leads to a $150 turn bet leads to a $300+ river bet
  • Without check-raises, your checking range becomes entirely weak hands, making you exploitable
  • In 3-bet pots especially, your OOP range is capped (no 4-bet candidates remain) — your checking range MUST contain some strong hands or opponents exploit you freely

Theme 6: When to Fold Strong Hands

Not every big hand should go to the felt. The reviews highlighted key folding spots:

Fold When
  • Multiway + big raise: A8 two pair facing a pot-sized check-raise with the button still to act. The raiser's range is mostly flushes, and the button could be trapping.
  • Opponent's range is capped to beats-you: When a tight player 4-bets small, their range may be exactly AA/KK/QQ. QQ becomes a fold or a call (not a shove).
  • AJs facing a 3-bet and a cold call: Reverse implied odds are massive. Every good card for you is also good for your opponents' ranges.

The key distinction: when you're getting good odds and beat bluffs + overvalued hands, call. When you're getting mediocre odds and the opponent's range is almost all better hands, fold — even with two pair.

Theme 7: The "Gift Economy" of Small Stakes

Reality Check
Many of the pots Michael won were gifts from opponents playing terribly:
  • KK not 3-betting preflop, then donk-betting and jamming the turn
  • Opponents not bluffing the river when they should (busted draws checking back)
  • AK not 4-betting preflop, giving hero a cheap look with a worse hand
  • Players calling 3-bets with K3 of hearts

This inflates your win rate. As you move up in stakes, these gifts become rarer. A player winning $50/hour at 1-3 may only win $30/hour at 2-5. This is normal.

The solution: game select aggressively. Pick when you play, where you play, and who you play against. Even some high stakes games are very soft if you choose well.
