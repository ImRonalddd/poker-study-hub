1. Shorthanded Play (4-Handed)

Key Principle
Four-handed is NOT a radically different game. Just assume the first few players at a full table folded. The cutoff is now the first to act.

The Bunching Effect

When players fold before you, they're folding weaker hands. This means remaining players are slightly more likely to hold premium cards. But four-handed, only one player has folded before the button, so the effect is minimal.

Range Adjustments

Position8-Handed4-Handed
Cutoff (first to act)~25.5%~30%
Button RFI~41%~45%

The extra hands are small pairs, suited connectors, and marginal offsuit broadways. Just a little bit wider.

Opponent Mistakes
Too tight: They play UTG charts from the cutoff because "first to act = tight."
Too loose: They think shorthanded means play every hand.
Both are wrong. Adjust your ranges only slightly.

Postflop

  • Fewer multiway pots — you'll battle heads-up more
  • More 3-bet and 4-bet pots (SB 3-bets the button more frequently)
  • With only 3 opponents, develop targeted reads on each player

2. Heads-Up Play

Warning
Heads-up is a fundamentally different game from any other format. Ranges are roughly double what you're used to, and mixed strategies become critical.

Structure

Small blind = button. Small blind acts first preflop; big blind acts first postflop. This heavily favors the button player.

Preflop Ranges (100bb)

PositionActionFrequency
Button/SBRaise81% of hands (no limping)
Big Blind3-betStrong linear range + bluffs
Big BlindCallHands that flop well
Big BlindPlay total~75% of hands
Insight: 3-Bet Sizing
From the big blind, 3-bet to 11-12 big blinds, not 9. If you make it 9 vs. a 3bb open, the button is getting 2:1 odds and only needs 33% equity to call — which most hands have.

Mixed Strategies

Against the same opponent repeatedly, pure strategies become exploitable. Use randomizers. Example: A9s might 3-bet 50%, call 50%. Doug Polk and Daniel Negreanu used charts + random number generators in their famous heads-up match.

Facing 3-Bets (Button)

  • 4-bet with absolute best hands + tiny frequencies of bluffs
  • Call with hands that flop well (especially small/medium pairs)
  • Best 4-bet bluffs: A5s, A4s, A3s, A2s (blockers)
Practical Tip
Live: Negotiate no rake for heads-up — casinos want you to start tables.
Online: Avoid unless you've studied specifically; most online HU players are very strong.

3. Playing with a Straddle

Definition
A straddle is a voluntary third blind, typically posted by UTG for 2x the big blind. The straddler acts last preflop.

Why Straddling Is -EV

You only lose money from the blinds in NLHE. Why voluntarily create a third blind out of position? Do not straddle from UTG.

What Changes

  • Effective stack halved: $1000 at 5/10/20 = 50bb effective
  • Great for non-straddlers: You play a bigger game without paying extra
  • Bad if straddler is on your left: They take equity from your blinds

Combating Wild Straddlers

Core Strategy
"You beat maniacs by letting them bluff you. You do NOT beat them by forcing them to play straightforwardly."

With AA: If there's a raise and calls in front, just call. Let the straddler re-raise, then trap. If you 3-bet, the straddler folds even with garbage.

With marginal hands (97s): If you foresee a big re-raise coming, just fold preflop.

4. Mississippi Straddle

Definition
The button posts a straddle (2x BB). In most venues, the small blind acts first preflop. The button acts last both preflop and postflop.

Impact

Blinds get crushed. The small blind must act first before the entire table — worse than UTG. Both blinds should fold a lot.

Strategy by Position

PositionAdjustment
Small BlindVery tight. Limp entire playable range. Include limp-reraise with AA, KK, QQ, AK + blocker bluffs.
Big BlindTight. Only 1bb to call but terrible equity realization.
EP through COTighter than normal. Raise bigger (4-4.5x). No limping.
Button (straddler)Defend wide in position. Raise over limps. Call raises with suited connectors.
The Lame Game Problem
If everyone plays correctly: fold around to button, button wins the blinds, next hand. This is why many casinos ban button straddles.

5. Short-Stacked Games (20-40bb)

How It Differs

  • More all-in preflop situations
  • Fewer postflop betting rounds
  • Easier to solve — opponents who've studied make fewer errors
  • Rake trap: At 20bb with $10 rake, the game may be unbeatable

Use Tournament Charts as a Starting Point

Tournament charts presume no rake + an ante. Cash games have rake but no ante. Therefore: play tighter than tournament charts and cut marginal hands.

Bluff Selection Shifts
DepthPreferred BluffsWhy
Deep (200bb+)Suited connectorsImplied odds, playability
Standard (100bb)Mix of bothBalanced
Shallow (20-40bb)Blocker hands (Ax, Kx)Block calling range, no room to play postflop

At 20bb

Use shove-or-fold strategy extensively. Against serial limpers who fold to all-ins, shove wide — gaining 10-40% of your stack when everyone folds is massive. High cards (AT offsuit) beat suited connectors (T7s) at this depth.

Example: 20bb Shove with A9o vs Serial Limpers

Setup: $1/$2 cash game, effective stack $40 (20bb). Four players limp to you on the Button. Pot: $10. You hold \(A\diamondsuit 9\clubsuit\).

Action: Shove all-in for $40.

Why A9o beats T7s here: At 20bb, you don't have the stack depth to realize implied odds post-flop. Suited connectors need deep stacks to profit from flopped draws. A9o wins outright if everyone folds (which happens ~65-70% vs typical limpers). When called, A9o has 30-35% equity against a reasonable calling range (any pair, any Ax).

\[\text{EV (shove)} = 0.67 \times \$10 + 0.33 \times \left(0.33 \times \$90 - 0.67 \times \$40\right)\] \[= \$6.70 + 0.33 \times (\$29.70 - \$26.80) = \$6.70 + \$0.96 = +\$7.66\]

You gain +$7.66 on a $40 investment — a 19% return in one hand. Compare this to limping/folding: EV = $0.

EV cost of folding: You give up +$7.66 every time you encounter this spot. Over a 4-hour session with 3 such spots per hour = 12 missed opportunities × $7.66 = $92 of missed profit per session.

At 40bb

Mix of raise/call/shove/fold. 3-bet non-all-in with best hands + blocker bluffs. If rake is big, use 3-bet-or-fold from the small blind (no calling range).

Example: 40bb JJ in a 3-Bet Pot — Shove vs Call

Setup: $2/$5 cash game, your stack $200 (40bb). CO opens to $15. You 3-bet to $45 from the Button with \(J\heartsuit J\clubsuit\). CO 4-bets to $120.

Decision: Call the $120 (leaving $80 behind) or shove $200?

Calling is correct at this stack depth. CO's 4-bet range at 40bb is very tight: AA, KK, QQ, AK (and maybe AQs). Against that range, JJ has only ~38% equity vs {QQ+, AK} and ~30% vs {KK+, AKs}.

\[\text{EV (shove to \$200)} = 0.38 \times \$400 - 0.62 \times \$200 = \$152 - \$124 = +\$28\] \[\text{EV (call \$120, jam flop)} = \text{Similar but preserves fold equity on non-A/K/Q flops}\]

Calling gives you additional information on the flop. If an ace or king flops, you can sometimes fold the remaining $80 — an option the shove destroys. Against tight 4-bettors, flatting with $80 behind and folding AK flops saves ~$30 in EV compared to blind shoving.

6. Three-Blind Games

Definition
Three forced bets: small blind, middle blind, big blind (e.g., $1/$2/$4). Often occurs when a "round of straddles" becomes permanent.

Blind Strategy

PositionStrategyWhy
Small Blind3-bet or fold onlyWorst position. If you call, two players behind can overcall or re-raise. Devastating equity under-realization.
Middle Blind3-bet or fold mostlyActs like the small blind in a 2-blind game. Big blind still to act behind.
Big BlindNormal BB strategyBest relative position. Can defend somewhat wider due to extra dead money.
Common Trap
"It's just a little bit more" is what the middle blind thinks when calling. It's NOT. You're not closing the action, you're out of position, and you'll under-realize your equity drastically.
Example: Middle Blind Calling Trap with 9♥8♥

Setup: $1/$2/$4 three-blind game, 100bb effective ($400). UTG raises to $14. Folds to Middle Blind (you hold \(9\heartsuit 8\heartsuit\), $4 already in). Big Blind still to act behind.

"It's only $10 more to call into a $20 pot — I have 5:1 pot odds!" This thinking is wrong for three reasons:

  1. The BB is still to act. BB can 3-bet, and you'll be in a squeeze with a marginal hand facing $40+ OOP. Your $14 investment is now burning.
  2. Equity realization is terrible. 98s flops a pair or better ~32% of the time. But multiway OOP against a raiser, you can't bet, can't bluff, can't protect. You realize maybe 60% of your theoretical equity.
  3. EV comparison:
\[\text{EV (call)} \approx 0.60 \times \text{(theoretical equity value)} - \text{(positional tax)}\]

Against UTG's range (top 15%) with 98s, you have ~37% equity 3-way. But you realize maybe 22% of that equity OOP multiway. Required equity to profit: 14 / (14+20) = 41%. You don't meet it.

\[\text{EV (fold)} = \$0 \quad \text{vs} \quad \text{EV (call)} \approx -\$2.50\]

Over 10 sessions: Calling 98s in the middle blind 5 times per session × (-$2.50) × 10 sessions = $125 of unnecessary losses from one leak. 3-bet or fold instead — 3-betting takes the initiative and folds out the BB; folding costs nothing.

Other Positions

  • Tighter from early positions — more players can wake up with a hand
  • Button can stay roughly the same
  • Effective stack is halved (100bb at 1/2/4 becomes ~50bb equivalent)

If You Have a Deep-Stack Edge

You do NOT want the blinds getting bigger. It mitigates your postflop advantage. The "let's gamble and straddle" mentality only helps if opponents are blasting off postflop.

The Universal Framework

For Every New Format, Ask
  1. How have the positions changed?
  2. How has the effective stack changed?
  3. Who has the position advantage?
  4. How does this affect equity realization?
  5. What mistakes are my opponents making in this format?
