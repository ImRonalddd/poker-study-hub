Core Principle

For every new format, ask: "What has changed?" -- How have the positions changed? How has the effective stack changed? Who has position advantage? How does this affect equity realization?

1. Shorthanded Play (4-Handed)

The Bunching Effect

  • When players fold before you, they are folding weaker hands on average
  • Remaining players are slightly more likely to hold premium cards
  • At 4-handed, when it folds to the button, only 1 player has folded -- less bunching, so you can open slightly wider

Range Adjustments

Position8-Handed4-HandedDifference
Cutoff (first to act)~25.5%~30%+4.5%
Button RFI~41%~45%+4%
Common Opponent Mistakes
  1. Too tight: They think "under the gun = tight" regardless of table size, playing UTG charts from the cutoff
  2. Too loose: They think "shorthanded = play everything" and splash around with garbage

Postflop Considerations

  • Fewer multiway pots -- you will battle heads-up more often
  • More 3-bet and 4-bet pots (button-raise/small-blind-3-bet dynamic is frequent)
  • Maximally exploit each specific opponent -- with only 3 opponents, you can develop highly targeted reads

2. Heads-Up Play

Key Insight

Going to 2-handed is a dramatically different game. Ranges are substantially wider, making postflop play more complex. Small blind = Button (acts first preflop, last postflop).

Preflop Strategy (100bb)

Position% of HandsStrategy
Button/SB~81%Raise everything you play, no limping, 3bb standard
BB vs Button raise~75%3-bet linear + bluffs, call with IO hands, fold worst ~25%

Facing 3-Bets on the Button

  • 4-bet with absolute best hands + tiny frequencies of other hands
  • Call with hands that flop well (especially small/medium pairs)
  • Ace-x suited blockers (A5s, A4s, A3s, A2s) are the highest frequency 4-bet bluffs
Practical Tips
  • Live poker: Negotiate no rake for heads-up play -- casinos often agree
  • Online poker: Very difficult; opponents have usually studied extensively
  • Mixed strategies are important: Against the same opponent repeatedly, pure strategies become exploitable. Use randomizers.

3. Playing with a Straddle (UTG Straddle)

What Is a Straddle?

A voluntary third blind posted by the UTG player (typically 2x the big blind). The straddler acts last preflop.

Why Straddling Is Bad

You are putting in extra money out of position. You only lose money from the blinds in NLHE -- why voluntarily create another blind? Do not straddle from UTG.

What Changes When Someone Straddles

  1. Effective stack is halved: Playing 5/10/20 with $1000 means 50bb effective
  2. One additional player is out of position: Very bad for SB and BB
  3. Great for non-straddlers: You're playing a bigger game without paying extra

Combating Wild Straddlers

SituationWith Premium Hand (AA, KK)With IO Hand (97s)
Raise + calls in frontSlow-play: call, let straddler re-raise, then get it inFold: don't face a huge re-raise
Small re-raise from straddlerCall or re-raise depending on depthCall if odds are good
Large re-raise from straddlerRe-raise all-inFold
Key Insight

"You beat maniacs by letting them bluff you. You do NOT beat them by forcing them to play straightforwardly."

4. Mississippi Straddle (Button Straddle)

Definition

The button posts a straddle (typically 2x BB). The small blind acts first preflop. Unlike UTG straddle, the button straddler gets position both preflop AND postflop -- potentially even profitable deep-stacked against weak opponents.

Impact on Positions

Blinds Get Crushed
  • Small blind must act first before ALL other players -- effectively worse than UTG
  • Both blinds should fold a lot
  • SB: limp with playable range + limp-reraise (AA, KK, QQ, AK), raise with QQ-TT, AK-AJ, KQ
  • Other positions (UTG-CO): also tighter than normal, raise 4-4.5x instead of 3-3.5x

Button straddle strategy: Defend wider than you might expect -- you're in position getting good odds. Against limps: raise frequently. Against a raise: call with suited connectors, suited hands, decent broadways.

The "Lame Game" Problem

If everyone plays correctly, the game devolves into: fold around to button, button wins the blinds, next hand. This is why many casinos disallow Mississippi straddles.

5. Short-Stacked Cash Games (20-40bb)

Key Differences from 100bb Play
  • More all-in preflop situations
  • Fewer postflop betting rounds
  • Easier to solve mathematically
  • Beware the rake trap: At 20bb with a $10 rake, the game may be unbeatable

Strategy: Use Tournament Charts, Then Adjust

  • Tournament charts assume no rake + ante -- cash games have rake and no ante
  • Therefore, play tighter than tournament charts suggest
  • Cut marginal hands from the bottom of ranges (Q8, K7, A2)

Key Adjustments by Stack Depth

Stack DepthStrategy
20bbHeavy shove-or-fold. Against limpers: shove wide with blockers. High card hands UP, IO hands DOWN. Open-shove more to avoid rake.
40bbMix of raise/call/shove/fold. 3-bet non-all-in with best hands + bluffs. Calling range exists IP. JJ/TT can shove over 3-bets. SB: 3-bet-or-fold if rake is big.

Bluff Selection at Shallow Stacks

Stack DepthPreferred BluffsWhy
Deep (200bb+)Suited connectorsImplied odds, playability
Standard (100bb)Mix of bothBalanced approach
Shallow (20-40bb)Blocker hands (Ax, Kx)Block opponent's calling range, no room for playability

6. Three-Blind Games

Structure

Small blind, middle blind, big blind (e.g., $1/$2/$4 or $5/$10/$20). One additional blind = more dead money, but one more player to act behind. Effective stack is halved.

Position-by-Position Strategy

PositionStrategy
Small Blind (worst)Must 3-bet or fold -- no calling range. Two players still to act behind.
Middle BlindShould also 3-bet or fold the majority of the time. Terrible relative position on flop.
Big Blind (best blind)Can defend somewhat wider due to extra dead money. Good relative position when both other blinds call.
EP through ButtonTighter ranges than normal (especially EP). More players can wake up with a hand.
Common Mistakes
  1. Middle blind calls too loosely ("it's just a little bit more" -- but it's not)
  2. Small blind tries to see flops cheaply instead of 3-betting
  3. Players don't adjust open-raising ranges for the third blind
  4. Not recognizing that the game is now effectively shallower

Cross-Cutting Themes

Exploit > GTO in Non-Standard Games
  • Opponents in non-standard formats make MORE mistakes
  • Shorthanded: Opponents either too tight or too loose
  • Straddles: Straddlers are usually looking to gamble
  • Short-stacked: Many players use 100bb strategies at 20bb
  • Three-blind: Middle blind plays way too loose
The Rake Factor
  • Short-stacked games can be rake traps
  • Rake disproportionately hurts limped/small pots
  • Adjust by: playing tighter, raising bigger, using shove-or-fold more
  • At 20bb with a $5+ rake, the game may be mathematically unbeatable
Game Selection Is Critical
  • As tables get smaller, opponent quality matters MORE
  • Heads-up online is extremely tough -- most opponents have studied
  • Live heads-up against a recreational player = huge edge
  • Start tables at the casino for profitable shorthanded opportunities
