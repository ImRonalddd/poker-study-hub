2. Equity / Expected Value (EV)

Lesson: equity-2

Core Formula -- Expected Value (All-In / No Future Betting)

Formula $$EV = (P_{win} \times \text{Amount Won}) + (P_{lose} \times \text{Amount Lost})$$
  • Amount Lost is entered as a negative number
  • Amount Won = pot + opponent's bet (everything you collect when you win)

Equity Share of a Pot

$$\text{Your Share} = \text{Equity}\% \times \text{Total Pot}$$ $$\text{Profit} = \text{Your Share} - \text{Amount You Put In}$$

Worked Examples

ScenarioWin%Win AmountLose AmountEV
Fair coin flip $100/$10050%+$100-$100$0
Weighted coin $200/$10050%+$200-$100+$50
All-in $100 into $300 pot, 30% equity30%+$400-$100+$50
AKo vs JTs all-in, $10k pot59.5%----$5,948 share
Key Insight

You can be behind in raw equity and still have a profitable call because dead money in the pot compensates.

3. Pot Odds

Lesson: pot-odds-2

Traditional Format

Formula $$\text{Pot Odds} = \frac{\text{Pot} + \text{Opponent's Bet}}{\text{Your Call}} \;:\; 1$$

Converting to Required Win %

Formula $$\text{Required Win\%} = \frac{\text{Call Amount}}{\text{Pot} + \text{Opponent's Bet} + \text{Call Amount}}$$

Or equivalently: \(\text{Required Win\%} = \frac{\text{Call}}{\text{Total Pot After You Call}}\)

Key Rules
  • Maximum required win% when facing a bet is ~50%
  • As bet size decreases → continue more often
  • As bet size increases → continue less often
  • Always account for rake in real games

4. Ranges

Lesson: ranges
Definition

A range is all possible hands someone could realistically hold at a specific point in a hand. Ranges narrow as actions are taken.

Hand Grid Layout

  • Diagonal: Pocket pairs (AA, KK, ... 22)
  • Above diagonal (right): Suited hands
  • Below diagonal (left): Offsuit hands

Range Narrowing Process

  1. Preflop: Assign a range based on position and action (raise, limp, fold)
  2. Flop: Remove hands inconsistent with their action (bet vs check)
  3. Turn/River: Continue narrowing

Categorizing Hands in a Range

  • Premium made hands -- top pair+, sets, straights, flushes
  • Draws -- flush draws, straight draws
  • Marginal made hands -- second pair, weak top pair
  • Junk -- missed draws, underpairs, air

Range vs. Range Thinking

  • Strong preflop range vs weak calling range on A-J-4: raiser has ~69.5% equity → bet frequently
  • Strong preflop range vs weak calling range on 7-6-5: roughly 50-50 → be cautious
  • Board texture determines which range benefits more
Exploitative Insight

Weak-tight players who bet = strong, check = weak can be exploited by betting aggressively when they check (they fold ~67% of marginal/junk hands).

5. Putting It All Together: Equity + Pot Odds + Ranges

Lesson: equity-pot-odds-ranges

Decision Framework

  1. Calculate pot odds → required win%
  2. Estimate opponent's range based on their actions
  3. Calculate your equity vs that range (use Equilab)
  4. If equity > required win% → call (or raise)
  5. If equity < required win% → fold (or bluff-raise)
Worked Example: 4-Bet All-In at 100bb
  • Hero 4-bets to 22bb, villain shoves 100bb
  • Call 78 more into total pot of 201
  • Required equity: 78/201 = 38.8%
  • Calling range vs villain's jam range: 88+, AQs+, AKo

Key Relationships

FactorEffect on Calling Range
Opponent's range gets widerCall wider
Opponent's range gets tighterCall tighter
Pot odds get better (small bet)Call wider
Pot odds get worse (big bet)Call tighter
Effective Stack Matters
  • Shorter stacks → better pot odds → wider calling ranges
  • Deeper stacks → worse pot odds when facing all-in → tighter calling ranges

Sets on a Flop

  • Each rank on board has 1 card visible → 3 combos per set
  • Three different ranks → 3 + 3 + 3 = 9 total set combos
  • Paired board (e.g., 9-9-6): 1 combo of 99 + 3 combos of 66 = 4 set combos
Sets vs Trips
  • Set: Pocket pair + one matching board card (more disguised)
  • Trips: One card in hand + two matching board cards (less disguised, harder to get paid)

Blockers

Definition

A blocker is a card in your hand that reduces opponent's combos of specific holdings.

  • Holding A-spade on 3-spade board → opponent cannot have nut flush; their flush combos reduced by ~33%
  • Holding an Ace when 3-betting → opponent has only 3 combos of AA (not 6) and 12 combos of AK (not 16)
  • If opponent only plays suited versions of a hand, combos drop dramatically (e.g., 75s = 4 combos vs 75 = 16)

7. GTO vs Implementable Strategies

Lesson: gto-vs-implementable-strategies
Definition: GTO

A strategy where if your opponent knows your exact strategy and plays perfectly, they break even at best (lose the rake). Like perfect Tic-Tac-Toe -- unexploitable. Computed by solvers. Impossible for humans to execute perfectly due to mixed frequencies.

The Problem with Pure GTO

  • Requires precise mixed strategies (e.g., 4-bet AKo 68%, call 32%)
  • Different frequencies for nearly identical hands (AKs vs AKo)
  • Board-texture-dependent adjustments are innumerable
  • Cannot memorize every situation

Implementable Strategies (What the Course Teaches)

  • Simplify GTO into rules humans can execute
  • Maintain correct overall frequencies (value:bluff ratios, betting frequencies)
  • Assign hands to pure actions (always raise OR always call, not mixed)
  • Small deviations from GTO are acceptable if frequencies stay balanced

Example: Facing a 3-Bet in the Hijack

HandGTO ActionImplementable Action
AA4-bet 100%4-bet 100%
KK4-bet 54%, call 46%4-bet 100%
AKs4-bet 42%, call 58%Call 100%

Key Insight

The implementable version has slightly more value combos AND slightly more bluff combos, which roughly cancels out.

Exploitative Adjustments

  • Opponent only 3-bets premium? → Stop bluffing, call tighter
  • Opponent 3-bets wide with junk? → Add more bluffs, widen 4-bet range
  • Opponent 3-bets wide AND 5-bets wide? → Stop bluffing, add more value hands (e.g., 4-bet TT for value)
