2. Equity / Expected Value (EV)

Lesson: equity-2

Core Formula -- Expected Value (All-In / No Future Betting)

Formula \[EV = (P_{win} \times \text{Amount Won}) + (P_{lose} \times \text{Amount Lost})\]
  • Amount Lost is entered as a negative number
  • Amount Won = pot + opponent's bet (everything you collect when you win)

Equity Share of a Pot

\[\text{Your Share} = \text{Equity}\% \times \text{Total Pot}\] \[\text{Profit} = \text{Your Share} - \text{Amount You Put In}\]

Worked Examples

ScenarioWin%Win AmountLose AmountEV
Fair coin flip $100/$10050%+$100-$100$0
Weighted coin $200/$10050%+$200-$100+$50
All-in $100 into $300 pot, 30% equity30%+$400-$100+$50
AKo vs JTs all-in, $10k pot59.5%----$5,948 share
Key Insight

You can be behind in raw equity and still have a profitable call because dead money in the pot compensates.

Full situation: You are in the Big Blind at a 6-max $1/$2 game, 100bb effective stacks. A player raises to $6 from the Button. You call with \(9\diamondsuit 8\diamondsuit\). The flop comes \(K\spadesuit 5\clubsuit 2\heartsuit\) -- you miss completely. You check, they bet $8 into a $13 pot, you fold. The turn is a blank. This is NOT the scenario.

Instead: Later, on the turn in a different hand, the board is \(Q\heartsuit J\clubsuit 4\diamondsuit 9\clubsuit\). You are in the Big Blind with \(K\clubsuit T\clubsuit\) -- an open-ended straight draw plus a flush draw. The pot is $300. Your opponent, who raised preflop from the Cutoff, bets all-in for $100.

Your draw: Any Ace or 8 makes a straight (8 outs), plus any King gives you top pair (3 outs), plus you can re-evaluate against their range. Against their value range of sets and two-pairs, you have roughly 30% equity overall (the flush draw partially cancels against their disguised holdings).

EV of calling: \(\text{EV} = (0.30 \times \$400) + (0.70 \times -\$100) = \$120 - \$70 = +\$50\)

Even though you lose 70% of the time, the $300 already in the pot means every $100 call earns you $50 on average. The key insight: you are not deciding whether to "invest" $100 to "win" $100 -- you are deciding whether to invest $100 to own 30% of a $400 pot ($120 value). The pot is the prize, not just your opponent's bet.

EV cost of folding: By folding, you collect $0. By calling, you collect +$50 in expectation. You forfeit $50 every time you make this fold, even though it feels "safe."

3. Pot Odds

Lesson: pot-odds-2

Traditional Format

Formula \[\text{Pot Odds} = \frac{\text{Pot} + \text{Opponent's Bet}}{\text{Your Call}} \;:\; 1\]

Converting to Required Win %

Formula \[\text{Required Win\%} = \frac{\text{Call Amount}}{\text{Pot} + \text{Opponent's Bet} + \text{Call Amount}}\]

Or equivalently: \(\text{Required Win\%} = \frac{\text{Call}}{\text{Total Pot After You Call}}\)

Quick Reference Table

Key Rules
  • Maximum required win% when facing a bet is ~50%
  • As bet size decreases → continue more often
  • As bet size increases → continue less often
  • Always account for rake in real games
Example: Pot Odds on the River with a Flush Draw That Missed

Situation: You are on the Button in a $1/$2 cash game, 100bb effective stacks ($200 total). You raised to $6 preflop with \(A\heartsuit 5\heartsuit\). The Big Blind called. The pot is $13.

Flop: \(K\heartsuit 9\heartsuit 2\clubsuit\) -- you have the nut flush draw and an overcard. Opponent checks, you bet $8 (pot grows to $29). They call.

Turn: \(3\diamondsuit\) -- a blank. Pot: $29. Opponent checks. You bet $18. They call. Pot: $65.

River: \(J\clubsuit\) -- the flush draw misses. Opponent bets $150 into the $65 pot.

Pot odds calculation: You must call $150 to win ($65 + $150 + $150) = $365 total. Required equity: $150 / $365 = 41%.

Should you call? You have Ace-high. Your opponent fired $150 into a $65 pot -- a 2.3x overbet on the river. Against their range (which includes sets, two-pair, and some bluffs), your Ace-high has very little showdown equity. You need 41% to call -- your hand wins perhaps 5-10% of the time. This is a clear fold, even though the pot is large. The pot odds require you to win nearly half the time, and your Ace-high cannot do that.

EV cost of the wrong play (calling): EV = (0.07 x $365) + (0.93 x -$150) = $25.55 - $139.50 = -$113.95 per call. Calling here loses over $100 on average. The large overbet by the opponent prices you out unless you have a strong read they are bluffing more than 59% of the time with that sizing.

4. Ranges

Lesson: ranges
Definition

A range is all possible hands someone could realistically hold at a specific point in a hand. Ranges narrow as actions are taken.

Hand Grid Layout

  • Diagonal: Pocket pairs (AA, KK, ... 22)
  • Above diagonal (right): Suited hands
  • Below diagonal (left): Offsuit hands

Range Narrowing Process

  1. Preflop: Assign a range based on position and action (raise, limp, fold)
  2. Flop: Remove hands inconsistent with their action (bet vs check)
  3. Turn/River: Continue narrowing

Categorizing Hands in a Range

  • Premium made hands -- top pair+, sets, straights, flushes
  • Draws -- flush draws, straight draws
  • Marginal made hands -- second pair, weak top pair
  • Junk -- missed draws, underpairs, air

Range vs. Range Thinking

  • Strong preflop range vs weak calling range on A-J-4: raiser has ~69.5% equity → bet frequently
  • Strong preflop range vs weak calling range on 7-6-5: roughly 50-50 → be cautious
  • Board texture determines which range benefits more
Exploitative Insight

Full situation: $1/$2 cash game, 100bb effective stacks. A weak-tight, straightforward player raises to $6 from early position (UTG). He only raises premium hands: AA, KK, QQ, JJ, TT, AKs, AQs, AJs, AKo, AQo. You call from the Button with \(J\clubsuit T\clubsuit\).

Preflop range: You know he raised only those hands. He does NOT have hands like 87s or KJo -- he would fold those preflop.

Flop: \(K\spadesuit 7\heartsuit 6\diamondsuit\). Pot: $13. He bets $8 (his standard c-bet with everything). You call. Pot: $29. His range is unchanged -- he bets the flop with 100% of his raising range, so you learn nothing from his bet.

Turn: \(Q\diamondsuit\). Board: \(K\spadesuit 7\heartsuit 6\diamondsuit Q\diamondsuit\). Pot: $29. He checks.

What his check means: This player only bets his strong hands on the turn. His strong hands on K-7-6-Q are: AA, KK, QQ, AK, KQ -- he bets all of these. His checking range is everything else: AQ (second pair), JJ, TT, 99, 88 (underpairs), and air (AJ, smaller pairs). The range analyzer shows he folds approximately 67% of the time when you bet into his checking range, because only AQ calls while JJ-88 and air fold.

The exploit: Bet the turn -- with ANY two cards. If you bet $18 into the $29 pot, he folds 67% of the time and you win the pot uncontested. You only need to win 38% of the time to profit from this bet (18 / (29 + 18 + 18) = 27.7% breakeven). You win 67%. This is a massive profit.

EV of the bet: 67% of the time you win $29 = $19.43. 33% of the time he calls and you continue (assume you lose the remaining street for simplicity): -$18 x 33% = -$5.94. Net EV of the bet = roughly +$13 per turn bet in this spot.

Root cause: He put all his strong hands into one action (betting) and all his weak hands into another (checking). This makes him perfectly transparent and easily exploitable. The counter is to include some strong hands in your checking range.

5. Putting It All Together: Equity + Pot Odds + Ranges

Lesson: equity-pot-odds-ranges

Decision Framework

  1. Calculate pot odds → required win%
  2. Estimate opponent's range based on their actions
  3. Calculate your equity vs that range (use Equilab)
  4. If equity > required win% → call (or raise)
  5. If equity < required win% → fold (or bluff-raise)
Worked Example: 4-Bet All-In at 100bb

Situation: You raise to 3bb from the Hijack seat with a 100bb effective stack. The Small Blind 3-bets to 10bb. You 4-bet to 22bb. The Small Blind shoves all-in for 100bb total.

Villain's presumed jamming range: TT+, AQs+, AKo -- a tight, value-heavy range of premium hands.

Pot odds calculation: You already put in 22bb. You must call 78bb more (100 - 22). The total pot after your call will be 201bb (your 100 + villain's 100 + the 1bb dead BB). Required equity: 78 / 201 = 38.8%.

Finding the calling range using Equilab:

  • AKo vs this range: ~47% equity -- easy call
  • AQo vs this range: ~38.6% equity -- just barely a fold (0.2% below threshold)
  • AQs vs this range: ~41% equity -- clear call (suitedness adds ~2.5%)
  • 99 vs this range: ~39.1% equity -- razor-thin call (just above threshold)
  • 88 vs this range: ~39.0% equity -- also a call (88 and 99 have similar equity because both are dominated by all pairs in the range, and both block some straight combos)
  • 77 and below: drops below 38.8% -- fold

Final calling range: 88+, AQs+, AKo. With 88 and 99, you are making a tiny profit -- a razor-thin edge. But consistently folding these spots gives your opponent free equity over time.

Why your read matters: If villain has even one extra bluff (e.g., A5s in their jam range), AQo jumps to ~40% and becomes a call. If they remove KQs, your equity drops and 99 becomes a fold. Your assessment of the opponent's range is the most critical variable in every all-in decision.

Key Relationships

FactorEffect on Calling Range
Opponent's range gets widerCall wider
Opponent's range gets tighterCall tighter

Effective Stack Matters
  • Shorter stacks → better pot odds → wider calling ranges
  • Deeper stacks → worse pot odds when facing all-in → tighter calling ranges

Blockers

Definition

A blocker is a card in your hand that reduces opponent's combos of specific holdings. Blockers are one of the most powerful concepts in poker -- they change how likely your opponent is to hold specific hands.

Example: Nut Flush Blocker

Situation: $2/$5 cash game, 100bb effective stacks ($500). You are in the Big Blind with \(A\spadesuit T\diamondsuit\). The Hijack raises to $15. You call. The pot is $31.

Flop: \(9\spadesuit 6\spadesuit 2\heartsuit\). You check, they bet $20. You call (pot: $71). Turn: \(K\spadesuit\) -- three spades on board. You check. They check behind. River: \(4\spadesuit\) -- four spades on board. You check. They bet $80 into the $71 pot.

Analysis: You have Ace-high -- not a made hand. But you hold the \(A\spadesuit\), meaning your opponent cannot have the nut flush (ace-high flush). Their possible flush combos, assuming they play AKs, AQs, AJs, KQs, KJs, QJs, JTs, T9s in spades (8 reasonable combos), are reduced as follows: the \(A\spadesuit\) eliminates AKs-spades, AQs-spades, AJs-spades = 3 combos eliminated out of 8 = 37.5% fewer total flushes.

Why this matters: Their betting range on the river now contains more bluffs (semi-bluffs that missed, like \(Q\spadesuit J\diamondsuit\) with one spade, missed straight draws) relative to flushes than you would expect without holding the blocker. Calling $80 to win $231 requires winning 34.6% of the time. If without the blocker their range were 60% flushes and 40% bluffs, you'd win only 40% and calling would be borderline. With the blocker, their range shifts toward perhaps 45% flushes and 55% bluffs -- a clear profitable call.

EV cost of ignoring the blocker: If you fold when you should call, you forfeit +$35 in EV per river situation of this type. Over a year of play, this type of fold in blocker spots adds up to a significant leak.

Example: Preflop Ace Blocker as a 3-Bet Bluff

Situation: $1/$2 cash game, 100bb effective stacks ($200). The player UTG+1 raises to $6. It folds to you in the Hijack. You hold \(A\diamondsuit 5\diamondsuit\). You 3-bet to $18 as a bluff, planning to fold if they 4-bet (unless they are very aggressive).

Why A5s specifically? Your opponent's 4-bet (fighting back) range consists primarily of AA and AK. Without your ace in hand, their danger combos are:

  • AA: 6 combos
  • AK: 16 combos
  • Total: 22 combos that punish your bluff

With the \(A\diamondsuit\) in your hand, those combos shrink to:

  • AA: only 3 combos (C(3,2) -- only 3 aces remain)
  • AK: only 12 combos (3 remaining aces x 4 kings)
  • Total: 15 combos -- a 32% reduction in the hands that punish you

If you instead 3-bet bluffed with \(7\clubsuit 6\clubsuit\), no blockers apply and the full 22 danger combos exist. The A5s bluff is intrinsically 32% more likely to succeed (or at least not encounter the hands most likely to 4-bet you). Additionally, the flush draw on \(A\heartsuit T\heartsuit 3\diamondsuit\) type boards gives you a backup equity source, making A5s doubly preferable to 76s as a bluff.

EV cost of using 76s instead of A5s: Suppose the opponent 4-bets 22% of the time against random bluffs (22 danger combos). Against A5s, they 4-bet only 15% of the time (15 combos adjusted). If you always fold to a 4-bet, each 3-bet costs you $18 when called off -- the 7% reduction in 4-bet frequency saves you approximately $1.26 per 3-bet attempt. Over hundreds of 3-bets, this compounds significantly.

  • Suited vs offsuit matters for combos: If your opponent only plays 75 suited (from a middle-position raise), there are only 4 combos of 75s. If they play all 75 hands, there are 16 combos. On a board like A-6-4, a 3 or 8 making a straight is far less worrying if your opponent only plays the suited version.

7. GTO vs Implementable Strategies

Lesson: gto-vs-implementable-strategies
Definition: GTO

A strategy where if your opponent knows your exact strategy and plays perfectly, they break even at best (lose the rake). Like perfect Tic-Tac-Toe -- unexploitable. Computed by solvers. Impossible for humans to execute perfectly due to mixed frequencies.

The Problem with Pure GTO

  • Requires precise mixed strategies (e.g., 4-bet AKo 68%, call 32%)
  • Different frequencies for nearly identical hands (AKs vs AKo)
  • Board-texture-dependent adjustments are innumerable
  • Cannot memorize every situation

Implementable Strategies (What the Course Teaches)

  • Simplify GTO into rules humans can execute
  • Maintain correct overall frequencies (value:bluff ratios, betting frequencies)
  • Assign hands to pure actions (always raise OR always call, not mixed)
  • Small deviations from GTO are acceptable if frequencies stay balanced

Example: Facing a 3-Bet in the Hijack

Full situation: $1/$2 cash game, 100bb effective stacks ($200). You are in the Hijack and open to 3bb ($6). Everyone folds. The Small Blind 3-bets to 10bb ($20). The Big Blind folds. Action is back to you. You have already invested $6. To continue, you must either 4-bet or call. The effective stack started at 100bb; after your open and the SB's 3-bet, there is $43 in the pot (your $6 + SB's $20 + BB's $2 dead). Your 4-bet will be to approximately 22bb ($44). If you 4-bet and the SB shoves 100bb, you have to call 78bb more into a ~201bb pot (38.8% equity required).

Key Insight: Why This Works

The GTO solver mixes KK (54% 4-bet, 46% call), AKs (42% 4-bet, 58% call), and AKo (68% 4-bet, 32% call). This is impossible for humans to execute precisely at the table. You cannot flip an internal mental coin and 4-bet exactly 54% of the time with KK.

The implementable fix: Always 4-bet KK and AKo for value. Always call AKs. This gives approximately 2 more value combos than GTO uses (12 combos of AKo x 100% + 6 combos of KK x 100% = 18 combos, vs GTO's ~16 effective value combos at mixed frequencies). To compensate, we add 2 extra bluff combos: A3s and A5s as pure 4-bet bluffs = 8 bluff combos total, vs GTO's ~4-6.

Why the "errors" cancel: Extra value hands make us slightly over-aggressive for value. Extra bluffs make us slightly over-aggressive with air. These two effects push in opposite directions from the opponent's perspective -- one gives them more opportunities to call and be wrong, the other gives them more opportunities to fold. Net effect: our 4-bet frequency and value-to-bluff ratio stay close to GTO. The strategy is vastly easier to execute while sacrificing minimal EV -- likely less than 0.5bb per 100 hands.

EV cost of pure GTO mixing vs. implementable: A player who tries to execute the GTO mixed strategy but who gets the percentages wrong by even 10-15% (very common) loses more EV than a player who uses the pure-action implementable strategy. Correct frequencies matter more than theoretically-correct mixing.

Exploitative Adjustments

  • Opponent only 3-bets premium (QQ+, AK)? Stop bluffing with A3s/A5s -- they won't fold. Tighten calling range because their range is stronger. If they shove, required equity is the same (38.8%) but our actual equity with hands like 99 drops below threshold. Now call only TT+, AQs+, AKo.
  • Opponent 3-bets very wide (any two suited, all pairs)? Add many more bluff combos to your 4-bet range. Their range is so wide that your bluffs go through more often AND your value hands have more equity. Even hands like QJs can be profitable 4-bet bluffs against a wide 3-bettor.
  • Opponent 3-bets wide AND 5-bets all-in with junk? Stop bluffing entirely -- they call everything. Instead, add more value hands. 4-bet TT for value (normally a call in implementable) because their shove range is so wide that TT has 50%+ equity against it. You profit from calling their wide shove.
