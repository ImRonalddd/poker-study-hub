1. Course Mindset & Structure

The Cash Game Masterclass applies to all stakes, online and live, from micro-stakes to high-stakes. The course assumes an 8-handed table with a small blind and big blind. For 6-max, simply imagine the first two seats (UTG, UTG+1) have folded.

Key Insight

You make money when your opponents make mistakes that you do not also make. Most profit comes from fundamentally sound play plus exploiting opponents' specific errors.

The approach requires paying attention to your opponents. When you identify what someone does incorrectly, you adjust to exploit it. For example, if an opponent always bets when checked to, you check strong hands to them (they'll over-bluff) and bet your bluffs directly (they'll fold assuming you have it).

Definition -- Effective Stack

The effective stack is the shortest stack relevant in the hand. If you have 150bb and your opponent has 80bb, you are playing an 80bb effective stack because that's the most either player can win or lose in the hand.

Tools you need:

  • Equilab (Windows) or Poker Cruncher (Mac) -- free equity calculators
  • PokerCoaching Preflop App -- GTO preflop charts
  • Range Analyzer -- in the PokerCoaching tools section

2. Equity & Expected Value

Definition -- Equity

Equity is your share of the pot based on how often you expect to win. If you have 60% equity in a $100 pot, you "own" $60 of it on average.

Definition -- Expected Value (EV)

Expected Value is how much you win or lose on average from a decision. When there is no more future betting (all-in situations), EV is calculated directly from win probability and amounts.

The EV Formula

For situations with no future betting (all-in or river calls):

\[EV = (P_{\text{win}} \times \text{Amount Won}) + (P_{\text{lose}} \times \text{Amount Lost})\]

Where Amount Lost is negative.

Worked Example -- Coin Flip

Scenario: Fair coin flip. Win $100 when heads, lose $100 when tails.

\[EV = (0.50 \times 100) + (0.50 \times -100) = 50 - 50 = \$0\]

Exactly break-even, as expected.

Worked Example -- Weighted Payoff

Scenario: Fair coin flip. Win $200 when heads, lose $100 when tails.

\[EV = (0.50 \times 200) + (0.50 \times -100) = 100 - 50 = +\$50\]

You profit $50 per flip on average despite winning only half the time.

Worked Example -- Poker All-In

Scenario: Opponent goes all-in for $100 into a $300 pot. You know you'll win 30% of the time.

If you win: you collect $300 (pot) + $100 (their bet) = $400. If you lose: you lose $100 (your call).

\[EV = (0.30 \times 400) + (0.70 \times -100) = 120 - 70 = +\$50\]

Despite winning only 30% of the time, calling is profitable because the pot gives you a large payout when you do win.

Equity Share of a Pot

\[\text{Your Equity Share} = \text{Your Equity \%} \times \text{Total Pot}\] \[\text{Profit} = \text{Equity Share} - \text{Amount You Invested}\]
Worked Example -- AKo vs JTs

You get all-in preflop with AKo vs JTs. Total pot is $10,000. AKo wins ~59.5% of the time.

\[\text{Your Share} = 0.595 \times 10{,}000 = \$5{,}948\]

If you invested $4,500 into the pot:

\[\text{Profit} = 5{,}948 - 4{,}500 = +\$1{,}448\]

JTs would lose: \((0.405 \times 10{,}000) - 4{,}500 = -\$448\)

Key Insight

Once money goes into the pot, it is no longer yours. Your only decision is whether your equity share of the total pot justifies the additional investment required.

3. Pot Odds

Definition -- Pot Odds

Pot odds express the ratio of what you can win to what you must risk. They tell you the minimum win percentage needed to justify a call.

Traditional Ratio Format

\[\text{Pot Odds} = (\text{Pot} + \text{Opponent's Bet}) \;:\; \text{Your Call}\]

Simplify by dividing both sides by the right number.

Converting to Required Win Percentage

\[\text{Required Win\%} = \frac{\text{Your Call}}{\text{Pot} + \text{Opponent's Bet} + \text{Your Call}}\]

Think of it as: your call divided by the total pot after you call.

Worked Example -- Half-Pot Bet

Scenario: Opponent bets $10 into a $10 pot.

Pot odds: ($10 + $10) : $10 = 2:1

\[\text{Required Win\%} = \frac{10}{10 + 10 + 10} = \frac{10}{30} = 33.3\%\]
Worked Example -- Third-Pot Bet

Scenario: Opponent bets $100 into a $300 pot.

\[\text{Required Win\%} = \frac{100}{300 + 100 + 100} = \frac{100}{500} = 20\%\]

Against a small bet, you only need to win 1 in 5 times.

Worked Example -- Overbet

Scenario: Opponent bets $1,000 into a $700 pot (overbet).

\[\text{Required Win\%} = \frac{1{,}000}{700 + 1{,}000 + 1{,}000} = \frac{1{,}000}{2{,}700} = 37\%\]

Even against an overbet, you never need more than ~50% to call.

Key Insight -- The 50% Ceiling

When facing a bet, you never need more than ~50% equity to call. Because you're always matching your opponent's bet, the formula is always \(\frac{\text{call}}{\text{call} + \text{call} + \text{pot}}\). Even if they bet a million into a $10 pot, you need just under 50%.

Key Insight -- Bet Size and Defense

Smaller bets = continue more often (you need to win less). Larger bets = continue less often (you need to win more). This is why small c-bets on the flop often get called -- the defender's pot odds are too good to fold most hands.

4. Ranges

Definition -- Range

A range is all the hands someone could realistically hold at a given point in the hand. You never try to put an opponent on one specific hand; you think about which hands are consistent with every action they've taken.

The Hand Grid

The standard 13x13 grid represents all 169 distinct starting hands:

  • Diagonal (top-left to bottom-right): Pocket pairs -- AA, KK, ... 22
  • Above diagonal: Suited hands (e.g., AKs, QJs)
  • Below diagonal: Offsuit hands (e.g., AKo, QJo)

How Ranges Narrow

Every action an opponent takes removes hands from their range:

  1. Preflop: A tight player raises only premium hands. A loose player raises many hands. Their raise/limp/fold decision narrows their range immediately.
  2. Flop: If they continuation-bet with everything, their range doesn't narrow. If they only bet strong hands, a bet removes weak hands and a check removes strong hands.
  3. Turn/River: Each action further narrows the range.
Worked Example -- Exploiting a Weak-Tight Player

Setup: Weak-tight player raises preflop with premium hands only, c-bets flop 100% of the time, then only bets turn with strong hands (top pair+).

Board: K-7-6-Q. They check the turn.

Analysis: Their check means they do NOT have AA, KK, QQ, AK, KQ (they'd bet these). They only have marginal hands like AQ, JJ, TT, 99, 88.

Of these remaining hands, only AQ will call a bet. JJ-88 will fold. That means they fold approximately 67% of the time when they check turn.

Action: Bet aggressively when they check -- they'll fold most of their range.

Range vs Range Thinking

Rather than asking "how do I play my specific hand?", the right question is "how does my range compare to my opponent's range on this board?"

Worked Example -- Range Advantage by Board

Your range (UTG raiser): 77+, ATs+, KJs+, AJo+

Opponent's range (BB caller): TT-33, suited connectors, suited aces -- no AA/KK/QQ/JJ/AKs (would have 3-bet)

Board A-J-4: Your range has ~69.5% equity. You connect with this board heavily (many aces, AJ, etc.) while opponent is missing their best hands. Bet frequently.

Board 7-6-5: Your range has ~50.3% equity. Opponent has many two-pairs and straights. Be cautious.

Key Insight -- Balanced Ranges

You should have strong hands in both your betting range and checking range. If you always bet your strong hands and check your weak hands, observant opponents will exploit you by folding to your bets and bluffing your checks.

5. Putting It Together

The Decision Framework

  1. Calculate pot odds -- what is the minimum win% needed?
  2. Estimate opponent's range -- based on position, actions, tendencies
  3. Calculate your equity vs that range (use Equilab/Poker Cruncher)
  4. Compare: Equity > Required Win% = Call. Equity < Required Win% = Fold.
Worked Example -- 4-Bet All-In Decision (100bb Deep)

Setup: You raise to 3bb from HJ. SB 3-bets to 10bb. You 4-bet to 22bb. SB shoves 100bb.

Step 1 -- Pot Odds: You must call 78bb more (100 - 22) into a total pot of 201bb (your 100 + their 100 + SB).

\[\text{Required Win\%} = \frac{78}{201} = 38.8\%\]

Step 2 -- Opponent's Range: Estimated as QQ+, AKs, AKo (maybe AQs, A5s).

Step 3 -- Your Equity:

Result: Call with 88+, AQs+, AKo. Fold 77-, AQo, AJs-.

Worked Example -- Short Stack vs Maniac (25bb)

Setup: You raise to 2.2bb on BTN. BB (a maniac) shoves 25bb with an extremely wide range including hands like J8s, 97s, K5o, etc.

Pot Odds: Call 22.8bb into ~50.5bb total = 45.1% needed.

Calling Range: 22+, A5o+, A2s+, K9s+, QTs+, KTo+ -- much wider than normal because the opponent's range is so wide.

Against a tight shove range in the same spot: only TT+, AQs+, AKo.

6. Counting Combinations & Blockers

Definition -- Combinations (Combos)

The number of specific card arrangements that make up a given hand. Counting combos tells you how likely an opponent is to hold a particular hand.

Counting Unpaired Combos

\[\text{Combos} = \text{unseen cards of rank A} \times \text{unseen cards of rank B}\]
Worked Example -- AK on A-6-4 Board

One Ace is on the board. No Kings visible.

\[\text{AK combos} = 3 \text{ (aces left)} \times 4 \text{ (kings left)} = 12\]

Down from 16 preflop to 12 on this board.

Suited combos: only 3 (the A♥K♥ combo is gone since A♥ is on board).

Worked Example -- A6 Two-Pair on A-6-4

One Ace gone, one 6 gone.

\[\text{A6 combos} = 3 \times 3 = 9\]

If opponent only plays suited A6: only 2 combos remain (A♦6♦ and A♠6♠, since A♥ and 6♣ are on board).

Worked Example -- You Hold an Ace

Board: A♥-6♣-4♦. You hold A♦.

\[\text{Opponent's A6 combos} = 2 \text{ (aces left)} \times 3 \text{ (sixes left)} = 6\]

If they only play A6 suited: 1 combo (only A♠6♠ is possible).

Definition -- Set vs Trips

Set: You hold a pocket pair and one matching card is on the board (e.g., you have 77 on a 7-K-2 board). More disguised.

Trips: You hold one card matching a pair on the board (e.g., you have K7 on a 7-7-2 board). Less disguised, harder to get paid off.

Blockers

Definition -- Blocker

A blocker is a card in your hand that reduces the number of combinations of a specific holding your opponent can have.

Worked Example -- Nut Flush Blocker

Board has three spades. You hold A♠ (but no other spade).

Your opponent cannot have the nut flush. Their possible flushes drop by ~33% because all Ax-spade combos are eliminated. This makes bluff-catching more profitable since fewer of their bets represent the nuts.

Worked Example -- 3-Bet Blockers

You hold A5o and 3-bet. Your opponent now has:

  • AA: 3 combos instead of 6 (you block one Ace)
  • AK: 12 combos instead of 16 (3 aces x 4 kings)

This is one reason suited aces are good 3-bet bluffs -- they block the opponent's strongest hands.

Key Insight -- Suited vs Offsuit in Ranges

Many players only play the suited version of marginal hands (e.g., 75s but not 75o). This means instead of 16 combos, they have only 4. When a board makes a straight possible with a hand like 7-5, ask: does my opponent even play 75o? If not, there are only 4 possible combos of straights, not 16.

7. GTO vs Implementable Strategies

Definition -- Game Theory Optimal (GTO)

A strategy where if your opponent knows your exact strategy and plays perfectly against it, they break even at best (and lose the rake in cash games). It is the mathematically unexploitable strategy, computed by solvers.

Why Pure GTO Fails for Humans

  • Requires mixed strategies: "4-bet AKo 68% of the time, call 32%"
  • Nearly identical hands get different frequencies (AKs: 4-bet 42%, AKo: 4-bet 68%)
  • Tiny board texture differences change the strategy completely
  • Impossible to memorize or execute precisely at the table
Definition -- Implementable Strategy

A simplified version of the GTO strategy that humans can actually execute. It assigns pure actions to hands (always raise OR always call, not a mix) while maintaining approximately correct overall frequencies.

How to Build an Implementable Strategy

  1. Study the GTO solver output to understand what it does and why
  2. Look at the overall percentages of hands in each action (raise / call / fold)
  3. Assign hands to pure actions to match those percentages approximately
  4. Accept small deviations -- slightly too many value hands can be offset by slightly too many bluffs

Key Insight -- Why Call with Kings/Queens Preflop?

In GTO, you sometimes just call with KK/QQ/JJ facing a 3-bet so your calling range stays strong. If you always 4-bet your big pairs, your calling range becomes weak (only medium pairs and suited connectors), and your opponent can exploit you by bluffing your calls aggressively.

Key Insight -- Implementable Beats Imperfect GTO

A well-executed implementable strategy will outperform an imperfectly-executed GTO strategy. Your opponents who try to play "solver poker" but mix incorrectly will have wrong frequencies. Your pure-action strategy has the right frequencies and is easy to execute consistently.

8. Common Traps

Trap -- "I'm Supposed to Win"

Remove the concept of "supposed to" from your poker thinking. If you have 60% equity, you are "supposed" to win 60% and lose 40%. Losing 40% of the time is normal, not bad luck. Variance is inherent -- even 10 losses in a row at 60% equity is possible (probability ~0.01%).

Trap -- Wrong Pot Odds Calculation

Many players calculate pot odds by dividing their call by the pot before their call (e.g., $1000 / $1700 = 59%). The correct calculation includes your call in the denominator: $1000 / ($700 + $1000 + $1000) = 37%. The first method dramatically overestimates how often you need to win.

Trap -- Ignoring Pot Odds When "Flipping"

Players often fold AQs preflop because "I'm just flipping against his range." But if you have 49% equity and only need 39% to call profitably, folding is a massive mistake. Always compare equity to the required win percentage, not to 50%.

Trap -- Putting Opponent on One Hand

"I put him on AK." This is fundamentally wrong thinking. You should always think in ranges. Your opponent could have dozens of possible hands. Assigning a single hand is a guess; assigning a range is analysis.

Trap -- Betting = Strong, Checking = Weak

Many players (especially at low/mid stakes) bet all their good hands and check all their weak hands. This makes them extremely exploitable because when they check, opponents know they have junk and can bluff aggressively. Keep strong hands in both your betting and checking ranges.

Trap -- Fearing Unlikely Hands

Players panic when a "scary" card comes: "He could have 7-5 for a straight!" But if your opponent raised from UTG, they almost certainly don't play 7-5 offsuit (0 combos) and probably not even 7-5 suited (4 combos at most). Use combo counting to assess actual likelihood before panicking.

Trap -- Trying to Memorize Solver Output

Memorizing "4-bet AKo 68% of the time" is futile. You cannot execute mixed strategies accurately at the table. Instead, understand why the solver does what it does and build pure-action implementable strategies that maintain the correct overall frequencies.

Trap -- Forgetting the Rake

In marginal spots, rake can turn a slightly profitable call into a losing one. A pot that's $201 before rake might be $191 after rake. Always factor in your casino's rake structure, especially in small pots where rake takes a larger percentage.
