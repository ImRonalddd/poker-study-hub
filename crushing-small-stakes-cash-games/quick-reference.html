Crushing Small Stakes Cash Games

Jonathan Little — PokerCoaching.com
Quick Reference Cheat Sheet Cash Game 100bb Deep
Print tip: use Ctrl+P (or Cmd+P) and set margins to minimum for best results.

1 — Core Principles

The Winning Edge
  • You profit when opponents make mistakes you do not
  • Playing only strong hands = small winner, never big winner
  • You must identify and exploit specific opponent tendencies
  • Learn GTO baseline first, then deviate to exploit
Trap — Nut-Peddling
  • Tight-only play = rake eats your edge
  • Small stakes opponents: limp too much, skip river value bets, almost never bluff
  • These predictable errors are your profit source — actively exploit them
Stack Depth Assumption

Course assumes 100 big blind effective stacks, 8-handed with rake. If no rake (time rake / club), play looser ranges throughout. If 6-max online, treat as 8-max with UTG and UTG+1 having folded.

Key Insight — BB 3-Bet Sizing OOP

When 3-betting out of position (e.g., BB vs BTN 3 bb open), size to roughly 13–14 bb. Too small (9 bb) and villain calls or 4-bets every time. You are not trying to make them fold; you are making it expensive for them to continue with position.

Trap — SB Flatting

Calling from the SB vs a raiser is almost always wrong: you pay rake and have the worst relative position postflop (you act first on every postflop street). Default strategy: 3-bet or fold. Exception: BB is a bad player — then flat pairs, suited aces, suited connectors for multi-way implied odds.

Facing a 3-Bet: 4-Bet or Fold (No Calling Range vs Early Position)

Rule — No Cold Calling vs. Early-Position 3-Bets

When there is a raise and a 3-bet before action reaches you, 4-bet or fold. Implied odds for pairs and suited connectors are crushed when 3-bet to 10 bb; you are getting at best 10-to-1 implied odds, which is not sufficient.

Situation 4-Bet Value Bluff / Marginal 4-Bet Size Facing All-In Jam
BB vs LJ raise + HJ 3-bet AA, KK, QQ, JJ, AKs, AQs None — fold everything else 23–24 bb Call AA–QQ, AKs only
BB vs CO/BTN raise + 3-bet TT+, AKs–AJs, AQo AJs, AQo, KJs (bluff — fold these to jam) 23–24 bb Call AA–QQ, AKs, maybe JJ
BTN facing 4-bet (after your 3-bet) 5-bet AA–QQ, AKs/AKo Call JJ, 99, 55, some suited connectors vs wide 4-bettor — Fold AQo, KJs unless villain is a maniac
Trap — Calling 4-Bets Too Wide

In small stakes games, almost no one 4-bet bluffs. If you get 4-bet, assume a very strong range and overfold. Facing a 4-bet jam with AQo? Muck it immediately. If the player is aggressive and wide, then AQo can be a marginal call — but this player type is rare at small stakes.

4 — Player Type Exploit Guide

Tight Passive (Nit)

  • Reads: Only raises premiums. Check-calls medium pairs. Check-folds trash. Rarely bluffs. Overplays top pair.
  • Pre-flop: Raise their limps aggressively (3–4 bb). 3-bet them light when they open.
  • Post-flop: Bet freely when they check — they'll fold. When they raise or lead, give full credit.
  • Avoid: Bluffing multiple streets. They fold, then snap-call when they finally have something.
  • Bonus: They overplay top pair — get full value with your sets and two-pairs.

Loose Passive (Calling Station)

  • Reads: Wide preflop range; check-calls frequently; rarely raises or bluffs river.
  • Pre-flop: Raise their limps large; don't give cheap multi-way flops.
  • Post-flop: Value-bet relentlessly — 3 streets with top pair. Use larger sizes since they call wide.
  • Avoid: Bluffing at all. They will call you down with bottom pair. Never bluff them.
  • Bonus: If they do raise, they almost always have it.

Tight Aggressive (TAG / Reg)

  • Reads: Balanced-ish ranges; c-bets frequently; will fold to aggression sometimes.
  • Pre-flop: 3-bet a polarized range; steal often from late position.
  • Post-flop: Float in position; raise c-bets with equity; bluff missed draws on turn/river.
  • Avoid: Passive flatting OOP; letting them realize equity cheaply.
  • Bonus: Check-raise on dry board = usually strong; respect it.

Loose Aggressive (LAG / Maniac)

  • Reads: Wide 3-bet range; frequent c-bets; will bluff river frequently.
  • Pre-flop: Tighten 4-bet threshold; call wider vs their 3-bets with suited hands.
  • Post-flop: Call down lighter on safe runouts; let them bluff off stacks.
  • Avoid: Hero-folding big hands against them. Widen your calling range significantly.
  • Bonus: When a maniac does something unusual or very large — that can still be strength.
Key Insight — Attacking Limps

When a player limps, their range is typically weak — their best hands are usually raises. Attack limps with a 3–4 bb raise. If the SB limps into you in the BB, raise aggressively (4 bb): they fold often, you collect the blinds for free without paying rake.

5 — C-Bet Sizing Guide

Situation Size Rationale
Dry board (Q♥7♠2♣) — heads-up, in position 25–33% pot No draws; thin range; small bet achieves same folds as big bet
Wet board (9♥8♠5♣) — heads-up, in position 50–75% pot Charge draws; protect made hands; deny equity
Wet board — multiway (3+ players) 50–67% pot Bet for protection; do not slow-play strong hands when multiple players can outdraw you
Dry board — multiway 25–40% pot or check Fewer callers; small bet or check is sufficient
Thin value bet — out of position (middle pair, TPNK) 20–33% pot Invites calls from worse; small enough that villain rarely raises with air
Thin value bet — in position (middle pair, TPNK) ~50% pot Position allows larger sizing while still getting called by worse hands
Overbet bluff on river (villain range capped) 125–200% pot Villain range capped at one pair; they rarely call big bets; break-even at 67% fold (2x pot)
Overbet value vs call-happy villain 125–200% pot Non-believer calls wide — exploit by sizing up, not down
Trap — Slow-Playing on Wet Boards

If you flop a set on 9-8-5 and check, every turn card is potentially a disaster (7, 6, T, flush cards). Bet to build the pot and charge draws. Slow-playing is only reasonable on truly dry boards where you cannot be outdrawn.

Key Insight — Capped Range = Prime Bluff Spot

When villain check-calls the flop and check-calls the turn, their range is capped at one pair on the river (they would have raised two-pair+ earlier). This is your prime bluff spot. They usually cannot call a large river bet. Finish your bluffs — don't give up on the river after betting flop and turn.

8 — Thin Value & Bluffing Rules

Thin Value Betting

The 51% Rule

If your hand beats villain's calling range more than 50% of the time, a value bet is profitable. Don't wait for certainty. Accept that some thin value bets get called by better — that is normal and correct.

  • OOP thin value: 20–33% pot
  • IP thin value: ~50% pot
  • vs call-happy villain: size up, not down
  • Top pair no kicker on checked-down river: bet
  • Middle pair when draws miss: bet in position

Bluffing

Pot-Size River Bluff Math

A pot-size river bluff needs villain to fold 50%+. A 2x pot overbet needs only 67% fold. Passive small-stakes players fold one pair to large bets far more than 67% of the time.

  • Bluff when opponent is capped (check-called twice)
  • Big bluffs work better than small bluffs vs weak players
  • Don't bet flop + turn then quit on river — finish the bluff
  • Best bluff hands: block nuts, don't unblock auto-folds
  • Ace of flush suit on river = strong bluff (blocks their flushes)
  • If your bluffs always get through, you are not bluffing enough
Worked Example — River Overbet Bluff

Setup: You raise BTN (3 bb), BB calls. 100 bb effective.

Board: K♠ 8♥ 3♣ (you c-bet 50%, BB calls) — Turn 2♦ (both check) — River 5♥

Analysis: BB check-called flop (capped at KX or worse), checked turn (no two-pair+ likely). River 5 is a blank. BB's range is likely top pair / second pair with no strong hand. You can overbet 150–200% pot here — villain folds KJ, K9, K8 most of the time. You only need 67% fold equity to break even with a 2x pot bet.

Worked Example — Bluff Blocker Selection

Board: J♠ T♠ 7♣ 6♦ 2♥ (no flush possible)

You hold: 8♣ 8♦ (pocket eights)

Analysis: Your eights block the 9-8 straight (a hand villain might value-bet). If villain has a J or T with no straight, they are unlikely to have 9-8 in their range (you block it). This makes a river bluff attractive — your hand has almost no showdown value, you block part of their continuing range, and you don't unblock their folding range (KQ, AJ, etc.).

9 — Key Formulas

Pot Odds & Equity

Equity needed = Call / (Pot + Call) Rule of 4: Outs × 4 ≈ % (2 cards left) Rule of 2: Outs × 2 ≈ % (1 card left) MDF (Min Defense Freq): MDF = 1 − Bet / (Bet + Pot) [% of your range you must defend vs a bet]

Bluff Break-Evens

Bluff BE = Bet / (Bet + Pot) [need villain to fold this % of the time] 1/3 pot bluff → need 25% fold 1/2 pot bluff → need 33% fold Pot bluff → need 50% fold 2x pot bluff → need 67% fold

Preflop Sizing Reference

Standard open: 2.5–3 bb (in position) 3–4 bb (out of position) 3-bet OOP: ~3× open + 1 unit 3-bet IP: ~2.5–3× open 4-bet: ~2.3–2.5× the 3-bet (~23–24 bb vs 3-bet to 10 bb) vs limpers: base + 1 bb per limper

SPR (Stack-to-Pot Ratio)

SPR = Effective Stack / Pot SPR < 3: Commit with top pair+ SPR 3–8: Comfortable with 2-pair+ SPR > 8: Need strong hand to stack off (sets, straights, flushes) Pot raise = (3 × last bet) + dead money

10 — Making Big Folds (Dry Boards)

Definition — Dry Board

A board with no flush draws and no straight draws (e.g., Q♥7♠2♣). When a passive opponent triple-barrels a dry board, they almost never bluff — they expect you to have a pair and they keep betting because they have value. Overfold your bluff-catchers.

Situation Action Reasoning
Passive villain bets flop + turn + river (Q72r) Fold second pair or worse They know you'll call with a pair. If they still fire river, they have value. Overfold.
Same board, you hold top pair (Qx) no kicker Likely call You beat some value bets (JJ, TT overpairs); cannot easily fold top pair
Villain check-calls twice then leads river Fold one-pair hands Lead after two check-calls = turned/rivered strong hand; very rarely a bluff
Dry board vs a known maniac Call wider (top pair+) Maniacs blast off with nothing; adjust threshold upward significantly
Draws miss on river, villain fires big Context-dependent; tend to call vs aggressive players Big river bet when draws miss can be a missed-draw bluff; factor in villain type

Key Insight — Abnormal = Strong

In small stakes games, when a player does something unusual (odd sizing, unexpected timing, out-of-the-ordinary aggression), it is almost always a sign of strength. Unusual behaviors are rarely calculated deceptions at these stakes. Trust your alarm bells.

12 — Quick Decision Checklist

Pre-Flop Checklist

  1. What position am I in?
  2. What does villain's action tell me about their range?
  3. Am I in or out of position postflop?
  4. Is villain too tight? Attack more, 3-bet wider.
  5. Is villain too loose? Tighten up, play fewer bluffs.
  6. Use correct size: larger OOP, smaller IP.

Post-Flop Checklist

  1. What is villain's likely range given all prior action?
  2. Is my hand ahead or behind most of that range?
  3. Wet board = protect / bet; dry board = thinner sizes OK.
  4. Passive villain = bet thin value; aggressive villain = call down.
  5. Does their bet size reveal anything?
  6. Is villain capped? If yes, bluff opportunity.
Value Bet?

Does my hand beat >50% of villain's calling range? If yes, bet. Use smaller sizing OOP, larger IP or vs call-happy players.

Bluff?

Is villain capped? Does board texture favor my range? Do I block the nuts? Do I have fold equity? Big bets work better than small bets.

Fold?

Is villain passive and betting multiple streets on a dry board? Does my hand lose to most of their value range? Overfold vs passive opponents on dry boards.
